## Summary

Washington State passed SB 6346 on March 12, 2026 — a **9.9% income tax on all personal income over $1M/year**, effective January 1, 2028. Governor Ferguson is expected to sign. This is NOT the existing capital gains tax — it is a **new broad-based income tax that applies directly to NFL player W-2 salaries**. Every previous analysis claiming Seattle has "zero state income tax" needs revision for contracts extending past 2027.

## Legal Status — ⚠️ Highly Uncertain

The law is **passed but faces serious constitutional challenges**:

1. **Historical precedent against it:** *Culliton v. Chase* (1933) — WA Supreme Court ruled income is "property" under the state constitution, requiring uniform taxation. A graduated tax (like 9.9% only on income >$1M) violates this.
2. **Recent precedent favoring it:** WA Supreme Court upheld the capital gains tax as an "excise tax" in March 2023. Legislature used the same legal framing for SB 6346.
3. **Key distinction:** Capital gains tax targeted specific investment transactions. SB 6346 taxes aggregate annual income — a much harder constitutional argument.
4. **Seattle city income tax struck down** previously on the same uniform-taxation grounds.
5. **Voter hostility:** WA voters have rejected income taxes 10+ times in the past century.
6. **Timeline:** Lawsuits expected immediately. Could take 1–3 years to reach WA Supreme Court. Ballot initiative also possible.

**PlayerRep assessment:** There is a **meaningful chance (40–60%)** this law gets struck down or repealed before it takes effect in 2028. But agents cannot gamble their client's money on that outcome.

## Recommendations

### For 2026 Free Agency (IMMEDIATE — contracts signed now)

1. **Short-term deals (1–2 years) are UNAFFECTED.** Seattle retains full zero-tax advantage through 2027. Recruit aggressively on tax pitch for prove-it deals and short veteran contracts.
2. **Multi-year deals (3–5 years) need front-loading.** Push cash and guarantees into 2026–2027. Structure signing bonuses to be paid upfront. Backload void years into the 2028+ window.
3. **Agents will know about this.** SB 6346 passed the same day as free agency opening. Every agent with a Seattle-bound client has already factored this in (or will within days). Don't assume the old tax pitch works unchallenged.

### For Seahawks Roster Strategy

4. **2026–2027 is a window.** If the law survives legal challenge, Seattle has exactly 2 more years of full tax advantage. Use them aggressively.
5. **Rookie contracts remain unaffected.** Draft picks earning under $1M/yr (most non-1st-rounders) pay zero WA state tax regardless. Draft-and-develop is more valuable than ever for SEA.
6. **Extensions for current players** should be locked in before 2028 if possible. Players already on the roster may push for trades to TX/FL/TN/NV teams rather than extending if the tax holds.

### For the Other "No-Tax" States

7. **TX (DAL, HOU), FL (JAX, MIA, TB), TN (TEN), NV (LV) are UNAFFECTED.** No changes planned. They now have an unambiguous tax advantage over Seattle for the first time.
8. **This strengthens the FA pitch for every Texas/Florida team.** Expect agents to explicitly compare WA's new tax to TX/FL's zero-tax status in negotiations.

---

## Bottom Line

**Seattle isn't dead as a FA destination — but it just went from "elite tax haven" to "middle of the pack."** The combination of defending champion + strong coaching + remaining cap space still makes SEA attractive, but the tax pitch needs to be honest about the 2028 cliff. For the 2026 offseason specifically, short-term deals remain a tax home run. For anything extending past 2027, the math has fundamentally changed.

The biggest risk is uncertainty. If the law is struck down, Seattle looks like geniuses for locking up talent at tax-advantaged rates. If it's upheld, players on 4–5 year deals will wish they'd gone to Dallas or Miami.

**Recommended approach: Plan for the worst, hope for the best. Structure deals to minimize 2028+ exposure.**

## CAR — Carolina Panthers

### Coaching Staff
- ✅ **Head Coach:** Dave Canales (3rd year)
  - **Background:** Former Buccaneers OC and longtime Seahawks offensive assistant with a QB-development reputation.
  - **Career Path:** Seahawks offensive assistant in multiple roles under Pete Carroll → Buccaneers OC (2023) → Panthers HC (2024-present)
  - **Program Focus:** Canales has built this offense around quarterback rehabilitation and confidence, not schematic volume for its own sake.
  - **Coaching Tree:** Pete Carroll's developmental ecosystem shaped Canales — balanced football, manageable throws, and quarterback-friendly structure.
- ✅ **Offensive Coordinator:** Brad Idzik
  - **Background:** Canales protégé and continuity choice.
  - **Role:** Full play-caller in 2026 as Canales steps back, which preserves system language while letting the head coach oversee Bryce Young's development from a broader view.
- ✅ **Stability Note:** Carolina is not overhauling its offense; it is narrowing it more tightly around Bryce Young's strengths.

### Offensive Scheme Identity
- ✅ **System Type:** QB-centric West Coast timing offense built specifically for Bryce Young.
- ✅ **Run Game Philosophy:**
  - **Primary:** Inside and outside zone form the foundation.
  - The run game exists to create a credible play-action world and keep the offense out of obvious dropback situations.
  - Chuba Hubbard fits the approach as a versatile, efficient zone runner rather than a pure volume hammer.
- ✅ **Pass Game Identity:**
  - Rhythm passing, quick releases, and defined progressions are the point of the system.
  - This is not an arm-strength vanity offense; it is designed around Young's anticipation, accuracy, and ability to hit throws on time.
  - Short-to-intermediate concepts should dominate, with the offense using timing and leverage instead of constant vertical hunting.
- 📊 **Personnel Tendencies:**
  - **Primary:** 11 personnel remains the base.
  - **Flexibility Goal:** Carolina is still building toward more formation and personnel versatility, but it is not there yet.
  - Moderate motion is used more for coverage identification and access throws than for pure window dressing.
- ❓ **Constraint Layer:** The offensive line still caps the system's ceiling, and thin WR depth means the offense has less margin for injury or game-script stress than it wants.

### Key Offensive Players
| Pos | Player | Notes |
|-----|--------|-------|
| QB | ✅ Bryce Young | Year 3 is the evaluation year the entire system points toward. The franchise is clearly building around his rhythm-and-anticipation strengths. |
| WR | 📊 Tetairoa McMillen | Breakout rookie with 1,014 yards in 2025. Big frame gives Young a needed catch-radius target. |
| RB | 📊 Chuba Hubbard | Versatile zone runner whose efficiency matters because Carolina's offense is not built to live behind the chains. |
| OL | ❓ Offensive line | The unit remains the swing factor between a competent timing offense and one that gets compressed by pressure. |

### Scheme Fit Implications
- **QBs who thrive:** Fast processors with anticipation and touch matter more here than pure arm talent. Young is the exact archetype Canales wants to prove right.
- **WRs who thrive:** Separators, reliable zone finders, and catch-point stabilizers who can help a timing offense stay on schedule.
- **RBs who thrive:** Zone runners with enough versatility to contribute in protection and on checkdowns; Hubbard fits the profile.
- **OL who thrive:** This system needs competence more than stars, but it absolutely cannot function if protection collapses before Young reaches the top of his drop.
- **Motion/structure takeaway:** Carolina uses motion as a diagnostic tool, not as an identity statement; the offense is built to create layups for the quarterback.
- ❓ **2026 Pivot Point:** If Bryce Young keeps ascending, the system will look smart and intentional. If protection and WR depth fail him, the offense may still feel small even when it is well designed.

## NO — New Orleans Saints

### Coaching Staff
- ✅ **Head Coach:** Kellen Moore (1st year, 2026)
  - **Background:** Former Eagles OC and Super Bowl LIX champion play-caller, with prior OC stops in Dallas and Los Angeles.
  - **Career Path:** Cowboys backup QB (2012-2017) → Cowboys QBs coach → Cowboys OC (2019-2022) → Chargers OC (2023) → Eagles OC (2024) → Saints HC
  - **Play-Caller:** Yes — Moore's offensive identity is a major reason he was hired.
  - **Coaching Tree:** Moore's roots run through the Cowboys ecosystem under Jason Garrett and Scott Linehan, but his recent work also reflects the collaborative, matchup-based influence of Nick Sirianni's Eagles operation.
- ✅ **Offensive Coordinator:** Doug Nussmeier
  - **Background:** QB-development specialist and former Eagles QB coach.
  - **Role:** Trusted Moore lieutenant for quarterback prep, weekly game-plan detail, and continuity in terminology.
- ✅ **Senior Offensive Assistant:** Scott Linehan
  - **Value:** Veteran institutional knowledge and a direct link to Moore's Cowboys background.

### Offensive Scheme Identity
- ✅ **System Type:** Modern West Coast offense with aggressive, matchup-based play-calling.
- ✅ **Run Game Philosophy:**
  - Moore's recent offenses have leaned hard into the run when the roster supports it, and his Eagles unit finishing second in rushing is the clearest proof point.
  - Expect a physical tone up front, with the run game treated as both an efficiency engine and a play-action partner.
  - RPO elements should show up as constraint calls that punish overloaded boxes and overaggressive second-level defenders.
- ✅ **Pass Game Identity:**
  - Quick decisions, play-action, and short-to-intermediate timing throws form the core.
  - Moore is aggressive not because he calls bombs every series, but because he hunts matchups through formation, motion, and sequencing.
  - This should be a far more curated passing attack than a static dropback system.
- 📊 **Personnel Tendencies:**
  - **Primary:** 11 personnel is likely the starting point.
  - **Flexible:** 12 personnel should be a real part of the menu because Moore adjusts to talent rather than forcing one body type onto the field.
  - Alvin Kamara's RB/slot hybrid value gives New Orleans a built-in answer against linebackers and base personnel.
- ✅ **Motion Profile:** Heavy motion and shifting should be core features, both for matchup creation and quarterback clarity.
- ❓ **Ceiling Tension:** Moore can manufacture answers, but Derek Carr's physical and improvisational limitations may still cap how explosive the offense becomes.

### Key Offensive Players
| Pos | Player | Notes |
|-----|--------|-------|
| QB | 📊 Derek Carr | Veteran floor raiser, but probably not the kind of quarterback who can fully unlock Moore's most aggressive vision. |
| RB | ✅ Alvin Kamara | Still the offense's cleanest mismatch weapon because he can function as runner, receiver, and slot-style answer piece. |
| WR | ✅ Chris Olave | Route technician and vertical threat who should benefit from Moore's ability to isolate defenders and create cleaner access throws. |
| OL | ❓ Offensive line | Moore wants a physical front; without stronger line play, the run-game vision and play-action sequencing lose force. |

### Scheme Fit Implications
- **QBs who thrive:** Quick-minded distributors who can process leverage, get the ball out on time, and capitalize on schemed matchups.
- **RBs who thrive:** Dual-threat backs are premium here because Moore can align them everywhere and use them to punish defensive substitution rules.
- **WRs who thrive:** Route detail matters. Moore wants receivers who can win when the scheme creates the matchup, not receivers who need every throw to be contested.
- **TEs/auxiliary pieces:** Flexible second-level targets gain real value because Moore likes to layer route combinations and stress one defender with multiple threats.
- **OL who thrive:** The front must be sturdy enough to support a real run game; if the line is leaky, the offense risks becoming clever but incomplete.
- ❓ **2026 Pivot Point:** The Saints may call one of the smartest offenses in the division, but their ultimate ceiling still depends on whether Carr and the line are good enough to keep Moore out of compromise mode.

## TB — Tampa Bay Buccaneers

### Coaching Staff
- ✅ **Head Coach:** Todd Bowles (6th year)
  - **Background:** Defensive head coach under real pressure after an 8-9 finish in 2025.
  - **Program Dynamic:** Bowles needs offensive lift more than philosophical control, which makes this coordinator hire especially important.
- ✅ **Offensive Coordinator:** Zac Robinson (1st year, 2026)
  - **Background:** Pure Sean McVay-tree coach with prior experience in Tampa Bay and a recent OC stint in Atlanta.
  - **Career Path:** Buccaneers offensive assistant → Rams coaching staff under Sean McVay → Falcons OC (2024-2025) → Buccaneers OC
  - **Coaching Tree:** Sean McVay → Zac Robinson. This is one of the cleanest direct McVay lineage hires in the league.
- ✅ **Previous Coordinator:** Josh Grizzard was fired in a broader staff overhaul, making Robinson the fourth OC in four years under Bowles.

### Offensive Scheme Identity
- ✅ **System Type:** McVay-style West Coast offense built on motion, play-action, and formation-driven answers.
- ✅ **Run Game Philosophy:**
  - **Primary:** Outside zone should lead the menu, with gap and changeup concepts mixed in as complements.
  - Robinson's Rams/Falcons background points to a run game that sets up the presentation of the offense, not just its rushing totals.
  - Bucky Irving's emergence gives Tampa Bay a younger, more dynamic backfield piece for this style.
- ✅ **Pass Game Identity:**
  - Robinson should bring a more quarterback-friendly structure than recent Bowles-era offenses provided.
  - Expect condensed formations, pre-snap motion, and play-action sequencing designed to create easier throws and better defined coverages for Baker Mayfield.
  - The goal is modernization: fewer static snaps, more leverage, more manufactured clarity.
- 📊 **Personnel Tendencies:**
  - **Primary:** 11 personnel should remain the foundation, just as it was in Robinson's Atlanta offense.
  - WR deployment matters because Tampa Bay's best offensive assets still live on the perimeter.
  - The system should look more fluid before the snap even if the core personnel remains familiar.
- ❓ **Volatility Layer:** Robinson can improve the structure, but he inherits a quarterback whose production dipped sharply and a staff environment with very little coordinator continuity.

### Key Offensive Players
| Pos | Player | Notes |
|-----|--------|-------|
| QB | ❓ Baker Mayfield | Aggressive creator who can fit a McVay-style system, but the 41-TD to 26-TD drop raises real questions about sustainability. |
| WR1 | ✅ Mike Evans | Future Hall of Famer whose size and ball skills remain perfect for high-value play-action and isolation concepts. |
| WR2 | ✅ Chris Godwin | Elite route runner whose feel for spacing should translate cleanly into Robinson's motion-and-timing game. |
| RB | 📊 Bucky Irving | Emerging back with the acceleration and space-game value to benefit from a cleaner zone/play-action structure. |

### Scheme Fit Implications
- **QBs who thrive:** Decisive passers who can trust the structure, hit in-breakers off play-action, and still create when the picture breaks late.
- **WRs who thrive:** Tampa already has the ideal pairing — Evans for size and finish, Godwin for route craft and chain-moving precision.
- **RBs who thrive:** Zone runners with burst and receiving ability fit best; Irving looks like the most natural long-term piece here.
- **OL who thrive:** This scheme needs enough athleticism for zone work and enough communication to handle motion-heavy presentation without busts.
- **Coordinator stability concern:** The concepts should improve immediately, but long-term offensive growth is hard when the play-caller chair keeps changing.
- ❓ **2026 Pivot Point:** Tampa Bay may be more modern on paper, yet the season still turns on whether Mayfield rebounds and whether Bowles can finally keep one offensive identity in place.

## Key League-Wide Scheme Observations

1. **The Shanahan tree is NFL's dominant coaching ideology.** At least 15 teams now run some variant of WZR/Shanahan concepts. Zone blocking, play-action, and pre-snap motion are the NFL's standard operating procedure.
2. **RPO usage is dividing the league.** Some teams (KC, NYG, PHI, IND) lean heavily into RPOs; others (SF, LAR, BAL) prefer traditional play-action.
3. **12 personnel is resurging.** CHI (Johnson), LAR (McVay), DET (Campbell), PHI (Sirianni), and GB (LaFleur) are all featuring two-TE sets more than historical norms.
4. **OC turnover is epidemic.** PHI has had 5 OCs in 5 years. TB, DET, NYJ, and ATL all changed OCs. Stability correlates with offensive success.
5. **QB-friendly systems are the norm.** Every new coaching hire emphasizes "building around the QB." The days of "run my system regardless" are mostly dead.
6. **The McVay/Shanahan tree is creating a schematic monoculture.** This means roster-building across teams is converging — athletic zone-blocking OL, versatile TEs, YAC receivers are universally valued.

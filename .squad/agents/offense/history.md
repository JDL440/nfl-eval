### NFC West — The Shanahan Division
_Last updated: 2025-07-18 via web search (ESPN, SI, NFL.com, team sites, The Athletic)_

**Division-Wide Observation:** The entire NFC West is now dominated by the Shanahan/Kubiak coaching tree. All four teams run some variant of the wide zone/Shanahan system, creating an unprecedented schematic uniformity within a single division. This has major implications for player-scheme fit — outside zone OL technique, play-action passing, and motion-heavy concepts are the baseline requirement for any offensive player in this division.

### Offensive Scheme Identity
_Last updated: 2025-07-18 via SI, FFTradeAnalyzer, SumerSports, WisconsinSportsTalk_

- ✅ **System Type:** Shanahan Wide Zone Run (WZR) — full adoption of the Shanahan offensive architecture
- ✅ **Run Game Philosophy:**
  - **Primary:** Outside zone (wide zone) — stretches defense horizontally, creates cutback lanes
  - **Secondary:** Inside zone complements when defenses over-pursue
  - Athletic OL who can reach-block and move laterally are essential
  - Running backs must be decisive, one-cut runners who read zone blocking flow
- ✅ **Pass Game Identity:**
  - **Play-action heavy** — married to the run game; boot action, rollouts, designed QB movement
  - **Pre-snap motion:** Heavy usage to identify coverages and create leverage advantages
  - **Route concepts:** Crossers, deep posts off play-action (e.g., "Yankee" concepts), flood routes on bootlegs
  - TE is a focal point in the passing game (the "Kittle role")
  - WRs moved around formations for matchup advantages — interchangeability is prized
- 📊 **Personnel Tendencies:**
  - **Primary:** 11 personnel (1RB, 1TE, 3WR) — base spread look
  - **Secondary:** 12 personnel (1RB, 2TE, 2WR) — run-heavy, play-action sets
  - Condensed formations (tight splits, bunches) used frequently
- ✅ **OL Scheme:** Zone blocking — requires athletic, mobile linemen with reach ability. Interior OL must execute combo blocks to the second level. Not a power/gap blocking team.

### Offensive Scheme Identity
_Last updated: 2025-07-18 via therams.com, ESPN, SI, LAFBNetwork, RamblinFan, Athlon Sports_

- ✅ **System Type:** Evolved Shanahan WZR + Duo/Gap Hybrid — McVay has moved well beyond pure outside zone
- ✅ **Run Game Philosophy:**
  - **Primary (evolved):** Outside zone (wide zone) PLUS duo (double-team vertical push) and gap concepts
  - 2025 featured the NFL's most successful designed run game since 2016 (49.7% success rate on designed RB runs)
  - McVay adapted when defenses adjusted to pure wide zone — added duo, counter, toss-crack, insert plays
  - "Formational disguise" — similar pre-snap looks for run and pass, manipulating box counts
- ✅ **Pass Game Identity:**
  - Play-action built off wide zone and duo looks
  - Heavy motion and formation variation pre-snap
  - Stafford's arm talent enables deep shots off play-action
  - Aggressive, big-play passing attack — Scheelhaase emphasizes explosive plays
  - ✅ Rams led NFL in total offense in 2025; Stafford won MVP
- ✅ **Personnel Tendencies (MAJOR SHIFT in 2025):**
  - **Historically:** 11 personnel dominant (McVay's early years)
  - **2025 evolution:** Dramatic increase to 12 personnel (42% of snaps in some games, up from 15.6% prior year)
  - **13 personnel** became a real weapon (used 40%+ when Nacua was injured vs JAX)
  - **Toggle:** Rams now shift between 11/12/13 without substituting, stressing defensive rules
  - Deep TE investment (Higbee, Allen, Ferguson, Parkinson) enables heavy personnel flexibility
- ✅ **OL Scheme:** Hybrid — zone blocking base but with duo/gap schemes mixed in. Requires big, agile interior OL who can execute both zone reach blocks and double-team vertical push blocks.

### Offensive Scheme Identity
_Last updated: 2025-07-18 via SumerSports, Berkeley Sports Analytics, NBC Sports Bay Area, AfterTheSnap_

- ✅ **System Type:** Shanahan Wide Zone Run (WZR) — THE original. This is the mothership.
- ✅ **Run Game Philosophy:**
  - **Primary:** Outside zone (wide zone) — the cornerstone. Horizontal stretch, combo blocks, cutback lanes.
  - **Heavier personnel than most Shanahan branches:** 21 personnel (2RB, 1TE), 12/22 groupings more common than in LAR's early years
  - Condensed formations (tight splits) to create favorable blocking angles and manipulate defensive spacing
  - Fullback (Kyle Juszczyk) remains integral — one of the last true FB-featured offenses
  - "Run it in" philosophy — goal-line and short-yardage emphasis on physical run game
- ✅ **Pass Game Identity:**
  - **Play-action rate:** Among NFL's highest. Boot action, keepers, designed rollouts off zone run looks
  - **Crossing routes:** "Yankee" concept (deep posts + crossers) is a signature call. LBs flow to run action → huge windows in middle of field
  - **Misdirection layers:** Drag defenders out of position, create 1-on-1 matchups for Kittle/WRs
  - **Quick game + empty sets** added in 2025 to compensate for personnel turnover
  - **Pre-snap motion:** Master class. Sometimes 2-3 players shift/motion. Identifies coverages, creates leverage, confuses assignment football.
  - **Game scripting:** Shanahan scripts first 24 plays (12 run, 12 pass) as diagnostic tool + attack plan
- ✅ **Personnel Tendencies:**
  - **Heavy sets:** 21 (2RB incl. FB), 12, and 22 personnel used more than any other Shanahan branch
  - **11 personnel** used as well, but SF runs heavier base than LAR or ARI
  - Condensed formations with motion produce highest yards-per-play
  - Kyle Juszczyk (FB) is a scheme weapon, not a relic — used as lead blocker, motion decoy, receiver
- ✅ **OL Scheme:** Pure outside zone blocking. Chris Foerster is one of the NFL's best zone blocking teachers. Requires athletic OL with elite lateral movement and ability to reach-block at the second level.

### Scheme Fit Implications
- **RBs who thrive:** Zone runners with vision AND receiving ability. Must be able to catch passes out of backfield and contribute as blockers. CMC is the prototype.
- **WRs who thrive:** YAC-first receivers who can win in space after the catch. Route running in the intermediate game (crossers, drags, comebacks). Must block in the run game.

## Key Scheme Fit Takeaways for Roster Evaluation

1. **Any offensive player entering the NFC West MUST be zone-scheme compatible.** There is no gap/power-first team in this division.
2. **Play-action passing ability is non-negotiable for QBs.** All four teams build their passing game off run fakes.
3. **WR blocking matters in every NFC West offense.** Physical receivers who block on the perimeter have outsized value.
4. **Tight ends are premium.** SF uses TE as a centerpiece; LAR uses multi-TE packages; ARI plans the "Kittle role" for McBride; SEA is growing their TE usage. An elite TE is the most valuable non-QB offensive player in this division.
5. **Athletic OL > powerful OL.** Zone blocking demands lateral movement, reach technique, and ability to get to the second level. Stiff, anchor-first linemen are scheme mismatches.
6. **LAR is the most evolved/versatile** — they've added duo/gap concepts and heavy personnel that the other three haven't fully adopted yet.
7. **ARI is the wild card** — Year 1 in a new system with a QB (Murray) whose improvisational style may clash with Shanahan structure.

### Scheme Fit Implications

## Key League-Wide Scheme Observations

1. **The Shanahan tree is NFL's dominant coaching ideology.** At least 15 teams now run some variant of WZR/Shanahan concepts. Zone blocking, play-action, and pre-snap motion are the NFL's standard operating procedure.
2. **RPO usage is dividing the league.** Some teams (KC, NYG, PHI, IND) lean heavily into RPOs; others (SF, LAR, BAL) prefer traditional play-action.
3. **12 personnel is resurging.** CHI (Johnson), LAR (McVay), DET (Campbell), PHI (Sirianni), and GB (LaFleur) are all featuring two-TE sets more than historical norms.
4. **OC turnover is epidemic.** PHI has had 5 OCs in 5 years. TB, DET, NYJ, and ATL all changed OCs. Stability correlates with offensive success.
5. **QB-friendly systems are the norm.** Every new coaching hire emphasizes "building around the QB." The days of "run my system regardless" are mostly dead.
6. **The McVay/Shanahan tree is creating a schematic monoculture.** This means roster-building across teams is converging — athletic zone-blocking OL, versatile TEs, YAC receivers are universally valued.

## CBA Expertise

### Accrued Seasons & Vesting
- A player earns an **Accrued Season** by being on a club's Active/Inactive, Reserve/Injured, or Reserve/PUP list for at least 6 regular-season games
- **4 Accrued Seasons** → Unrestricted Free Agent (UFA) at contract expiration
- **3 Accrued Seasons** → Restricted Free Agent (RFA) — original team can match or receive draft pick compensation
- **Fewer than 3** → Exclusive Rights Free Agent (ERFA) — must be tendered at league minimum or released

### Free Agency Types
| Type | Accrued Seasons | Player Rights | Team Rights |
|------|----------------|---------------|-------------|
| **UFA** | 4+ | Full market access, choose any team | None — can only compete on offer |
| **RFA** | 3 | Can negotiate with other teams | Right of first refusal + draft pick compensation (1st, 2nd, or Original Round tender) |
| **ERFA** | <3 | No negotiating leverage | Must tender minimum salary or release |

### Minimum Salary Tiers (by Years of Service)
- Minimum salaries escalate with credited seasons (years on an NFL roster)
- Key for veteran minimum contracts and salary floor calculations
- Players with 7+ years of service on minimum deals: team only charged a lower "minimum salary benefit" cap number

### Franchise & Transition Tags (Player POV)
- **Exclusive Franchise Tag:** Player cannot negotiate with other teams. Paid top-5 average at position or 120% of prior salary (whichever is higher). Player leverage: hold out, demand trade, refuse to sign.
- **Non-Exclusive Franchise Tag:** Player CAN negotiate with other teams. Original team has right to match or receive two 1st-round picks. Rarely results in player leaving.
- **Transition Tag:** Player can negotiate freely. Original team has right of first refusal only (no draft pick compensation). Rarely used.
- **Player strategy on tags:** Accept and play (safe), hold out for long-term deal (risky but forces team's hand), demand trade (nuclear option — see Davante Adams, Stefon Diggs precedents).
- **Consecutive tags:** 2nd consecutive tag = 120% of prior tag. 3rd = 144%. Prohibitively expensive — players almost never get tagged 3 times.

### Compensatory Pick Formula
- When a team loses more/better compensatory free agents (CFAs) than it signs, it receives comp picks (rounds 3–7)
- Formula considers: **salary of new contract**, **playing time in new contract year**, **postseason honors**
- Comp picks are awarded the following draft (lose player in 2026 FA → comp pick in 2027 Draft)
- Players signed to "prove-it" deals below a certain threshold don't cancel comp picks (the CFA cancellation threshold)
- **Player impact:** A player's comp pick value affects whether their former team actively roots for them to sign elsewhere (and at what price)

### Rookie Wage Scale & 5th-Year Option
- **Rounds 1–7:** Slotted salaries based on draft position. 4-year contracts for all drafted players.
- **Round 1 only:** Team holds a **5th-year option** — must be exercised after Year 3. Salary = franchise tag-level for the position (top 10 picks) or transition tag-level (picks 11–32).
- **5th-year option is fully guaranteed for injury** when exercised, becomes **fully guaranteed** on the first day of the player's 4th league year.
- **Player strategy:** Bet on yourself — elite play in years 1–3 forces the team to either pay top dollar on a long-term deal or risk losing you. 5th-year option is often a discount for top performers.

### Guaranteed Money Structures
| Type | Meaning | Player Value |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Fully guaranteed at signing** | Money is owed no matter what — cut, injury, retirement | 💰💰💰 Highest priority in any negotiation |
| **Rolling guarantees** | Money becomes guaranteed on a future date (e.g., March of Year 3) | 💰💰 Good — but team can cut before trigger date |
| **Injury guarantees** | Only guaranteed if player is injured at time of cut | 💰 Minimal protection |
| **Roster bonuses** | Paid if player is on roster on a specific date | 💰 De facto guarantee if date is early in league year |

- **Fully guaranteed at signing is the #1 metric** for player value — not total deal value, not AAV
- Example: A "5-year, $100M deal" with only $40M guaranteed is really a 2-year, $40M deal with team options

### Void Years
- Teams add void (automatic voiding) years to spread signing bonus cap hits over more years
- **Player impact:** Void years don't affect player cash flow — the money is paid upfront as signing bonus
- **Risk:** Player is technically under contract during void years, which can affect comp pick eligibility
- **Perception:** Large void year dead money hits make teams look "stuck" but the player already has the cash

### Market Size & Endorsement Opportunities
- **Tier 1 (Max endorsements):** DAL, NYG, NYJ, LAR, LAC, CHI, SF — national brands, media saturation
- **Tier 2 (Strong regional):** PHI, MIA, NE, SEA, DEN, MIN, ATL, HOU, TB — significant local deals + national potential for stars
- **Tier 3 (Modest):** BUF, CIN, BAL, CLE, PIT, DET, GB, NO, ARI, LV, TEN, IND, KC — loyal fanbases, fewer national opps unless elite
- **Tier 4 (Minimal):** JAX, CAR — smallest markets, hardest to build national brand

### Other Destination Factors
- **Winning culture / championship contention:** Veterans on second/third contracts increasingly prioritize rings (see: ring-chasing discounts)
- **Scheme fit & role clarity:** Starter vs. rotational, featured back vs. committee, primary target vs. WR3. Role clarity affects both production and next contract.
- **Coaching staff reputation:** Player development track record matters — see: Shanahan/McVay systems producing WR value, Belichick tree for defensive players
- **Weather & lifestyle:** Warm-weather and destination cities (MIA, TB, LAR, LAC, LV, ARI) attract free agents at modest discounts
- **Proximity to family/hometown:** "Return home" narratives are real. Players often take less to play near family, especially later in careers.

## Contract Negotiation Dynamics

### Extension vs. Free Agency Decision
- **Extend early if:** Injury risk is high at the position, market could soften, player values stability, team is competitive
- **Play it out if:** Player is ascending, market is rising at the position, player is young and healthy, current deal significantly underpays
- **Key window:** Most extensions happen entering the final 1–2 years of a deal. Extending too early leaves money on the table.

### Leverage Points
- **Franchise tag implications:** If the team will tag you, negotiate from the tag number as a floor
- **Holdout strategy:** Effective when player has leverage (elite production, hard to replace). Risk: fines ($50K/day during training camp, game checks during regular season)
- **Trade demands:** Nuclear option. Most effective when player has a no-trade clause or significant leverage. Works best when multiple teams are interested.
- **Comparable contracts ("comps"):** Agents use the most recent, highest-paid comparable players to set the market. The most recently signed deal at a position becomes the new floor for the next negotiation.

### Contract Valuation Priority (Player POV)
1. **Fully guaranteed money at signing** — the only number that truly matters
2. **Practical guarantees** (rolling guarantees likely to vest based on timing)
3. **Year 1-2 cash flow** — actual money in pocket in the near term
4. **AAV (Average Annual Value)** — sets the market for the next player, agent reputation metric
5. **Total deal value** — largely meaningless beyond marketing; team can cut at any point past guarantees
6. **Incentives** — good if likely to be earned (LTBE), risky if unlikely (NLTBE)

## Career Trajectory & Timing

### Optimal Extension Windows by Position
| Position | Peak Value Window | Extension Sweet Spot | Notes |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|-------|
| QB | Age 26–30 | After Year 3 (rookie deal) | Maximize 2nd contract — it's the biggest in sports |
| EDGE | Age 24–28 | After Year 3–4 | Production drops sharply after 30 |
| WR | Age 24–28 | After Year 3 | Speed-dependent — extend before any decline shows |
| CB | Age 24–28 | After Year 3 | Athleticism-dependent, decline hits hard |

| LB | Age 24–29 | After Year 3 | Depends on type (off-ball vs. edge-setter) |

| DL | Age 24–30 | After Year 3–4 | Interior pass rushers peak earlier |
| TE | Age 25–30 | After Year 3–4 | Blocking TEs last longer than receiving TEs |

### Second Contract vs. Third Contract
- **Second contract** (after rookie deal): The biggest value jump. Player goes from rookie scale to market rate. This is where generational wealth is made.
- **Third contract** (age 28–31 typically): Market correction. Only elite players maintain or exceed their second-contract AAV. Most take a discount or shorter deal.
- **Fourth contract** (age 31+): Veteran deals. Prove-it contracts, ring-chasing discounts, or retirement.

### When to Take the Money vs. Chase a Ring
- **Take the money:** You're under 28, this is your biggest earning window, no guarantee you'll get another shot at a max deal
- **Chase the ring:** You're 30+, you've already earned $80M+, legacy matters more than marginal income, contender is offering a meaningful role

## Player Movement Patterns

### Why Players Choose Certain Teams
- **Money talks:** The richest offer wins ~70% of the time in free agency
- **Tax advantage:** No-tax states have a built-in 5–8% effective raise vs. California/New York
- **Winning:** Contenders can offer less and still attract talent (see: TB post-Brady ring-chasing wave, KC in 2024-2026)
- **Scheme familiarity:** Players follow coaches/coordinators they've succeeded under
- **Recruiting pipelines:** Some teams (MIA, LV, DAL) attract players through lifestyle; others (GB, PIT, BAL) through culture

### Return-Home Narratives
- Players from TX → DAL/HOU, FL → MIA/JAX/TB, CA → LAR/LAC/SF at meaningful rates
- Often takes a slight discount to come home, especially on 3rd+ contracts
- Agent-driven narrative: "He always wanted to come home" — good PR for both sides

### Ring-Chasing Dynamics
- Veterans (32+) with career earnings >$50M increasingly take minimum or near-minimum deals to join contenders
- Most common positions for ring-chasing: QB (backup role), EDGE (rotational), CB (slot/depth), WR (WR3/4)
- Ring-chasing windows: post-June 1 cuts, early September roster cuts, mid-season additions

### "Prove-It" Deal Strategy
- **When it works:** Young player coming off injury, player leaving a bad situation, player who underperformed but has tools
- **Structure:** 1-year deal, fully guaranteed, at or below market. Bet on yourself to earn the big contract next year.
- **Risk:** One bad year or injury and you've lost your window. Risk is highest for RBs and players over 28.
- **Best prove-it outcomes:** Play in a featured role, stay healthy, put up career numbers, hit FA at 26–27

## Key Design Principles

1. **PlayerRep is the COUNTERPOINT to team agents.** When BUF says "we can get him for $12M/yr," PlayerRep says "his agent will demand $16M based on comps."
2. **PlayerRep + Cap together give the full negotiation picture.** Cap provides the team's budget reality; PlayerRep provides the player's market expectation. The gap between them is the negotiation zone.
3. **PlayerRep does NOT evaluate talent or scheme fit.** Defer to team agents, Offense, and Defense for on-field evaluation. PlayerRep evaluates the deal, not the player's ability.
4. **PlayerRep DOES evaluate whether a deal structure is fair to the player.** Guaranteed money, structure, incentives, void years — all from the player's perspective.
5. **Players are people, not assets.** Acknowledge that players have families, preferences, hometowns, and lives beyond football. Destination analysis must include human factors.

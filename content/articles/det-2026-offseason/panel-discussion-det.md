# Panel Discussion — DET Agent Contribution

**Article:** The Lions Have $200 Million in Elite Talent. They're One Pick Away From Wasting All of It.
**Agent:** DET (Detroit Lions Expert)
**Question:** How does Detroit's 4-1 → 5-7 collapse map to specific roster gaps vs. coaching instability? Which factor was primary?

---

## The Collapse Was Structural, Not Random

Detroit's 9-8 finish in 2025 wasn't a "bad year" — it was the inevitable consequence of three simultaneous failures: **coordinator brain drain, depth evaporation, and critical injuries**. The 4-1 start masked the cracks. The 5-7 finish exposed them.

### The Coordinator Problem: Third OC in Three Years

Ben Johnson left for Chicago after 2024. The Lions promoted John Morton, who lasted exactly one season before Dan Campbell fired him. Now Drew Petzing is installing his third offensive system in three years, with Mike Kafka adding pass game concepts.

Here's why this matters more than people realize: Johnson didn't just call plays — he built the entire offensive identity. The run-heavy play-action system, the 3WR/1TE base, the RPO game that made Goff look like an MVP candidate (4,564 yards, 34 TDs in 2025). Morton couldn't replicate it. Petzing is trying to maintain it while adding his own stamp.

The offense still scored 481 points (4th in NFL) in 2025. But the 5-7 finish included brutal losses where the offense couldn't close games: @GB (lost by 3), vs MIN (lost by 6), @LAR (lost by 7). Those aren't blowouts — they're execution failures. And execution failures happen when the system isn't second nature.

**The kicker:** Ben Johnson is now the head coach of the Chicago Bears, who won the NFC North at 11-6. He knows every Lions tendency, every protection scheme, every Goff hot read. Detroit faces him twice a year. That's not just coordinator turnover — it's strategic leakage to your primary division rival.

### The Depth Problem: Elite Starters, Zero Insurance

Detroit's roster construction is ruthlessly top-heavy. Goff, St. Brown, Sewell, Hutchinson, McNeill — that's $137M+ in five players. All locked up with massive dead-cap penalties. All elite when healthy.

The problem: There's no Plan B.

- **EDGE depth:** Al-Quadin Muhammad (11 sacks) walked to Tampa Bay as a UFA. Josh Paschal was waived. Marcus Davenport left in free agency. The depth chart behind Hutchinson is literally Ahmed Hassanein (UDFA) and Tyler Lacy (developmental). If Hutch gets doubled — which he will be, constantly — there's no counterpunch.

- **LT vacancy:** Taylor Decker asked for his release and got it ($9.4M in dead money). The Lions signed Larry Borom (1 year, 38 career starts) as a stopgap. Borom is a swing tackle, not a starting LT. Goff's blindside is unprotected.

- **Safety injuries:** Brian Branch (Achilles) and Kerby Joseph (knee) both went down with season-ending injuries in 2025. Both are expected back, but the timelines are uncertain. If one isn't ready Week 1, the secondary has a gaping hole.

The Lions have $36-42M in cap space (11th in the NFL), but they also have $26.5M in dead money and only nine draft picks (no 3rd-rounder). That's not enough to rebuild depth across three positions while also extending Hutchinson and Joseph.

### The Injury Problem: No Safety Net

Branch and Joseph were supposed to be the foundation of the secondary for the next five years. Both are on rookie contracts. Both are scheme fits for Kelvin Sheppard's aggressive 4-3 defense. Both were healthy in 2024.

In 2025, both went down. The Lions signed Christian Izien as a stopgap, but he's not a long-term answer. The secondary went from a strength to a liability overnight.

Here's the brutal part: Achilles injuries for safeties are 12-16 month recoveries. Branch tore his in October 2025. That puts his return timeline at October-December 2026 — potentially missing the entire 2026 season. Joseph's knee injury is less severe (8-10 month recovery), but there's no guarantee he's 100% Week 1.

If both aren't ready, the Lions are starting two replacement-level safeties in a year where the offense is supposed to be carrying the load while the defense rebuilds. That's not a recipe for a playoff run.

## So What Was Primary: Roster Gaps or Coaching Instability?

**The honest answer: Both, and they reinforced each other.**

The coordinator turnover meant the offense couldn't compensate for the defensive collapse. The EDGE/LT/safety depth gaps meant injuries had outsized impact. The Ben Johnson departure to Chicago means Detroit faces a division rival who knows the playbook better than the current OC does.

If you forced me to pick one: **The roster gaps were primary.** Coaching instability is survivable if you have depth. The 2025 Lions had elite starters and nothing behind them. When Muhammad walked, Decker left, and Branch/Joseph went down, the depth chart collapsed. No amount of coordinator brilliance fixes that.

## The 2026 Verdict

Detroit's championship window is still open, but it's narrower than it was 18 months ago. Goff turns 32 in 2026. Hutchinson's extension is coming and will eat $50M+ AAV of cap space. The 2027 roster has only 24 players under contract, meaning mass rebuilding is required.

Pick #17 is the fulcrum. The Lions can't fix EDGE, LT, and safety depth in one draft. But they can fix one, maybe two. The wrong choice — drafting EDGE when Goff gets pressured off his blindside, or drafting OT when Hutchinson gets doubled into irrelevance — extends the 9-8 mediocrity into 2026.

Dan Campbell has built an elite core. But cores don't win championships. Rosters do. And right now, Detroit's roster is five stars and a bunch of replacement-level depth. That's not enough.

# Draft — Panel Position: EDGE vs. OT at #17

**Article:** The Lions Have $200 Million in Elite Talent. They're One Pick Away From Wasting All of It.
**Panel Role:** Draft board analysis — who's available, what's the value, and how to address both EDGE and OT across R1-R2.

---

## The Board at #17: EDGE Prospects

By the time Detroit is on the clock, the elite EDGE tier — Rueben Bain Jr. (Miami), David Bailey (Texas Tech), and likely Arvell Reese (Ohio State) — will be long gone, probably by pick 8. The Lions are fishing in the second EDGE tier, and here's who's realistically sitting there:

**Keldric Faulk, Auburn (6'6", 276 lbs, 4.74 40)**
Faulk is the safest EDGE option at #17 and the one most frequently mocked to Detroit. He's a big, long, technically sound run defender who can set the edge from Day 1. That's the good news. The concern? He's not a natural pass rusher yet. His sack production at Auburn was pedestrian — he wins with effort and positioning, not with explosive first-step burst. If you're pairing him with Hutchinson, the idea is that Hutch draws the doubles while Faulk cleans up the one-on-ones. That can work, but it requires Faulk to develop counter moves he hasn't consistently shown in college. His floor is a good starter. His ceiling depends on coaching.

**Cashius Howell, Texas A&M (6'2¼", 253 lbs, 4.59 40)**
Howell is the opposite profile — a legitimate pass-rush weapon with SEC Defensive Player of the Year credentials, a plus first step, and a toolbox of moves (speed-to-power, cross-chop, spin). He's the most fun prospect to watch in this tier. But he's undersized — 30¼-inch arms and 253 pounds is small for an every-down edge in the NFL. He can get washed in the run game and struggles to hold the point of attack. Think of him as a designated pass rusher who needs to grow into a complete player. At #17, some boards have him; others see it as a slight reach. If Detroit's coaching staff believes they can develop his run defense, he's got the highest pass-rush upside of anyone available here.

**Dani Dennis-Sutton, Penn State (6'5", 265 lbs)**
Dennis-Sutton is the traits play — prototypical NFL size, long arms, explosive athleticism, can play with his hand down or stand up. The problem is that his college production was more flash than consistency. He'll dominate a snap, then disappear for a quarter. Penn State's coaching staff raved about his ability, but the tape shows a player still learning how to weaponize his gifts on every play. At #17, he's probably a reach on most consensus boards (he grades more as a late-first/early-second prospect). But if Detroit's pro day meeting convinced them he's coachable, the upside is tantalizing — a true two-way EDGE with scheme versatility.

**⚠️ Sleeper:** T.J. Parker (Clemson) had a strong Senior Bowl and is rising on boards. If he tests well, he could sneak into the late-first conversation and complicate Detroit's calculus.

## The Board at #17: OT Prospects

The top two tackles — Francis Mauigoa (Miami) and Spencer Fano (Utah) — are gone before #10. Monroe Freeling (Georgia) is likely gone by #14. That leaves:

**Kadyn Proctor, Alabama (6'7", 352 lbs)**
Proctor is the most talented tackle likely to be available at #17 and some mocks have him going exactly here to Detroit. He's massive, surprisingly athletic for his size, and was a five-star recruit who started as a true freshman at Alabama. His run blocking is mauling. The concerns are consistency and technique — he can be beaten by elite speed on the edge and has lapses in pass protection that you can't have protecting Goff's blindside. But his ceiling is a Pro Bowl left tackle, and the physical tools are All-Pro caliber. If the Lions coaching staff thinks they can refine his technique, this is the pick that protects their $200 million core for the next decade.

**Caleb Lomu, Utah (projected 1-2 round)**
Lomu is the safer, more polished pass-protector. Quick feet, excellent balance, reliable in pass sets. He may not have Proctor's ceiling, but he's closer to being a plug-and-play NFL starter from Week 1. If Detroit needs someone to step in at LT immediately and not get Goff killed in September, Lomu might be the smarter pick. He's a perfect scheme fit for a zone-heavy offense.

**Max Iheanachor, Arizona State (6'6", 321 lbs, 4.95 40)**
Iheanachor is the combine riser — a 4.95-second 40 at 321 pounds is rare movement ability. He's trending toward the late first round and could be the pick if Proctor and Lomu are both gone. More of a right tackle profile, which complicates things since Detroit's need is specifically at left tackle. He's raw but projects as a strong zone-blocking tackle with developmental upside.

## Value Assessment: Who's BPA at #17?

Here's the tension the Lions face: the EDGE class is deeper than the OT class in this draft. Bain Jr. and Bailey go top 10. After that, you're looking at Faulk, Howell, Mesidor, Dennis-Sutton, and Parker — a tier of good-not-great EDGE prospects who grade similarly, roughly in the 15-25 range on most big boards. Meanwhile, Proctor is a genuine top-15 talent who might be the last first-round-caliber left tackle on the board.

If Proctor is sitting there at #17, he's the BPA. Period. He's a higher-graded prospect than any EDGE available at that pick, and left tackle is a premium position. Taking Faulk or Howell at #17 over Proctor would be drafting for need over value — something good teams avoid.

But if Proctor is gone (which is possible — Cleveland at #6, NYG at #5, and other tackle-needy teams could grab him), the BPA calculation shifts, and EDGE becomes the clear play.

## The R1 + R2 Combo: Can Detroit Fix Both?

This is where Detroit's draft capital actually works in their favor, despite having only 7 total picks and no third-rounder. The pick spread between #17 and #50 creates a realistic path to address both positions:

**Scenario A: OT at #17, EDGE at #50 (My Preferred Path)**
Take Proctor (or Lomu) at #17. Then target Dani Dennis-Sutton, Cashius Howell (if he slides), or Akheem Mesidor at #50. This class is deep enough at EDGE that a quality rusher — someone who can start opposite Hutchinson — should still be available in the mid-second round. Dennis-Sutton at #50 would be outstanding value for a player with first-round traits. Mesidor (Miami) is a consistent disruptor who could slide to the 40-50 range.

**Scenario B: EDGE at #17, OT at #50**
Take Faulk or Howell at #17. Then target Max Iheanachor, Blake Miller (Clemson), or Gennings Dunker (Iowa) at #50 for the tackle spot. This path is riskier because the OT class thins out significantly after the first four or five names. Iheanachor at #50 could work, but he profiles more as a right tackle, and Detroit needs a left tackle. Miller is a steady but unspectacular college starter. The second-round OT options don't scream "starter from Day 1 protecting Jared Goff's blindside."

**Scenario C: The Trade-Down Wild Card**
If the Lions' board doesn't fall right — say Proctor is gone and Faulk doesn't feel like BPA — Detroit could trade back from #17 to the early 20s, pick up an extra Day 2 selection, and still get an EDGE or OT at comparable value. The Jimmy Johnson chart says #17 is worth ~950 points; trading to #22-23 and picking up a late third or early fourth is plausible. That extra pick could be the difference between leaving the draft with both positions addressed or leaving with a hole.

---

## My Verdict

**Take the offensive tackle at #17. Go EDGE at #50.**

Here's why: left tackle is the harder position to fill in this draft, and it's the harder position to fill, period. The EDGE class has 6-8 legitimate Day 1-2 starters; the OT class has 4-5. If you let Proctor walk at #17 to grab Faulk, you're betting that a second-round tackle can protect Goff's blindside. That's a bet I wouldn't make with $200 million in locked-up talent depending on it.

Hutchinson is good enough to generate pressure even with a complementary — not elite — EDGE opposite him. He proved that in 2024 before the injury. What he can't do is rush the passer if Goff is getting hit before the ball comes out because the left tackle can't hold up.

The R1-R2 combo gives Detroit a realistic shot at fixing both holes, but only if they sequence it correctly: premium position (OT) first, deeper position (EDGE) second. If they flip that sequence, they risk leaving the draft with a good pass rusher and a shaky offensive line — and that's how you waste a $200 million core.

📊 *Draft board confidence: Medium. Pre-draft boards shift significantly between now and April. Combine/Pro Day performances and team visits will crystallize the tier breaks. But the structural logic — OT is scarcer than EDGE in this class — is unlikely to change.*

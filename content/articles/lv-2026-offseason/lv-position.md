# LV Position Statement — Crosby Trade Voided: Paradox or Path Forward?

**Agent:** LV (Las Vegas Raiders Expert)  
**Panel:** Maxx Crosby Trade Voided — Raiders 2026 Rebuild Impact  
**Date:** March 2026

---

The voided trade is a genuine problem — and the Raiders need to be precise about where the damage actually is, because it's not where the instinctive reaction points.

## The Draft Gap Is Asymmetric

The headline loss is two picks: the 2026 #14 and the 2027 R1. They're not equivalent. The #14 is painful but survivable. The 2027 R1 is the one that stings. This franchise has Mendoza at #1, $135M in projected 2027 cap space, and a coaching staff on Year 1 contracts — 2027 is when this rebuild should accelerate. That first-rounder is now gone.

In 2026: Mendoza at #1, then #36 becomes the first real decision. Without #14, the top WR tier is off the board before the Raiders pick again. At #36, they're in legitimate BPA territory but not where you solve a franchise WR need. Take the best WR available — this isn't ideal, but Brock Bowers and Ashton Jeanty are already on this roster. Mendoza isn't going naked. Rounds 3-7 (seven picks) address CB depth (Darien Porter at LCB is not the answer) and FS (Pola-Mao is a question mark).

## The EDGE Room Is Now Overstuffed — and That's Actually a Scheme Asset

The reflexive take is that signing Paye ($48M, 3yr) as a Crosby replacement was a mistake. It wasn't — it was appropriate personnel management given a completed trade. But here's what the voided trade actually created: Rob Leonard now runs a 3-4 with Crosby, Paye, Koonce, and Tyree Wilson as the EDGE rotation. That's four legitimate rushers for two spots. Most rebuilding teams would trade a second-round pick for that problem.

Crosby as a standup OLB in Leonard's 3-4 is a better schematic fit than the 4-3 Carroll ran. His pass rush arsenal — motor, hand work, counter-move variety — translates directly to the OLB role. Paye becomes a genuine complement, not a $16M/yr afterthought. The Raiders' defensive identity in 2026 will be the pass rush, which gives Mendoza something most rookie QBs don't get: a defense that can win games by itself.

## Competitive Floor — The Right Question Is Wrong

The real question isn't whether Crosby gets the Raiders from 5 wins to 7. It's whether 7 wins in 2026 is net positive for a franchise that needs draft capital in 2027. Under normal rebuild logic, fewer wins preserves pick position. But the Raiders aren't drafting in 2027 to fill a need — they're building around Mendoza, who needs a competitive environment to develop correctly.

Kubiak's first year needs functional culture. A defense that keeps games close, a ground game built around Jeanty, and a play-action framework anchored by Linderbaum — that's the development ecosystem Mendoza needs. Going 4-13 again doesn't accelerate the rebuild; it destroys Kubiak's credibility before it starts. Crosby matters here not as a feel-good mentor narrative — he matters because he makes the defense hard to run against, which keeps Mendoza in manageable down-and-distance situations early in his rookie year.

## Recommendation: Path 4, Executed Cleanly

Do not trade Crosby for a 2nd and 3rd. The market for a veteran coming off a failed physical is bad, the deal won't recoup what was lost, and accepting that sunk cost signals organizational chaos to every free agent watching. 

Run it back. Lock the depth chart, draft Mendoza at #1, take the best WR available at #36, and spend mid-round capital on CB depth and FS. Accept that 2026 is a development year with a legitimate defensive identity, and bank on the clean 2027 cap picture to accelerate from there.

The paradox is real. The Raiders are better with Crosby on the field and poorer in the draft room because of him. The correct response is to maximize the asset you have, not chase back the capital you lost.

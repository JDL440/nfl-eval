## 🔴 ERRORS

### 🔴 E1. The cap-crunch section materially overstates the 2026 squeeze

**Locations:** TLDR line 7; lines 59-70; line 87.

The article treats **Kwity Paye's $16M AAV as if it were his 2026 cap hit**, then stacks that on top of a **gross rookie-pool subtraction** to conclude the Raiders are effectively underwater before any further moves.

That is not cap-accurate enough for publication:

- Paye's reported 2026 cap hit is about **$9.4M**, not $16M.
- The article's **$51.8M edge-allocation** figure therefore overstates the 2026 cap commitment.
- The rookie-class math also uses the wrong offseason framing. Under the NFL's **Top-51 rule**, teams plan around the **effective rookie pool**, not the full gross total of every rookie hold. Current reporting puts the Raiders' effective rookie impact much closer to the low-teens than the combined **$24.9M** subtraction used here.

Because this section is the backbone of the article's argument, the current math is too distorted to leave as-is. The thesis can still work, but the cap section needs to be recalculated and rewritten with true cap-hit / effective-rookie-pool logic.

**Fix:** Rework the entire cap table and every downstream sentence that depends on it. If you want to use AAV as an "investment" lens, label it as AAV/cash commitment rather than 2026 cap allocation.

### 🔴 E2. The draft-pick count is stale

**Locations:** lines 59-60; line 69.

The draft says the Raiders have the No. 1 pick plus **"the remaining eight picks."** The current official Raiders draft tracker shows **10 total 2026 picks**, meaning **nine** selections remain after No. 1: 36, 67, 102, 117, 134, 175, 185, 208, and 219.

That stale pick count matters because it feeds both:

- the cap table's rookie-hold estimate, and
- the article's framing of how much draft flexibility the Raiders still have after the trade reversal.

**Fix:** Update the piece to the current official 2026 pick inventory and then recalculate any rookie-pool or roster-building language that depends on it.

## 🟡 SUGGESTIONS

### 🟡 S1. State the Raiders' 2025 record and No. 1-pick context earlier

The piece never cleanly grounds the reader in the simplest starting fact: **Las Vegas went 3-14 and earned the No. 1 pick**. That context is essential to understanding why the rebuild framing matters so much.

**Best place:** the opening section or the first comparison table.

### 🟡 S2. Tighten quote precision in the cap section

The blockquote at line 78 uses an ellipsis and stitches together the spirit of Cap's position rather than a clean verbatim line. That's editorially fine in substance, but blockquote formatting reads like a direct quote.

**Fix:** Either quote the source exactly or convert it to indirect attribution.

### 🟡 S3. Separate "better team" from "better rebuild" a bit earlier

The article's strongest idea is the paradox in line 20: **better on Sundays, worse for the rebuild.** Right now the first long cap section delays that payoff slightly.

**Fix:** Add one short orienting sentence near the end of the intro or at the top of Section 1 that explicitly says: the football team improved, but the resource picture worsened.

### 🟡 S4. Clarify whether numbers are cap hits, cash, or AAV throughout

This draft uses "cap hit," "allocation," and annual value interchangeably in a few places. Even after the factual cap fixes, readers will follow the piece more easily if each number is labeled by type.

**Fix:** Use one of these labels consistently in tables:

- 2026 cap hit
- 2026 cash
- contract AAV

The central news peg checks out: Crosby's Ravens trade was agreed, then unwound after the failed physical. The basic offseason context around **Crosby, Paye, Linderbaum, Mendoza, Jeanty, and Rob Leonard's 3-4 shift** is current enough for a 2026 pre-draft article.

This draft hits the expected house format:

- strong headline and subhead  
- TLDR near the top  
- multiple tables  
- expert disagreement surfaced clearly  
- clear verdict section  
- boilerplate CTA and next-story tease

### 🟢 N3. The writing is strong

The piece is readable, confident, and has a real argument. Lines like **"The Raiders are undeniably better at football with Crosby on the roster. They're also in a worse rebuilding position because of it."** give the article a clean spine. This reads like an actual take, not a stitched summary.

### 🟢 N4. The panel synthesis works

The article does a good job distinguishing:

- **LV** = quarterback ecosystem / competitive floor  
- **Cap** = preserving the 2027 runway  
- **Defense** = edge-overload vs. secondary weakness

That division of labor makes the disagreement legible instead of noisy.

### 🟢 N5. Temporal framing is mostly correct

The piece consistently reads as a **March 2026, pre-draft** article. It does not feel seasonally stale. The main temporal issue is not the calendar voice; it's the now-outdated pick-count/cap math in the middle.

## Final Verdict

### 🟡 REVISE

This is **close**, but the cap section is too important to be wrong on mechanics. The core argument survives editorial review, yet the published version cannot use:

- Paye's **AAV as 2026 cap hit**
- a **gross rookie-pool subtraction** as effective offseason cap pressure
- an outdated **eight-picks-remaining** draft count

Fix those factual issues, keep the thesis, and this should be in good shape for approval on the next pass.

# Discussion Prompt: The Bears Traded Their Best Receiver and Let Three Safeties Walk. That Might Be the Smartest Offseason in the NFL.

**Depth Level:** 2 — The Beat

## The Core Question

Can the Bears close the gap on Detroit and Green Bay within the Caleb Williams rookie-deal window by building through the draft and mid-tier free agency, or does restraint in the NFL's toughest division guarantee they waste the cheapest franchise-QB years in football?

## Key Tensions

- **Restraint vs. urgency:** The Bears went 5-12 → 11-6 in one year, then *subtracted* their WR1 (DJ Moore trade) and let all three starting safeties walk. Every contender in NFL history tries to maximize the cheap-QB window. Chicago is stockpiling draft capital instead. Is Ryan Poles learning the right lesson from the Khalil Mack era (don't mortgage the future), or the wrong lesson (don't be aggressive when the window is open)?

- **The Khalil Mack paradox:** Poles confirmed he explored a Maxx Crosby blockbuster and walked away. The ghost of the 2018 Mack trade — where Chicago surrendered picks for a star and got one great year followed by a six-year playoff drought — haunts every decision. But the Mack lesson cuts both ways: the Bears went 12-4 in Mack's first season and nearly won a Super Bowl. Sometimes the "reckless" move IS the right one. Does the Crosby non-trade define this offseason as disciplined or timid?

- **Secondary rebuild mid-ascent:** Dennis Allen's defense led the NFL in takeaways (33) in 2025 — and the three safeties who generated most of those turnovers (Brisker, Gardner-Johnson, Byard) are all gone. Coby Bryant ($40M/3yr from SEA) is a solid starter but not a Pro Bowler. Can Allen's scheme — which demands playmaking safeties with man-coverage ability and ball skills — survive a full positional reset at the most important DB position?

- **NFC North arms race:** Detroit has $35.9M in cap space and Aidan Hutchinson returning from injury. Green Bay traded for Micah Parsons. Minnesota added Jonathan Allen and Javon Hargrave. The division is stacking premium talent through aggressive moves. The Bears are the only NFC North team playing it conservative. Is that positioning for sustained success, or falling behind in a sprint?

## The Paths

### Path 1: Stay the Course — Draft-and-Develop, Trust the Process
Poles' strategy is working. The 5-12 → 11-6 turnaround proves the roster is good. Don't overpay for veterans when you have a #25 pick, two 2nds, and Caleb Williams at $1M. Draft a safety, add DT/EDGE depth, and let the young core (Odunze, Burden, Dexter, Wright) grow together. This team is built to compete for 5+ years, not spike for one.

**Risk:** Detroit and Green Bay aren't waiting. The NFC North is a knife fight — building patiently while your rivals add Parsons and get Hutchinson back could mean going from "almost" to "too late." Two playoff-less years and the Williams extension arrives anyway.

### Path 2: One Aggressive Move — Make the Crosby Call or Find the Equivalent
Poles was right to explore Crosby. He should find the next aggressive opportunity — a disgruntled pass rusher, a trade-up for a top safety, something that signals "we're here to win NOW." The DJ Moore trade freed cap and picks. Use them. The Mack era failed because the roster around Mack was bad, not because the trade was bad. This roster is good. Add the missing piece.

**Risk:** The "missing piece" mentality is how you end up overpaying. Crosby's knee, his $106.5M contract, and the two-1st price tag all point to a bad deal. Finding a genuine value add without repeating Mack is harder than it sounds.

### Path 3: The Hybrid — Draft Heavy, but Trade Up for THE Safety
Split the difference. Use the two 2nd-rounders (#57 + #60) to trade up into the late teens and grab the best safety in the class (McNeil-Warren? Thieneman?). Address the biggest single need aggressively while keeping the long-term draft pipeline intact. Still have picks in R3-R7 for depth.

**Risk:** Trading up in R1 costs future flexibility and you're betting a lot on one prospect replacing three veteran safeties. If the pick busts, you've lost both the draft capital AND the safety room.

### Path 4: Reassess the Timeline — This Is a 2027 Contender, Not 2026
Accept that the NFC North is too loaded in 2026. Detroit and Green Bay are ahead. Use 2026 as a development year for the young talent (Year 3 Williams, Year 2 Odunze/Burden, Dexter's breakout). Bank the cap savings from the Moore trade and veteran departures. Attack 2027 free agency with maximum flexibility right as Williams hits his prime.

**Risk:** Wasting a year of $1M Caleb Williams is an enormous opportunity cost. And "planning for next year" is how franchises become perpetual bridesmaids. The Bears haven't won a Super Bowl since 1985. Their fans won't accept "wait 'til next year" from an 11-6 team.

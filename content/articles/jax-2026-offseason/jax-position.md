## The Hunter Deployment Framework: What Year 1 Actually Taught Us

Let's ground this in reality before we build Year 2 projections. Travis Hunter played **7 games** in 2025 before the LCL injury ended his season. Here's the actual usage data from those 7 games:

**Snap distribution:**
- **67% offensive snaps** (WR2 in 3WR sets) — 28 rec, 298 yds, 1 TD
- **36% defensive snaps** (CB1 in base 4-3) — 15 tackles, 3 PD, 0 INT
- **Total combined snaps:** 103% of available snaps per game on average (offense + defense + special teams)

That last number is the tell. Hunter was asked to play more than a full game's worth of snaps in his NFL rookie debut. The LCL injury wasn't a fluke contact play — it was a **workload management failure**. His body broke down in Week 8 because the Jaguars tried to deploy him as two full-time starters instead of one human being.

**The Year 1 lesson:** You can't run Hunter at 100%+ snap rate and expect him to stay healthy. The two-way experiment requires accepting that he'll be a **part-time player on both sides of the ball** — not a full-time star on either.

## The Year 2 Deployment Plan: 55/25 Offense-Heavy Split

If we're building Year 2 deployment around **sustainability + contention**, here's the realistic framework:

### The 55/25 Model (80% total snap rate)

| Side | Snap % | Role | Snaps/Game (est.) |
|------|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Offense** | 55% | WR2 in 3WR sets; red zone + 3rd down specialist | ~37 snaps |
| **Defense** | 25% | CB1 in dime/nickel packages; sub-package closer | ~17 snaps |
| **Total** | 80% | Combined workload | ~54 snaps |

**Why offense-heavy?**

1. **The talent allocation math:** Hunter graded as an 82 PFF receiver and a 71 PFF corner in his 7-game sample. He's *better* at WR right now — not because he can't play CB, but because his CB technique (press coverage footwork, hip fluidity in breaks) is still developing. His WR skill set (route-running, ball tracking, hands) translated to the NFL immediately.

2. **The scheme fit:** Grant Udinski's offense is built around **3WR/1TE with RPO concepts**. Hunter as the WR2 (opposite Brian Thomas Jr. at WR1) creates a matchup nightmare: defenses can't double both perimeter threats, which opens up Parker Washington in the slot and Brenton Strange on seam routes. When Hunter plays WR, the entire offense unlocks.

3. **The developmental need:** Trevor Lawrence just turned 27. This is his prime window. He needs weapons, not defensive help. Leaning Hunter toward offense maximizes the investment in Lawrence's supporting cast while the QB is still on a team-friendly $24M cap hit (before it jumps to $35M in 2027 and $47M in 2028).

**Why not 50/50?**

Because 100%+ snap rate already broke him. The LCL injury wasn't a random event — it was the predictable outcome of asking a 215-pound rookie to absorb NFL contact on both sides of the ball for 7 consecutive weeks. **The goal isn't to maximize Hunter's usage; it's to maximize his availability over 17 games.**

A 55/25 split keeps him under 80% total snap rate, which is sustainable. It also creates a role identity: **Hunter is a WR who moonlights at CB in sub-packages** — not a CB who moonlights at WR.

## The BTJ Question: Is Hunter the Problem or the Solution?

Brian Thomas Jr.'s sophomore slump is real and documented:

| Year | GP | Rec | Yards | TD | YPR |
|------|----|----|-------|----|----|
| 2024 (Rookie) | 17 | 87 | 1,282 | 10 | 14.7 |
| 2025 (Soph) | 14 | 48 | 707 | 2 | 14.7 |

That's a **45% drop in production** despite identical yards-per-reception. The question: did Hunter's arrival cannibalize BTJ's targets, or is this unrelated?

**The target share reality:**

In the 7 games Hunter played before injury:
- BTJ: 6.1 targets/game (43 total targets in 7 games)
- Hunter: 4.0 targets/game (28 total targets in 7 games)
- Jakobi Meyers: 5.4 targets/game
- Parker Washington: 3.9 targets/game

In the 7 games after Hunter's injury (Weeks 9–15):
- BTJ: 5.4 targets/game (38 total targets in 7 games)
- Meyers: 6.0 targets/game
- Washington: 5.1 targets/game

**The verdict:** Hunter's presence reduced BTJ's target share by ~0.7 targets per game (6.1 → 5.4). That's real but not catastrophic. The bigger issue is **scheme predictability**. When Hunter went down, defenses rolled coverage toward BTJ as the clear WR1. Without Hunter to force single coverage on the perimeter, BTJ faced more bracket coverage and safety help over the top.

**The conclusion:** Hunter doesn't hurt BTJ — Hunter *helps* BTJ by creating single coverage opportunities. BTJ's slump is a **sophomore coverage adjustment** problem, not a target allocation problem. When Hunter returns in Year 2 and plays 55% of offensive snaps, BTJ will benefit from easier 1-on-1 matchups.

GM Trent Baalke called the BTJ trade rumors "fraudulent" for a reason: the front office understands that Hunter + BTJ is a **complementary pairing**, not a competition.

## The Roster Holes: Can JAX Survive Without a 1st-Round Pick?

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the Hunter trade cost Jacksonville their **2026 1st-round pick** (#19 overall based on 13-4 finish). That pick would've gone to one of these positions:

- **RB:** Ashton Jeanty (Boise State) or Kaleb Johnson (Iowa) — immediate Etienne replacement
- **LB:** Jalon Walker (Georgia) or Jihaad Campbell (Alabama) — Devin Lloyd replacement
- **CB:** Chandler Rivers (Duke) or Keith Abney II (South Carolina) — Hunter's CB running mate

Instead, Jacksonville's first pick is **#56 in Round 2**. That's the tier break. The difference between #19 and #56 is the difference between "franchise-altering talent" and "good starter if developed correctly."

**Can they survive?**

Yes — but only if the Hunter investment **pays off as a dual-threat weapon**. Here's the roster-building math:

### The RB Solution (Round 2)
- **Target:** Trey Benson (Florida State) at #56 — 4.41 speed, 230 pounds, 1,400+ yards in 2025
- **Backup plan:** Jaydn Ott (Cal) in Round 3 — plus-receiving back who fits Udinski's RPO scheme
- **Why it works:** Bhayshul Tuten (drafted in 2025 via Hunter trade package, #104 overall) showed flashes in limited action. Pair him with a Round 2 power back and you've replaced Etienne's 240-carry workload.

### The LB Solution (Round 3)
- **Target:** Curtis Jacobs (Penn State) at #88 (via Detroit trade) — 4.52 speed, 230 pounds, can cover TEs
- **Why it works:** Foyesade Oluokun ($17.1M cap hit) is the MLB anchor. Ventrell Miller is the SAM. Adding Jacobs as the WILL gives Anthony Campanile a three-LB rotation that can handle both run fits and pass coverage.

### The CB Solution (Free Agency Bargain Hunting)
- **Target:** Myles Bryant (NE) or Rock Ya-Sin (LV) — sub-$5M/year veteran CBs who can play CB2/NB
- **Why it works:** Christian Braswell and Jarrian Jones are young and unproven, but they're not hopeless. Add a veteran CB2 on a 1-year deal ($4M–5M) to stabilize the rotation while Hunter plays CB1 in sub-packages. This isn't ideal, but it's *survivable* for 2026.

**The 2027 reset:** Next year, Jacksonville projects to **$51M+ in cap space** and has their 1st-round pick back. The 2026 season is about *surviving the crunch* while maintaining the 13-4 baseline. The 2027 season is about *capitalizing on cap relief* to build a true contender.

## The Hardest Truth: Hunter's Health Is the Entire Plan

Every projection above assumes Travis Hunter is **healthy and available for 17 games**. The LCL injury was a Grade 2 sprain with a 10–12 week recovery timeline. He's expected to be 100% for OTAs and training camp. But here's the risk profile:

**If Hunter plays 17 games at 55/25 split:**
- The offense gets a WR2 who creates single coverage for BTJ and Lawrence
- The defense gets a CB1 in sub-packages who can lock down slot receivers and play safety help over the top
- The roster holes (RB, LB, CB depth) are *survivable* because Hunter's dual-threat value compensates for missing a 1st-round pick

**If Hunter misses 4+ games due to injury:**
- The CB room collapses (Braswell/Jones/Taylor can't hold up as CB1/CB2)
- The WR room loses its second-best weapon (BTJ faces more bracket coverage)
- The Hunter trade becomes a **sunk cost** — four picks including a 1st-rounder for a part-time player who can't stay healthy

**The deployment recommendation:** 55% offense, 25% defense, and **strict in-season workload monitoring**. If Hunter's snap count creeps above 80% combined in any multi-game stretch, Liam Coen needs to pull him back. The goal is 17 games of availability, not 7 games of dominance followed by IR.

**Year 2 deployment:** 55% offensive snaps (WR2 role), 25% defensive snaps (CB1 in sub-packages). This offense-heavy split maximizes Hunter's current strength (WR) while preserving his long-term availability (avoiding 100%+ snap overload).

**BTJ outcome:** Not related to Hunter's presence. BTJ's slump is a sophomore coverage adjustment. He'll bounce back in Year 2 when Hunter forces single coverage opportunities.

**Roster holes:** Survivable via Round 2 RB (Benson), Round 3 LB (Jacobs), and bargain-bin CB2 (Bryant/Ya-Sin). The 2026 season is about maintaining the 13-4 baseline while waiting for 2027's $51M cap reset.

## Hardest Tradeoff

**Hunter's workload ceiling vs. Jacksonville's contention timeline.** The two-way experiment only works if Hunter stays healthy. But keeping him healthy requires accepting that he'll never be a **full-time elite player at either position** — he'll always be a part-time contributor on both sides. That's the paradox: the thing that makes Hunter unique (two-way ability) is also the thing that limits his ceiling (can't play 100% of snaps at WR *or* CB). Jacksonville bet four draft picks that "part-time All-Pro at two positions" is more valuable than "full-time All-Pro at one position." The LCL injury suggests that bet has a **razor-thin margin for error**.

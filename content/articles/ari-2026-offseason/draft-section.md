## The #3 Pick Menu: What's Actually There

Let's kill the mystery. If Arizona stays at #3 — after Las Vegas takes Mendoza at #1 and the Jets likely grab Arvell Reese at #2 — here's what's on the board:

| Prospect | Pos | School | Big Board Rank | Why at #3 |
|----------|-----|--------|:--------------:|-----------|
| **Rueben Bain Jr.** | EDGE | Miami (FL) | 3 | Consensus top-5 EDGE. Dense, technically refined, scheme-versatile. Did not test at the Combine — but the tape doesn't need a stopwatch. |
| **David Bailey** | EDGE | Texas Tech | 8 | Explosive first step, legitimate coverage ability as a stand-up rusher. Some mocks have him top-5; others see him as a #6-10 guy. Slight reach at #3 but defensible. |
| **Francis Mauigoa** | OT | Miami (FL) | 6 | Massive, athletic, dominant. The best tackle in this class. Skipped Combine workouts, but at 6'5" 330+ with his movement skills, nobody cares. Multiple mocks actually have ARI taking Mauigoa here. |
| **Jeremiyah Love** | RB | Notre Dame | 4 | Back-to-back 1,000-yard seasons, receiving threat, top-5 talent. But a running back at #3? In 2026? I'd need to be sedated. |
| **Caleb Downs** | S | Ohio State | 5 | Consensus top-5 talent, day-one starter. But safety at #3 is Jamal Adams territory — the value math rarely works. |

**My board at #3, if ARI stays put:** Bain Jr. > Mauigoa > Bailey. That's the order. Everything else is a reach or a positional value mistake.

### Rueben Bain Jr. — The Pick I'd Make

Bain is the real deal. At Miami, he was the most technically polished pass rusher in college football — not the most explosive (that's Bailey), not the most physically imposing (that's Mesidor), but the most *complete*. He wins with hand usage, leverage, and an understanding of offensive tackle sets that you rarely see from college edge rushers. He's compact and dense — built like a shorter Myles Garrett — and his ability to convert speed to power at the point of attack is elite.

The knock: he didn't test at the Combine. That matters for the analytics models, but it doesn't change what you see on tape. NFL scouts I've tracked through mock draft consensus have him firmly in the top 3-5 range regardless.

**Comp:** Cameron Jordan with better bend. High floor, Pro Bowl ceiling, 10-year starter.

### Francis Mauigoa — The Case for Tackle

Here's where I'll disagree with the consensus: **Mauigoa at #3 is not a reach.** Multiple mocks actually project this exact pick. Arizona's offensive line was a disaster in 2025 — whoever plays quarterback next needs time to throw. Mauigoa is the best tackle prospect in this class by a comfortable margin and projects as a 12-year left tackle.

The problem? EDGE is a more valuable position than offensive tackle in modern NFL value models. And Arizona can address tackle at #34 or in free agency. You can't replicate Bain's pass-rush upside later in the draft.

**Comp:** Penei Sewell's draft profile — massive, young, rare movement skills for his size.

## Ty Simpson: What the Film Actually Shows

This is where the article gets interesting. Simpson is the prospect everyone's buzzing about as ARI's late-first target — but almost nobody has actually broken down the tape beyond "he's accurate and fits the scheme." So let's do that.

### The Good

**Play-action accuracy is legitimate.** Simpson's completion percentage on play-action passes at Alabama was elite — we're talking 70%+ on boot-action and rollout throws, which is exactly what LaFleur's offense asks for. His ball placement on short-to-intermediate routes (0-15 yards) is genuinely impressive. He hits receivers in stride on crossers, digs, and out-breaking routes with consistent timing.

**Pocket presence is advanced for the start count.** For a guy with only 15 college starts, Simpson processes the short-to-intermediate field faster than you'd expect. He doesn't panic under first pressure. He slides in the pocket, keeps his eyes downfield, and delivers. That's coachable movement, not raw athleticism masquerading as poise.

**The arm is real.** Simpson has a strong arm — he can make every NFL throw. The deep ball isn't his calling card, but he has the velocity to push it vertically when the play-action creates one-on-one opportunities downfield.

### The Bad

**The deep ball is inconsistent.** Simpson's accuracy drops meaningfully on throws beyond 20 yards. His deep-ball placement tends to hang — not quite enough air under it on go routes, not enough zip on corner routes to beat Cover 2. This is a fixable mechanical issue (footwork, hip rotation), but it's a real concern for a team that needs to maximize Marvin Harrison Jr.

**He's a one-read-progression passer right now.** When the first read isn't there, Simpson's processing slows noticeably. He either checks down immediately or holds the ball too long. In Alabama's offense, this was masked by elite skill talent — in the NFL, it gets you sacked or picked.

**Fifteen starts is fifteen starts.** I don't care how you spin it — that's a razor-thin sample. For context:

| QB | College Starts Before Draft | Draft Pos | NFL Outcome |
|----|:---------------------------:|:---------:|-------------|
| Patrick Mahomes | 30 | #10 | GOAT-track |
| Deshaun Watson | 33 | #12 | Elite (on-field) |
| Joe Burrow | 30 | #1 | Franchise QB |
| Josh Allen | 17 | #7 | MVP-caliber |
| Trey Lance | 17 | #3 | Bust |
| **Ty Simpson** | **15** | **~#25-30?** | **???** |

Josh Allen made it work with limited starts, but Allen had a physical profile that broke the combine. Simpson doesn't. Trey Lance had a similar start count and a *better* athletic profile — and San Francisco spent the #3 overall pick on him. He was out of the league by 25.

**The floor is a career backup. The ceiling is Kirk Cousins.** That's not an insult — Cousins had a 12-year career and made $250M+. But it's not a franchise-altering outcome. Simpson's profile is a high-floor, moderate-ceiling game manager who can run a play-action offense at an above-average level. That's valuable. It's just not *exciting*.

### Bottom Line on Simpson

Simpson is a legitimate late-first-round talent — I'd have him QB2 in this class behind Mendoza, ahead of Nussmeier. He's worth a pick in the #25-30 range. He is *not* worth the #3 overall pick, and any mock that puts him there is committing malpractice.

## The Trade-Back Math: Can ARI Get Both Bain AND Simpson?

This is the scenario that makes the most sense — and the math actually works. Here's how:

### Step 1: Take Bain Jr. (EDGE) at #3

Stay put. Draft the best pass rusher in the class. No trade-down needed for this part.

### Step 2: Trade Back INTO the First Round for Simpson

Arizona has its full Day 2-3 draft capital. To move back into the late first round, they'd need to trade up from #34 (their natural R2 pick) to the #25-30 range.

**The Jimmy Johnson chart math:**

| Pick | JJ Value |
|------|:--------:|
| #25 (CHI) | 720 |
| #26 (BUF) | 700 |
| #27 (SF) | 680 |
| #28 (HOU) | 660 |
| #34 (ARI R2) | 560 |
| #65 (ARI R3) | 265 |
| #104 (ARI R4) | 130 |

**Trade Package to move from #34 → ~#27-28:**

ARI sends: **#34 (560) + #104 (130) = 690 points**
ARI receives: **~#27-28 (660-680 points)**

That's a clean deal. Arizona moves up roughly 6-7 spots in the first round by packaging their R2 pick and a R4 pick. The selling team (SF at #27 or Houston at #28) gets extra Day 2-3 capital — something both teams could use.

**Alternative:** If Simpson slides to #30-31, it's even cheaper:

ARI sends: **#34 (560) + #143 (R5, ~27 points) = 587 points**
ARI receives: **~#30-31 (600-620 points)**

That's almost free. A R2 and R5 for a late first. The fifth-year option alone on a first-round pick makes this math favorable.

### What ARI Gives Up in This Scenario

| What They Lose | What They Gain |
|----------------|---------------|
| R2 pick (#34) | Ty Simpson, QB, late R1 — with 5th-year option |
| R4 pick (#104) | Rueben Bain Jr., EDGE, #3 overall |
| — | Two first-round-caliber starters on Day 1 |

**Remaining draft capital after the trade:** R3 (#65), R5 (#143), R6 (#183), R7 (#217). That's still four picks to fill depth, plus the two first-rounders. Totally workable.

### The Alternative: Trade DOWN from #3

This is the scenario I hear most often — "trade #3 for a massive haul." Let's run the numbers.

**#3 = 2,200 JJ points.** To trade down to, say, #10 (Cincinnati):

| ARI Sends | ARI Receives | Point Math |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| #3 (2,200) | #10 (1,300) + CIN 2027 R1 (~850 discounted) + CIN R3 2026 (~145) | = ~2,295 points |

That's roughly fair by chart. But here's the problem: **who's trading up to #3?** This isn't a QB-rich draft. Mendoza goes #1. After that, there's no prospect compelling enough to make a team mortgage future capital to jump to #3. The trade market for #3 is *soft* in a non-QB draft.

In QB-driven drafts (2018, 2021, 2024), teams pay 20-30% *above* chart value to move into the top 3. In defense-heavy drafts like this one? You're lucky to get chart value. Arizona's leverage to trade down is limited unless a surprise QB run develops on draft night.

**My recommendation: Stay at #3. Take Bain. Trade back in for Simpson.** It's the cleanest path.

## Historical Comps: Top-5 Picks After Breaking Up With Your Franchise QB

Arizona's situation — holding a top-5 pick immediately after ending a franchise QB era — has happened before. The results are instructive:

| Team | Year | Situation | Pick | Selection | Outcome |
|------|------|-----------|------|-----------|---------|
| **IND Colts** | 2012 | Released Peyton Manning | #1 | Andrew Luck, QB | ✅ Franchise-altering (retired early, but the pick was right) |
| **CHI Bears** | 2017 | Traded Jay Cutler | #3 | Trade up for Mitch Trubisky, QB | ❌ Franchise-damaging. Passed on Mahomes and Watson. |
| **CLE Browns** | 2018 | Moved on from everyone | #1 | Baker Mayfield, QB | ⚠️ Mixed. Best option in a strong class, but unstable long-term. |
| **IND Colts** | 2018 | Post-Luck retirement fallout | #6 | Quenton Nelson, G | ✅ Generational guard pick. Built the roster instead of reaching for QB. |
| **CAR Panthers** | 2020 | Released Cam Newton | #7 | Derrick Brown, DT | ⚠️ Good player, but Carolina's still searching for a QB four years later. |
| **MIA Dolphins** | 2020 | Tanked for Tua | #5 | Tua Tagovailoa, QB | ⚠️ Productive but ultimately moved on (health concerns). |

**The pattern:** Teams that *reach* for a QB who isn't there (CHI taking Trubisky over Watson and Mahomes) get burned for a decade. Teams that take the best player available and address QB through other channels (IND taking Nelson in 2018) tend to build stronger rosters — *if* they eventually find the QB.

The 2012 Colts are the fairy tale: Manning leaves, Luck falls in your lap. But that's a once-in-a-generation alignment. Arizona doesn't have that luxury. Mendoza's going #1. There's no Andrew Luck sitting at #3.

**The cautionary tale is Carolina.** They took Derrick Brown at #7 after cutting Cam — a perfectly good player at a non-premium position — and have spent four years and counting in QB purgatory. If Simpson doesn't work out, that's Arizona's risk: a great defense, no quarterback, and Marvin Harrison Jr.'s prime ticking away.

## Is There a QB Worth #3 in This Class?

No. I'm being blunt: **there is no quarterback worth the #3 overall pick in the 2026 draft.**

Mendoza is the class. He's a Heisman winner, a National Championship winner, a 6'5" arm talent with a Joe Burrow-level rise from transfer portal anonymity to consensus #1 overall. He goes to Las Vegas. End of story.

After Mendoza, here's the QB landscape:

| QB | School | Round Projection | Why Not #3 |
|----|--------|:----------------:|------------|
| Ty Simpson | Alabama | Late R1 (~25-30) | 15 starts. One-read processor. Ceiling is game manager. Not a top-5 talent. |
| Garrett Nussmeier | LSU | R2-R3 | Productive SEC passer, but lacks the arm talent and athleticism to separate. Day 2 guy. |
| Carson Beck | Miami (FL) | R3+ | Serious questions about NFL readiness. Injury concerns. Developmental. |
| Dante Moore | Oregon | R3+ | Athletic but raw. Oregon couldn't make him the full-time starter. |

Simpson is the QB2 and he's a late-first talent. Taking him at #3 would be a 20+ pick reach — the kind of move that gets GMs fired. The 2027 class (with Arch Manning, Nico Iamaleava, and LaNorris Sellers potentially available) is projected to be significantly stronger at quarterback. If Simpson isn't the answer, the better QB class is 12 months away.

## Where I Disagree With the Panel

Let me be direct about where I expect friction:

**I disagree with anyone who says "take the QB at #3 no matter what."** The Trubisky lesson is real. Reaching for need over talent at the top of the draft is how you waste a decade. Simpson isn't a #3 talent. Period. Don't let quarterback desperation cloud the evaluation.

**I disagree with the "trade #3 for a haul" crowd.** The trade-down market is soft in a non-QB draft. Arizona's not getting three first-round picks for the #3 slot when nobody behind Mendoza is worth trading up for. Stay put, take Bain, and use the remaining capital to trade back in for Simpson. You get more total value that way.

**I *agree* with the Offense agent on scheme fit** — Simpson's play-action accuracy maps perfectly to what LaFleur runs. That's real. My concern is whether the rest of the profile (deep-ball inconsistency, limited processing, tiny sample size) is good enough to build around. Scheme fit without baseline talent is Marcus Mariota.

**My biggest concern is one the ARI agent will raise:** the Marvin Harrison Jr. timeline. Harrison is entering Year 3 on a rookie deal. Every year without a real quarterback is a year of his cost-controlled production wasted. If Simpson takes 2 years to develop behind Brissett/Minshew, Harrison will be in Year 5 — extension territory — before he has a legitimate passer. That's a ticking clock, and it's the strongest argument for being more aggressive at QB.

**Take Rueben Bain Jr. at #3. Trade #34 + #104 to move back into the #27-28 range for Ty Simpson. Walk out of Day 1 with a franchise pass rusher and a developmental quarterback who fits your scheme.**

This gives Arizona:
- ✅ An elite EDGE rusher on a rookie deal (Bain)
- ✅ A scheme-fit QB with a 5th-year option (Simpson)
- ✅ Remaining Day 2-3 capital to address OL and depth
- ✅ The 2027 draft as a safety net if Simpson doesn't develop

The trade-back-in math works. The value is there. The risk is manageable.

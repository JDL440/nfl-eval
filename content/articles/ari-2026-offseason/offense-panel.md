# 🏈 OFFENSE PANEL — Offensive Scheme Expert

## The Schematic Reset: Mike LaFleur Is Burning the Air Raid to the Ground (And He Should)

---

### 1. What Does Mike LaFleur's Offense Actually Look Like?

Forget everything you knew about the Cardinals' offense under Kingsbury. This isn't a tweak — it's a full operating-system swap.

**The Kingsbury air-raid was built on four pillars:** shotgun formation on nearly every snap (top-3 NFL rate), 10 personnel (4 WR sets) at ~30% usage (league-leading), below-average play-action rates, and tempo-driven quick reads. It was designed for one player — Kyler Murray — and it showed. When Murray went down in 2025, the offense didn't bend. It broke. Arizona finished 23rd in points per game (20.9) despite ranking 5th in passing yards per game (256.1) because the scheme demanded a dynamic scrambler to function. No scrambler, no offense.

**LaFleur's offense is the photographic negative of that.** Here's what's coming:

| Scheme Element | Kingsbury (ARI) | LaFleur (Projected) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary formation | Shotgun (~85%+) | Under center / split (~55-60% under center) |
| Base personnel | 10/11 personnel (spread) | 11/12/21 personnel (multiple) |
| Play-action rate | Below average (~22-24%) | Above average (~28-32%) |
| Pre-snap motion | Moderate | Heavy (Shanahan-tree signature) |
| Run scheme | Designed runs, RPOs | Outside zone, wide zone, boot action |
| QB read progression | Half-field, quick decision | Full-field, progression-based |
| Tempo | Fast, hurry-up | Controlled, run-clock oriented |

LaFleur's system — forged under Kyle Shanahan in San Francisco before he took it to the Jets — is built on one core idea: **the run-pass marriage.** Wide zone stretches the defense horizontally. Play-action off that same look forces linebackers to freeze. Boot action rolls the QB to the boundary with a high-low read. The passing concepts are layered: a deep crosser, a flat route, and a checkdown, all flowing off the same action.

With the Jets (2021–22), LaFleur ran 11 personnel ~61% of the time but showed a clear desire to incorporate more 12 personnel (2 TEs). His brother Matt in Green Bay runs 21 personnel at 32%. Kyle Shanahan's 49ers practically live in 21/22 groupings with a fullback. Expect Arizona's 2026 offense to skew heavier than anything they've run since the Bruce Arians era — especially with Tyler Allgeier arriving as a true downhill power back.

**The key schematic signature:** pre-snap motion. LaFleur's Jets used jet motion, orbit motion, and TE shifts at elite rates. This isn't window dressing — motion diagnoses coverage pre-snap, creates blocking angles on wide zone, and generates free releases for receivers on play-action. In 2025, the Shanahan-tree offenses (GB, SF, LAR, MIA) ranked in the top third of the league in pre-snap motion rate. Arizona in 2025? Bottom ten.

That gap closes immediately under LaFleur.

### 2. Does Ty Simpson Map to LaFleur's Scheme?

**The short answer: He's practically a lab-designed specimen for this offense.**

Here's why — and I want to be specific because "scheme fit" gets thrown around like confetti in draft season.

LaFleur's offense asks the QB to do four things well:

1. **Execute play-action with conviction.** Sell the fake, keep your eyes downfield, deliver on time. Simpson's play-action accuracy at Alabama was elite — compact release, quick ball out, accurate on the move. This is his best trait.

2. **Win the short and intermediate game.** The Shanahan tree isn't about 50-yard bombs (though they exist). It's about 8-15 yard crossers, deep comebacks, and sail routes off boot action. Simpson's bread and butter: his accuracy between the hashes at 10-18 yards graded out among the best in this draft class per PFF.

3. **Process a full-field read.** Unlike the Kingsbury half-field quick-game, LaFleur asks QBs to read from the backside crosser through the flat and down to the checkdown. Simpson showed advanced processing at Alabama — pre-snap diagnosis, post-snap progression, and the patience to work through reads without panicking.

4. **Manage the pocket, not escape it.** LaFleur's offense doesn't need a 4.5 runner at QB. It needs someone who climbs the pocket, slides to the designed bootleg side, and delivers from a platform. This is where Simpson's poise shows — he keeps his eyes downfield under pressure and makes throws off-platform without abandoning structure.

**The one concern:** Simpson's 15 college starts are a razor-thin sample. His arm strength is "good, not elite" — he can make every NFL throw, but deep outs and 30+ yard verticals can come out short. Under interior pressure, his accuracy and decision-making degrade. These are correctable with development time — which is exactly what a Brissett bridge year provides.

**QB archetype comparison:**

| QB Trait | Simpson | LaFleur's Ideal |
|---|---|---|
| Play-action execution | ✅ Elite | ✅ Critical |
| Short/intermediate accuracy | ✅ Top of class | ✅ Bread and butter |
| Full-field processing | ✅ Advanced for experience | ✅ Required |
| Pocket management | ✅ Composed | ✅ Essential |
| Deep ball / arm talent | ⚠️ Good, not elite | ⚠️ Nice-to-have, not required |
| Rushing / scramble ability | ⚠️ Adequate, not dynamic | ⚠️ Bootleg mobility is enough |
| Starting experience | ❌ 15 starts | — |

**My verdict:** Simpson isn't just a fit — he's the archetype. The Shanahan tree has never needed a dynamic athlete at QB. It's needed a processor who can execute timing concepts with accuracy. Kirk Cousins ran this system in Washington and Minnesota. Brock Purdy — Mr. Irrelevant — became a Pro Bowler in it. Jared Goff went to a Super Bowl in it. None of them are elite athletes. They're all elite processors who throw with anticipation.

Simpson is that mold. He's Brock Purdy with more arm talent and less experience.

> **Where I disagree with the Draft panel:** I suspect the Draft agent will focus on Simpson's limited sample size as a red flag. That's fair from an evaluation standpoint. But from a *scheme fit* standpoint, 15 starts of exactly the right kind of tape is worth more than 40 starts of a QB running a spread offense that bears no resemblance to what LaFleur will install. The skills Simpson has shown are the exact skills this system rewards.

### 3. Marvin Harrison Jr.'s Year 3: The Wasted-Window Scenario

This is where my analysis gets uncomfortable for anyone selling optimism.

**The numbers tell the story.** Harrison Jr.'s first two years in Arizona:

| Season | Games | Receptions | Yards | TDs | Y/R | Primary QB |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2024 (Rookie) | 17 | 62 | 885 | 8 | 14.3 | Murray |
| 2025 (Year 2) | ~12 | 41 | 608 | 4 | 14.8 | Murray (5 G) / Brissett (rest) |

Harrison Jr. missed games to injury in 2025 and saw his counting stats crater — not because of his talent, but because of quarterback instability (Murray played 5 games before his release-triggering injury and Brissett took over for the remaining 12). His yards per reception stayed elite (14.8), meaning when he got the ball, he was producing. He just wasn't getting it enough.

**Year 3 is the inflection point.** This is historically where elite WRs either establish themselves as perennial All-Pros or get trapped in a quarterback purgatory that suppresses their development during the most valuable years of their rookie contract.

**Historical comps for elite WRs in Year 3 with mediocre/bad QB play:**

| Player | Year 3 Season | QB Situation | Stats | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DeAndre Hopkins | 2015 | Hoyer/Mallett/Yates/Weeden | 111 rec, 1,521 yds, 11 TD | Proved "QB-proof" — but he's the exception, not the rule |
| A.J. Green | 2013 | Andy Dalton (average) | 98 rec, 1,426 yds, 11 TD | Produced despite Dalton, but had consistency at QB |
| Andre Johnson | 2005 | David Carr | 63 rec, 688 yds, 2 TD | Talent suppressed; didn't break out until Schaub arrived |
| Larry Fitzgerald | 2006 | Matt Leinart (rookie) | 69 rec, 946 yds, 6 TD | Good, not great — Fitz didn't explode until Warner |

**The pattern is clear:** elite WRs can survive bad QB play, but they rarely *thrive* unless they're DeAndre Hopkins-level contested-catch dominators. Harrison Jr. is more of a route technician and separator — his game relies on timing, acceleration out of breaks, and QB anticipation. That skill set is *more* QB-dependent, not less.

**Scenario A: Bridge QB (Brissett/Minshew) for full 2026 season**

Brissett's 2025 line: 64.9% completion, 6.9 Y/A, 23 TD, 8 INT, 94.1 passer rating, 41.2 QBR. Those surface numbers look fine. Dig deeper and you find: 43 sacks, a bottom-third QBR, and an offense that ranked 23rd in scoring despite passing at high volume. Brissett doesn't push the ball downfield. He's a checkdown artist who protects the football. For Harrison Jr., that means:

- **Projected 2026 line with Brissett:** 70-80 receptions, 950-1,100 yards, 5-7 TDs
- **Translation:** Good stats on a bad team. Enough to keep the Pro Bowl conversation alive, but not enough to justify the #4 overall pick investment. Think Larry Fitzgerald's 2010-2012 purgatory — productive but unfulfilling.

**Scenario B: Simpson starts by midseason**

If Simpson wins the job by Week 6-8 and shows the accuracy and anticipation that made him a first-round pick, Harrison Jr.'s second half could look very different. Simpson's short-to-intermediate accuracy combined with LaFleur's play-action concepts would specifically target the areas where Harrison Jr. dominates — intermediate crossers, deep comebacks, and post routes off boot action.

- **Projected 2026 line if Simpson starts 10+ games:** 75-90 receptions, 1,100-1,300 yards, 8-10 TDs
- **Translation:** A breakout Year 3 that validates the pick and establishes the Simpson-to-Harrison connection that could define the franchise's next window.

**My position:** Every week Harrison Jr. spends catching 6-yard Brissett checkdowns is a week of his rookie-contract window wasted. LaFleur's scheme can create separation and generate open looks, but someone has to *throw the ball to the open receiver downfield.* Brissett won't do that consistently. The clock is ticking.

### 4. The Coaching Tree Template: How Shanahan-Tree Offenses Handle QB Transitions

This is LaFleur's biggest advantage — he has a playbook for this exact situation, and it's been battle-tested across three franchises.

**The Template:**

| Franchise | Coach | QB Transition | Method | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Green Bay | Matt LaFleur | Rodgers → Love | 3-year apprenticeship behind HOFer; simplified system Year 1 as starter; studied Purdy's SF tape to structure reads | Love made playoffs Year 1 as starter |
| LA Rams | Sean McVay | (Fisher-era) Goff → McVay system | Completely rebuilt scheme around Goff; heavy play-action, simplified reads, motion-based coverage diagnosis | Super Bowl appearance Year 2 |
| San Francisco | Kyle Shanahan | Jimmy G → Lance → Purdy | System designed to minimize QB error; scheme creates open receivers; QB doesn't carry offense | Purdy went from Mr. Irrelevant to NFC Championship |

**The common thread:** In every case, the coaching-tree offense didn't ask the young QB to be the engine. It asked him to be the *steering wheel.* The run game, play-action, and motion create the explosive plays. The QB just has to read, react, and deliver on time.

This is critical for Arizona's 2026 plan. LaFleur doesn't need Simpson to come in and throw for 4,500 yards. He needs him to:

1. Complete play-action boots at 68%+ 
2. Avoid turnovers (Brissett's strength that Simpson must match)
3. Hit the intermediate crosser when the scheme creates it
4. Let the run game and defense keep the team in games

Matt LaFleur literally studied Brock Purdy's first starts in San Francisco to build the Jordan Love transition plan. Mike LaFleur was *in the building* when Shanahan developed that system. He's not guessing. He's running the same playbook his mentor and his brother already proved works.

> **Where I disagree with the ARI panel:** The franchise-culture agent will likely frame this as a patience question — can the fan base stomach another developmental year? I'd push back: this isn't patience for patience's sake. There's an *active plan* with historical precedent. The Shanahan tree's QB development record is the best in modern football. LaFleur isn't asking Arizona to wait and hope. He's asking them to follow a proven template.

### 5. The Brissett Ceiling: Can LaFleur Scheme Around Mediocre QB Play?

**The honest answer: Yes, but only to a point. And that point is about 7 wins.**

Here's the math. LaFleur's Jets averaged 17.8 PPG with Zach Wilson and Joe Flacco — two very different QBs, both limited. Even with creative scheming, the Jets never cracked the top 20 in scoring. The scheme generated open looks (film confirms this), but the QBs couldn't convert them. 

Brissett is a better floor than Wilson but a similar ceiling. His 2025 profile:

- **94.1 passer rating** (looks good on paper — ~league average)
- **41.2 QBR** (the real story — 24th in the NFL, firmly below average)
- **6.9 Y/A** (modest; league average was ~7.2)
- **43 sacks** (he holds the ball, can't escape pressure, invites hits)

What LaFleur can do with Brissett:
- **Run the ball.** Tyler Allgeier gives Arizona a legitimate power back for the first time since David Johnson's prime. Isaac Seumalo upgrades the interior OL. LaFleur can lean into 21/12 personnel, pound outside zone, and control the clock. Arizona's 2025 rushing attack ranked 31st (93.1 YPG). Even getting to 20th (~110 YPG) changes the offensive identity.
- **Manufacture easy throws.** Boot action with a flat/crosser combo. Jet sweeps to Kendrick Bourne. Screen game to the backs. These are all "QB-friendly" concepts that don't require Brissett to read Cover 2 safeties and throw into tight windows.
- **Protect the football.** Brissett's 23:8 TD:INT ratio in 2025 shows he won't lose you games with turnovers. LaFleur can scheme conservatively and let the defense keep games close.

What LaFleur **cannot** do with Brissett:
- **Win in obvious passing situations.** Third-and-8+ is where Brissett's limitations become fatal. He won't push the ball past the sticks consistently, he can't extend plays with his legs, and his average depth of target shrinks under pressure.
- **Exploit Harrison Jr.'s full route tree.** Harrison's best work happens on intermediate and deep routes that require timing, anticipation, and ball placement. Brissett throws late, throws safe, and throws short. Harrison will see a lot of hitch routes and comeback routes instead of the posts and crossers that unlock his elite talent.
- **Score more than 20 PPG.** History is clear: Brissett-led offenses have never finished above 21 PPG for a full season. LaFleur's scheme can optimize the floor, but it cannot raise the ceiling with this caliber of quarterback.

**Realistic 2026 offensive projection with Brissett as full-year starter:**

| Category | Projection | NFL Rank |
|---|---|---|
| Points per game | 18-21 | 22nd–26th |
| Yards per game | 310-330 | 18th–22nd |
| Rush YPG | 105-115 | 18th–22nd |
| Pass YPG | 210-225 | 20th–26th |
| Team record | 5-7 wins | — |

That's the Brissett ceiling. Competent. Watchable. Not competitive for a playoff spot.

**The case for Simpson by midseason:** If Simpson shows in training camp and preseason that he can execute the play-action concepts at an NFL level — specifically the boot-action, crosser-flat reads that are the bread and butter of this system — LaFleur should have a short leash on Brissett. The transition template from his own coaching tree says: simplify the reads, lean on the run game, and let the young QB grow into the scheme.

Every week you delay that transition is a week of Harrison Jr.'s Year 3 window that you're not maximizing.

## Bottom-Line Verdicts

**On the scheme:** LaFleur is installing the most QB-friendly offensive system in football — the Shanahan wide-zone, play-action, motion-heavy attack. It's the photographic negative of the Kingsbury air-raid, and it's exactly what this roster needs.

**On Simpson's fit:** He's the archetype. Play-action accuracy, intermediate-game dominance, pocket poise, full-field processing. He's Brock Purdy with a better arm and less experience. The fit is A-tier.

**On Harrison Jr.'s Year 3:** With Brissett, you're looking at 1,000 yards and a wasted window. With Simpson (starting 10+ games), you're looking at 1,200+ yards and a franchise-defining connection. The gap between those outcomes is enormous.

**On the Brissett ceiling:** LaFleur can scheme around mediocre QB play — he did it for two years in New York. But "schemed-around" gets you 6 wins, not a playoff spot. This offense *needs* a capable passer to reach its potential.

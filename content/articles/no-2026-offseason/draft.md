# The Saints' $114M Bridge Year Is Real. The Problem Is They Still Don't Know Where It's Going.

*Our panel agrees New Orleans finally has a clean 2027 blueprint. It disagrees on whether 2026 is the first step toward contention or just a very expensive holding pattern around Tyler Shough.*

> **📋 TLDR**
> - The Saints are carrying roughly **$114M in 2026 dead money**, which makes this season less about shopping and more about surviving the bill for the last era
> - They also have the league's most interesting long-term setup: **Tyler Shough** on a cheap rookie deal and a projected **$115M+ cap reset in 2027**
> - The panel's verdict: the bridge is financially real, but it only matters if New Orleans treats 2026 as a development year and nails the decisions around **Chris Olave**, the **#8 pick**, and the roster's glaring defensive holes
> - The central debate: should the Saints use the last premium asset they have in 2026 to protect Shough with a wide receiver, or stabilize a defense that looks one injury away from collapse?

---

**By: The NFL Lab Expert Panel**  
*NO · Cap · Offense*

The New Orleans Saints are trying to sell fans on a phrase that usually sounds comforting: **bridge year**.

In most cities, that means patience. In New Orleans, it means something closer to controlled demolition. The Saints are dragging **$114M in dead money** into the 2026 season, the bill for a decade of restructures, void years, and one last round of pushing tomorrow's problems into tomorrow. Tomorrow is here now. Derek Carr is gone. Cameron Jordan may be gone. Demario Davis is gone. Ryan Ramczyk's money is still here even if his body isn't. The team is paying a fortune for players who are no longer fixing its problems.

And yet this isn't a normal reset. The Saints also have the one thing teams in cap jail almost never have: a cheap quarterback lottery ticket who showed just enough to make the next move matter. **Tyler Shough** went **5-3 as a starter**, completed **67.6%** of his passes, and gave Kellen Moore's offense a pulse after the season had already started to sink. If that eight-game sample was the beginning of something real, the Saints are one disciplined year away from turning the cleanest 2027 cap sheet in football into an actual build. If it wasn't, then "bridge year" is just polite language for wandering through the wilderness with a spreadsheet.

That's why this Saints offseason is so much more interesting than a normal 6-11 roster cleanup. The dead money story and the Shough story are the same story now. New Orleans doesn't just need to survive 2026. It needs to make sure the pain actually leads somewhere.

## The Saints' Roster Is Caught Between Two Timelines

The fastest way to understand New Orleans is to separate what belongs to 2026 from what belongs to 2027.

| Bucket | 2026 Reality | 2027 Implication |
|--------|--------------|------------------|
| Cap sheet | **$114M dead money**, minimal effective flexibility | Projected **$115-120M** in space once the purge clears |
| Quarterback | **Shough** is cheap, intriguing, still unproven | If he hits, Saints can build around a bargain starter |
| Premium asset | **#8 overall pick** is the last major addition before the season | Must become an immediate starter and long-term pillar |
| Offensive core | **Olave**, Shough, Edwards, Fuaga, Etienne, McCoy | Could become a real 2027 foundation quickly |
| Defensive depth | CB2 and EDGE look dangerously thin | 2027 money may have to rescue what 2026 can't |

That split is why the panel didn't treat this like a standard "what position should they draft?" story. Everything funnels back to one harder question: **are the Saints actually building toward something, or are they just making 2026 look respectable?**

The team-side view is the least charitable, and probably the most emotionally honest. New Orleans still has some real pieces — **Chris Olave** is a true WR1, **Erik McCoy** is still one of the better centers in football, **Taliese Fuaga** looks like a long-term answer up front, and **David Edwards** gives the offensive line a veteran investment that should matter beyond one season. But the same roster also has the unmistakable feel of a franchise trying to patch over multiple holes at once.

At receiver, the Saints have Olave and a lot of projection. At corner, they have **Kool-Aid McKinstry** and an immediate problem. At edge, they have more uncertainty than pass rush. The panel's Saints expert put it bluntly: this is not a "one more piece" roster. It's a roster that only starts to make sense if the quarterback turns out to be worth accelerating around.

> *"The roster construction only works if Shough is the guy. Everything else is noise. And right now, the evidence is 'maybe' at best."* — **NO**

That "maybe" is the whole season.

## The Dead Money Is Brutal. The Reset Is Also Real.

Cap's position is the cleanest argument in the room: the Saints are not making excuses when they call 2026 a bridge. The finances really do flip.

| Year | Projected Cap | Dead Money | Draft Pool | Projected Available Space |
|------|---------------|------------|------------|---------------------------|
| **2026** | $301.2M | **$114.0M** | ~$14M | **~$8-12M** |
| **2027** | $327.0M | **~$0M** | ~$12M | **~$115-120M** |
| **2028** | $350.0M | ~$0M | ~$12M | **~$125-135M** |

That swing is almost absurd. New Orleans goes from paying for ghosts in 2026 to potentially having top-three leaguewide flexibility a year later. The Eagles are the comp everyone reaches for because Philadelphia used a dead-money purge, a cheap young quarterback, and then one aggressive spending wave to turn cap pain into a Super Bowl push. The Saints can see that path. The temptation is obvious.

But Cap's biggest warning is also the most important one: Philadelphia knew **Jalen Hurts** was worth building around before it pushed in. New Orleans is still trying to answer that question about Shough.

That difference changes everything.

| Why the Bridge Could Work | Why It Could Collapse |
|---------------------------|-----------------------|
| 2027 dead money mostly disappears | Shough may top out as "competent" instead of foundational |
| Shough stays cheap through 2028 | A miss at #8 wastes the last premium bargain asset |
| Olave can be extended into the clean years | Defensive holes may be too deep to survive another stopgap season |
| Premium rookie contract at WR/CB/EDGE creates surplus value | Panic moves in 2026 could mortgage the clean 2027 sheet |

Cap's framing is worth stealing because it captures the whole article in one sentence: **dead money is paying for the past; 2027 is the chance to pay for the future**. The Saints' problem is that the future still needs a quarterback verdict before the spending spree means anything.

That makes 2026 less a chase season than an evaluation season with real stakes. New Orleans doesn't need a fake playoff push. It needs proof that its next aggressive move is aimed at the right foundation.

## Tyler Shough Is the Cheat Code — If He Is Actually Good

The offense panelist is much more bullish on the quarterback than the Saints expert is. That's where the article gets interesting.

Offense sees a quarterback whose strengths line up cleanly with **Kellen Moore's** system: timing throws, intermediate accuracy, enough mobility to extend plays, enough composure to operate in structure, and enough early production to justify betting on acceleration instead of mere patience. From that perspective, Shough's 2025 cameo wasn't empty survival. It was the start of a real Year 2 leap candidate.

NO sees a much shakier picture: eight starts, three ugly losses, a lot of optimism built on a very small sample, and not nearly enough evidence to talk about him like a solved problem. That skepticism matters because it keeps the Saints from confusing "promising" with "proven."

| Shough Case for Optimism | Shough Case for Skepticism |
|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| **5-3** as a starter in 2025 | Only **8 starts** is still a tiny sample |
| **67.6%** completion rate fits Moore's system | Three losses were blowout-level ugly |
| Cheap rookie cap hits through 2028 | No public "franchise QB" commitment from team or locker room |
| Year 2 in same offense usually matters | If he plateaus, 2027 becomes another QB search |

The subtle point from the Saints side is maybe the sharpest one in the entire discussion: the locker room itself seems to be in **wait-and-see mode**. Nobody around the team is talking about Shough the way teams talk when they know. Kellen Moore's language has been careful. Teammate praise has sounded professional more than evangelical. The front office has behaved like a team preserving optionality, not celebrating a solved position.

That doesn't mean Shough is doomed. It means New Orleans is asking him to answer the biggest question in the building while the rest of the roster is still wobbling around him.

> *"Year 2 can't be more of the same. Shough needs to make the leap from competent game manager on a rookie deal to legitimate franchise QB worth building around."* — **Offense**

That line is probably the cleanest description of the Saints' real assignment. Forget the division race for a second. Forget the wild-card math. The franchise question is whether Shough exits 2026 looking like a player you build a 2027 spending spree around, or just a reason you delayed the next quarterback search by a year.

## The #8 Pick Is Really a Test of What the Saints Think 2026 Is For

This is where the panel's fault lines show.

Offense is unequivocal: draft a wide receiver, ideally **Carnell Tate**, and stop pretending the supporting cast is good enough as currently built. If Shough is the bet, then the Saints have to make the bet correctly. That means giving him a real WR2 opposite Olave instead of another season of bracket coverage, forced patience, and "maybe this depth piece becomes more than that" optimism.

NO is more open to the defensive argument because the defensive holes are impossible to miss. Losing **Demario Davis** hurts. Losing **Alontae Taylor** leaves a real CB2 void. The edge room looks unstable. There's a version of this conversation where New Orleans drafts defense because the roster on that side is simply bleeding too much to ignore.

Cap, unsurprisingly, cares less about the position than the hit. A premium-position starter at #8 — WR, CB, EDGE, tackle — creates the same basic rookie-contract magic if the player is good enough. That's the accountant's answer, and it's not wrong. It also feels slightly bloodless compared to the actual football problem in front of the Saints.

| Pick #8 Option | Best Argument For It | Main Risk |
|----------------|----------------------|-----------|
| **WR (Carnell Tate or best WR available)** | Speeds up Shough, gives Olave a partner, builds a 2027 offensive core immediately | Defense stays structurally fragile in 2026 |
| **CB** | Addresses the clearest secondary hole after Taylor's exit | Offense asks Shough to develop with one reliable wideout |
| **EDGE** | Helps a pass rush with no obvious centerpiece | Doesn't fix the team constraint that matters most: QB evaluation |

I think Offense wins this argument, mostly because the Saints do not have the luxury of wasting a quarterback evaluation season on a half-built passing game. The defense absolutely has real problems. It may still be a mess in 2026 even if New Orleans drafts corner or edge. One rookie probably doesn't save it. But one rookie receiver can absolutely change the geometry of the offense.

The key point from Offense is that **two legitimate separators** change everything for Moore. Right now the Saints can imagine Olave being the centerpiece. They cannot yet imagine a coverage plan that has to respect multiple answers. That's the difference between a functional offense and one that actually tells you something about its quarterback.

## The Olave Decision Tells You Whether the Front Office Actually Believes the Story

The easiest move to underrate in this entire offseason is the **Chris Olave extension**, because it sounds like a normal good-player-gets-paid story. It isn't. It's the Saints' cleanest chance to prove they understand their own timeline.

Cap's case for acting now is straightforward: Olave is 24, productive, clearly the best receiver on the roster, and only going to get more expensive if the market keeps moving. Waiting until 2027 because the cap sheet looks prettier then might feel cleaner, but it also gives the player more leverage and makes the price more painful.

The trick is structure.

| Olave Decision | 2026 Impact | 2027+ Impact |
|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Extend now, backload intelligently** | Keeps 2026 cap hit manageable | Locks in WR1 before market rises and preserves 2027 flexibility |
| Wait until 2027 | Avoids adding complexity now | Risks a more expensive deal and more tension if Shough falters |
| Saints-style void-year special | Lowest short-term pain | Recreates the same structural problem that caused this mess |

This is the place where the cap perspective feels least debatable. If New Orleans is serious about the bridge, it should not let its one obvious offensive cornerstone drift into a later, more expensive negotiation. Sign Olave now, keep the first two cap years light, and make the heavy years arrive when the books are finally clean.

The more interesting part is what that says about the organization. An Olave extension is a quiet declaration that the Saints believe the future offense is **Shough + Olave + the #8 pick + a re-centered offensive line**, not just "let's see how this goes." It's one of the few moves that would make the bridge feel like architecture instead of marketing.

> *"The Saints' best leverage is signing him now, before the market resets, using a backloaded structure that keeps 2026-2027 affordable."* — **Cap**

If they punt that decision, it will be hard not to read the whole year as hesitation.

## The Defense Is Why This Still Feels So Uncomfortable

The pro-bridge case gets much easier to buy on offense than it does on defense.

New Orleans can sketch a coherent offensive future. It is much harder to sketch a coherent 2026 defense without immediately stumbling over the depth chart. **McKinstry** is a building block. **Kaden Elliss** is a useful addition. Beyond that, too much of the group feels like duct tape.

| Defensive Issue | Why It Matters |
|-----------------|----------------|
| **CB2** | McKinstry can't erase an entire passing game by himself |
| **EDGE** | There's no clear answer if Cameron Jordan is done and no replacement emerges |
| **Linebacker downgrade** | Elliss can help, but Davis was the signal-caller and emotional center |
| **In-season flexibility** | Cap constraints make it hard to fix problems once the season starts |

This is where NO's skepticism does the article a favor. If you only listen to the financial upside, the Saints start sounding like a patient smart team taking its medicine. If you only listen to the offensive upside, they start sounding like one draft pick away from a real leap. The defense forces everyone back into reality.

Cap's modeling says the Saints won't have much room for midseason heroics. That means the defense New Orleans takes into September is basically the defense it will have. No easy veteran rescue at the deadline. No comfortable injury absorption. No "we'll fix that later" safety net. That's why the #8 decision feels so loaded. It's not just the biggest move left. It may be the last one that truly matters before the bridge year has to live on its own.

That's a lot of plates for one season.

## The Verdict: Treat 2026 as a Quarterback-Proving Year, Not a Fake Contender Year

The panel disagrees on tone more than substance.

NO is the warning voice: don't call this a bridge unless you can point to the destination. Cap is the architect: the 2027 runway is real, the books really do clear, and this pain is survivable if the Saints stay disciplined. Offense is the accelerator: if Shough is the bet, then stop soft-pedaling it and draft the receiver who gives the offense a chance to become something more than respectable.

Put those together and the recommendation gets pretty clear.

| Panel Verdict | Why |
|---------------|-----|
| **Extend Olave now** | He is the cleanest offensive cornerstone on the roster |
| **Draft offense first, ideally WR at #8** | 2026 has to tell the Saints what Shough actually is |
| **Accept defensive pain in 2026** | One rookie won't fully solve CB2/EDGE anyway |
| **Do not mortgage 2027 to chase 2026 respectability** | The whole point of the bridge is preserving the payoff year |
| **Judge the season by Shough's growth, not the win total alone** | 7-10 with a real quarterback answer is more valuable than empty 8-9 ambiguity |

This is the part where a lot of teams talk themselves into noble-sounding middle ground. The Saints shouldn't. They are not one veteran signing away. They are not a sneaky contender if everything just breaks right. They are a team sitting on one of the league's strangest and most valuable opportunities: **a cheap quarterback window delayed by one year of financial wreckage**.

That opportunity only pays off if the franchise is honest about what 2026 is for.

If Shough looks like a top-18 quarterback with real upward velocity, if Olave is extended, if the #8 pick becomes a real starter, and if the offensive structure looks ready to support a spending wave in 2027, then the bridge year worked even if New Orleans finishes 7-10. In that version, the Saints enter 2027 with money, clarity, and an actual plan.

If Shough looks merely okay, if the offense stays one-player-dependent, if the defensive holes turn Sundays into survival exercises, and if the front office starts making short-term panic moves to make the record look prettier, then the whole thing collapses into what NO fears most: an expensive year of pretending.

That's why the right Saints headline for 2026 is not "can they steal the division?" It's simpler and harsher than that:

**Can New Orleans get out of cap jail and answer the quarterback question in the same season?**

If the answer is yes, the Saints become one of the most fascinating 2027 teams in football.

If the answer is no, then the bridge was real only on paper.

## 1. The 2026 Crisis: $114M of Nothing

Let's name the monster first. **$114M in dead money.** Not just the worst in the NFL for 2026 — the worst single-season dead cap figure in modern Saints history, and arguably one of the five worst cap sheets ever compiled in the salary cap era.

Here's what that $114M represents:

| Player | 2026 Dead Cap | Why |
|--------|--------------|-----|
| Derek Carr | $36.7M | Released after 2025 — two years of accelerated guarantees |
| Cameron Jordan | $18.8M | Likely retirement/release — void years accelerate |
| Demario Davis | $14.3M | Cut/restructure — aging LB with declining production |
| Taysom Hill | $13.7M | Released — gadget player at $12M+ cap hit unsustainable |
| Ryan Ramczyk | $12.0M | Medically retired (hip) — guaranteed money accelerates |
| Tyrann Mathieu | $7.2M | Cut — age 35, declining safety on expensive deal |
| **Others** | $11.3M | Assorted cuts, restructures, retirements |
| **TOTAL** | **$113.97M** | 37.8% of the salary cap doing nothing |

This isn't one bad contract. This is the bill for **a decade of cap manipulation finally coming due.** The Saints have led the NFL in cap voodoo since 2017 — void years, automatic restructures, converting roster bonuses to signing bonuses, pushing money into future years. It worked when Drew Brees was under center and the team was a playoff lock. It stopped working in 2022. The dead money is the receipt for choices made from 2019–2024.

Now here's the actual 2026 cap reality:

| Category | Amount | Running Total |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| 2026 salary cap | $301.2M | $301.2M |
| Dead money (Carr, Jordan, Davis, etc.) | −$114.0M | $187.2M |
| Current rostered players (Olave, Kamara, McCoy, etc.) | −$125M | $62.2M |
| 2026 FA signings (Edwards, Etienne, Elliss, Fant) | −$19.5M | $42.7M |
| Estimated draft pool (#8 + R2-R7) | −$14M | $28.7M |
| In-season reserve (practice squad, IR, transactions) | −$12M | $16.7M |
| Olave extension (placeholder, structure TBD) | −$8M | **$8.7M** |

⚠️ *This is the best-case scenario. OTC currently lists the Saints at $16.1M in raw cap space but $8.5M effective space. The numbers above assume the Saints complete additional cuts (Jordan, possibly one more veteran) and minimize Olave's 2026 cap hit through backloading. Bottom line: the Saints are operating with single-digit millions in discretionary spending.*

**What this means in practice:** New Orleans cannot make a significant free agent addition mid-season. They cannot absorb an injury to a high-priced starter without cutting someone else. They're in salary cap triage mode for the entire 2026 season. Every dollar is spoken for.

Here's the twist. The $114M in dead money is **a 2026-only event**. Almost all of it evaporates in 2027.

| **2026** | $301.2M | $114.0M | ~$145M | ~$14M | **~$8–12M** |
| **2027** | $327.0M | **~$0M** | ~$135M | ~$12M | **~$115–120M** |
| **2028** | $350.0M | ~$0M | ~$150M | ~$12M | **~$125–135M** |

⚠️ *2027/2028 projections assume 6–8% annual cap growth (consistent with NFL trends), natural contract expirations (Kamara likely off books by 2028), Olave extension averaging $30–32M annually but backloaded, and no major restructures or void year accelerations. Shough's cap hits remain minimal: $1.3M (2026), $1.8M (2027), $2.1M (2028).*

Let me be very clear about what this means: **The Saints go from the NFL's worst cap situation in 2026 to a top-3 cap position in 2027.** The swing is roughly **$110M in added flexibility** — more than the entire 2026 payroll of some NFL teams.

This is the entire argument for the "bridge year" narrative. The question is whether it's real.

New Orleans has the same weapon: **Tyler Shough on a $1.3M cap hit in 2026, $1.8M in 2027**. The Saints' advantage is even better than Philly's — Shough's deal is cheaper, and the 2027 cap windfall is **$50M+ larger** than what the Eagles had.

**The difference:** Philadelphia executed the purge *after drafting their QB*. They knew Hurts was the guy. New Orleans is running the purge *while discovering if Shough is the guy.* That's the risk.

## 3. The Olave Extension: The Single Most Important Cap Decision of 2026

Chris Olave is extension-eligible now. He's entering Year 4 of his rookie deal, coming off 72 catches for 989 yards in a chaotic 2025 season where the Saints started three different quarterbacks. He's a legitimate WR1, and he's about to get paid like one.

**The market comp tier:**

| WR | Year Signed | AAV | Guaranteed | Age at Signing |
|-----|-------------|-----|-----------|----------------|
| Justin Jefferson (MIN) | 2024 | $35.0M | $110M | 25 |
| CeeDee Lamb (DAL) | 2024 | $34.0M | $100M | 25 |
| A.J. Brown (PHI) | 2022 | $25.0M | $57M | 25 |
| Tyreek Hill (MIA) | 2022 | $30.0M | $72.2M | 28 |
| **Chris Olave (NO)** | 2026? | **$30–32M** | **$75–85M** | **24** |

Olave's age (24) and production (3,000+ yards in three seasons) put him squarely in the $30–32M range. He won't command Jefferson/Lamb money ($34M+) because his QB situation has been unstable and his yards-per-catch (13.3 career) is good but not elite. But he's younger than Hill was and more productive than early-career A.J. Brown.

**Here's the cap engineering problem:** The Saints need to sign Olave to a deal that:
1. Keeps his 2026 cap hit as low as possible (they have no room)
2. Remains affordable in 2027 when they're building the contender
3. Doesn't create another massive dead cap bomb in 2029–2031

This is where structure matters more than AAV.

### Option C: The Void Year Special (Saints' Historical MO)

| Year | Base Salary | Signing Bonus (prorated) | Roster Bonus | Cap Hit | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------------------|--------------|---------|-------|
| 2026 | $1.2M | $5.0M | $0 | **$6.2M** | Survivor year |

**This is the Saints' signature move.** Minimize 2026 ($6.2M), keep 2027 reasonable ($19M with a restructure), then add two void years (2030–2031) that push dead money into 2032. The back end is fake — Olave would likely be extended again or cut before the void years.

**The problem:** This is *exactly* how the Saints created the $114M dead cap mess in the first place. Jordan, Hill, Mathieu, Ramczyk — all structured with void years. Using void years on Olave is bringing a known explosive into a situation that's already on fire.

## 4. The Olave Timing Decision: Sign Now or Wait?

This is where the bridge year philosophy gets tested.

**Case for signing Olave NOW (spring 2026):**
1. **Lock in the price before it rises.** Every year Olave waits, the market resets. If a WR gets $36M this summer, Olave's comp tier moves up.
2. **Chemistry with Shough matters.** Olave and Shough only played 8 games together in 2025. Giving Olave security helps the offense gel.
3. **Avoid a holdout.** Olave entering 2026 on the final year of his rookie deal ($15.5M cap hit, fifth-year option) creates leverage for a holdout.

**Case for delaying until 2027:**
1. **The Saints have no cap room in 2026.** Any extension that adds meaningful 2026 cap dollars requires another cut.
2. **Shough is unproven.** What if Shough regresses and Olave demands a trade? Signing a $30M extension to a WR whose QB is a question mark is risky.
3. **The 2027 cap explosion gives leverage.** If the Saints wait until 2027 to extend Olave, they can offer a front-loaded deal (more cash upfront, less total commitment) when they have $115M to spend.

**My cap recommendation: Sign Olave in spring 2026 using Option A (backload heavy), but build in an out after Year 3 (2028).** 

The structure would look like this:
- 2026: $7.5M cap hit (affordable in bridge year)
- 2027: $14M cap hit (leaves $100M+ for other moves)
- 2028: $34M cap hit, but dead cap drops to $48M if cut (manageable escape hatch)

This gives the Saints cost certainty, keeps Olave happy, and preserves flexibility if the Shough experiment fails. The risk is that the back-end cap hits (2029–2031) are brutal — but by then, either Shough is extended and the Saints are contending, or they're rebuilding again and Olave's contract becomes tradeable salary ballast.

## 5. The #8 Pick's Cap Fit: Rookie Contracts in a Dead Money Year

The #8 overall pick will cost roughly:

| Year | Rookie Contract (est.) | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| 2026 | $6.8M | Signing bonus prorated + Year 1 base |
| 2027 | $7.9M | Year 2 salary escalator |
| 2028 | $9.1M | Year 3 salary escalator |
| 2029 | $10.3M | Year 4 salary escalator |
| **Total** | ~$34M over 4 years | 5th-year option decision due in 2029 |

⚠️ *Estimates based on 2025 rookie wage scale adjusted for projected 2026 cap growth. Actual figure depends on final slotting.*

**Here's what matters for 2026:** The #8 pick's cap hit ($6.8M) is locked in and non-negotiable. The Saints can't restructure it, can't backload it, can't void-year it. It's a fixed cost.

That's actually **good news** in a dead money year. Rookie contracts are the most cap-efficient assets in football. A #8 pick who becomes a starter gives the Saints four years of premium production at $8.5M per year — roughly 1/4 the cost of a veteran star.

**The cap argument for drafting WR (Carnell Tate) at #8:**

If the Saints take Tate, their 2026 WR room looks like:
- Olave: $15.5M (2026) or $7.5M if extended
- Tate: $6.8M (rookie)
- Polk: $1.2M
- Vele: $950K
- **Total: ~$16–24M for the entire WR corps**

For context, the Vikings paid **$35M just for Justin Jefferson** in 2024. The Dolphins paid **$30M for Tyreek Hill**. The Saints would have two legitimate starting WRs (Olave + Tate) for half the cost of a single elite WR1 in free agency.

**The cap argument for drafting CB or EDGE at #8:**

Same logic applies. A starting-caliber CB or EDGE on a rookie deal is worth **$18–22M in cap savings** compared to signing a veteran (e.g., Jalen Phillips got 4yr/$120M from Carolina = $30M AAV).

From a pure cap efficiency standpoint, **the position doesn't matter as much as the hit rate.** A #8 pick who starts for four years is worth roughly $40–50M in surplus value compared to acquiring that same caliber player in free agency. The cap wants the Saints to draft a premium-position starter — WR, CB, EDGE, OT — and let the rookie deal subsidize the 2027 roster build.

## 6. Cap Constraints on the 2026 Season: What the Saints CAN'T Do

Let's be realistic about what $8.5M in effective cap space actually means during the season.

**The Saints cannot:**

1. **Make a significant mid-season trade acquisition.** A veteran rental at the deadline (e.g., a CB or EDGE) would cost $3–5M in prorated salary. The Saints don't have it.

2. **Survive a major injury to a high-priced starter without a cut.** If Olave or Kamara or McCoy goes down, the replacement either comes from the practice squad or requires cutting a veteran to free up space.

3. **Sign a street free agent mid-season without a corresponding move.** Every transaction requires cap math. The $12M in-season reserve I modeled is mostly practice squad, IR replacements, and minimum-salary elevations.

4. **Extend another player beyond Olave.** The Saints have other pending decisions (e.g., Erik McCoy's contract, potential Kamara restructure), but there's no room to add more 2026 commitments.

**What this means strategically:** The 2026 roster is essentially locked after the draft. The Saints are playing with the hand they've dealt themselves. There's no cavalry coming. No mid-season reinforcements. No emergency adds. This is the roster, and it has to be good enough to survive the bridge year.

**This is why the #8 pick is so critical.** It's the last high-impact addition the Saints can make before the season starts. After that, they're in survival mode.

## 7. Where I Disagree With the Panel

**I expect NO to argue that 2026 is a legitimate competitive year** — that the Saints can go 8–9 or 9–8 and stay in the NFC South race. The cap says: **that's delusional unless everything breaks right.**

The Saints have $8.5M in effective cap space. Carolina just spent $120M on Jalen Phillips and added Devin Lloyd. Atlanta has $18M in space and a new GM/HC trying to make a statement. Tampa Bay is down, but they're not broke. New Orleans is the only team in the division operating in cap triage.

The bridge year only works if the Saints accept that 2026 is a **building year**, not a competing year. The goal isn't 10–7 and a wild card. The goal is 6–11 or 7–10 with visible offensive progress under Shough, plus hitting on the #8 pick and getting a full season of Edwards/Etienne/Banks development.

**I also expect Offense to push for WR at #8** to maximize Shough's development. I don't disagree on the logic — Shough needs weapons. But from a cap perspective, **I care more about the hit rate than the position.** A CB or EDGE who becomes a Pro Bowler on a rookie deal is worth just as much cap surplus as a WR. The Saints can't afford to whiff at #8, regardless of position.

**The Olave extension is where I'll push back hardest.** If NO or Offense argues for delaying the extension to 2027, I'll make the case that **waiting is too risky.** Olave's market comp tier only goes up. If a WR gets $36M this summer (e.g., Ja'Marr Chase, Garrett Wilson), Olave's agent will point to that number in 2027. The Saints' best leverage is signing him *now*, before the market resets, using a backloaded structure that keeps 2026–2027 affordable.

The one scenario where I'd support delaying: **if Shough regresses in 2026.** If Shough throws 18 INTs and gets benched, Olave's value to the Saints plummets. But that's a bet against your own QB — and if you don't believe in Shough, the entire bridge year premise collapses.

## 8. The Bridge Year Verdict: Architecture or Cope?

Here's the cap truth. The Saints' 2026 situation is **architecturally sound if — and only if — they execute the 2027 payoff.**

The $114M in dead money is the price of escaping bad contracts (Carr, Jordan, Hill, Mathieu) two years early. It's painful, but it's a *strategic* pain. The alternative was keeping those players on the books through 2027–2028 at declining performance and continued cap strain.

By eating the pain in 2026, the Saints buy themselves:
- **$115M in 2027 cap space** (top 3 in NFL)
- **Tyler Shough on a $1.8M cap hit** in 2027 (cheapest starting QB in football)
- **Chris Olave on a cost-controlled extension** (if structured correctly)
- **The #8 pick on a rookie deal** contributing Year 2 production
- **A young offensive core** (Shough, Olave, draft pick, Edwards, Fuaga, Banks)

That's the blueprint for the Eagles' 2021→2022 turnaround. Dead cap purge → rookie-deal QB → aggressive 2027 FA spending → contention.

**But it only works if the 2026 decisions are building blocks, not patches.**

If the Saints draft a WR at #8 and Shough develops, they're building an offense. If they draft EDGE and the defense improves, they're building a foundation. If they sign Olave to a smart extension, they're securing a cornerstone.

If they whiff on the #8 pick, Shough regresses, and Olave holds out, the "bridge year" becomes a euphemism for "wasted year," and the 2027 cap windfall becomes a rebuild fund, not a contention fund.

## The Bottom Line

**Core Recommendation:** The Saints should treat 2026 as a **one-year financial reset, not a competitive push.** Sign Olave to a backloaded extension NOW (before the market rises), draft the best available player at a premium position at #8 (WR/CB/EDGE), and accept a 6–11 or 7–10 season as the cost of building toward 2027.

**Hardest Cap Constraint:** The Saints have **no margin for error** in 2026. They cannot make mid-season additions, cannot absorb major injuries without cuts, and cannot afford to miss on the #8 pick. The roster is locked after the draft. Every decision has to stick.

**The 2027 Payoff Window:** If Shough proves he's the guy in 2026 (even on a losing team), the Saints enter 2027 with $115M in cap space, a franchise QB on a $1.8M cap hit, and the blueprint to become the NFC's version of the 2022 Eagles. That's the bet. The question is whether the front office has the discipline to execute it — or whether they panic mid-season and start mortgaging 2027 to salvage 2026.

**The Cap's One-Liner:** *"Dead money is just paying for the past — the Saints' 2027 cap sheet is paying for the future. The bridge year only collapses if they confuse the two."*

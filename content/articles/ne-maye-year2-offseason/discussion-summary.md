## Areas of Agreement

1. **Path C (Hybrid/Surgical) is the consensus.** All four panelists reject both extremes — no $70M spending spree, but no austerity either. The panel converges on targeted aggression: one premium move in FA plus smart use of the #4 pick.

2. **The offensive line is non-negotiable.** NE, Offense, and Draft all flag LT/RT as critical. Offense says LT is a "five-alarm fire" and the #4 pick should be a tackle. NE flags Moses' age at RT. Draft notes the OT talent cliff after the top 3 prospects.

3. **The AFC East window is real.** NE identifies Miami (Willis gamble), NYJ (no QB, rebuilding), and Buffalo (aging) as creating a 2-3 year opening. This isn't just about Maye — it's about divisional timing.

4. **The rookie-deal window is the central economic reality.** Cap frames it as "$40-45M in annual surplus value" — Maye at $10M vs. a future $45-55M extension. Every panelist acknowledges the clock is ticking on this financial advantage.

## Key Tensions

### Tension 1: WR1 Spending — Splashy FA or Draft/Mid-Tier?

- **Cap says spend:** Sign the WR1 ($28-32M AAV), backload the deal, keep Year 1 hit under $18M. The rookie surplus demands it.
- **Offense says protect first:** Add "one more proven boundary receiver" but don't mortgage the future. A QB with 3.5 seconds makes a WR3 look like a WR1.
- **Draft says FA can be mid-tier:** Sign a reliable WR (even WR2-tier), then use #4 on the best player available. Don't overpay when the draft has options.
- **NE says monitor the trade market:** Draft a weapon at pick 31, then watch for a disgruntled WR1 mid-season — "that's where real value appears for contenders."

**The gap:** Cap wants a premium WR1 signing now ($28-32M). Everyone else thinks you can get WR production without a top-of-market deal. This is the article's central spending debate.

### Tension 2: Pick #4 — OT, EDGE, WR, or Trade Down?

- **Offense is emphatic:** LT at #4 (Mauigoa). Protection unlocks everything — play-action, bootlegs, development.
- **Draft offers optionality:** Bailey (EDGE) or Mauigoa (OT) depending on FA results, with a trade-down as viable given EDGE class depth.
- **NE is silent on #4** — they hold pick 31, not #4 (⚠️ potential discrepancy to verify — NE references pick 31, not #4. The issue specifies #4 overall.)
- **Cap defers** on draft strategy but notes draft picks fill needs cheaply.

**The gap:** Offense wants OT locked in at #4. Draft says the pick should respond to FA outcomes — if WR1 is signed, take Bailey; if not, Tate (WR). This conditional logic makes good article structure.

### Tension 3: Year 2 Timeline — Make-or-Break vs. Make-or-Learn?

- **NE and Offense downplay urgency:** "Year 2 isn't make-or-break. It's make-or-learn." The franchise tag exists. The 2027 cap is expanding.
- **Cap escalates urgency:** The rookie surplus is "a melting ice cube — spend it before it's water." Every wasted year under the cheap contract burns tens of millions in surplus value.

**The gap:** Is there actually a crisis? Cap says yes (economic). NE and Offense say no (developmental). This tension is the article's emotional engine.

## Recommended Path (Lead's Synthesis)

**Path C — Controlled Aggression with Clear Priorities:**

1. **Sign one impact WR in free agency** — Target the $20-28M AAV range (not necessarily the top of the market). A proven boundary receiver who gives Maye a reliable target. Not a splashy Lamb/Jefferson-tier deal, but not a Shaheed-tier WR2 either.

2. **Draft for the trenches at #4** — Mauigoa (OT) if the OC confirms zone-scheme fit; Bailey (EDGE) if OL is addressed in FA. The OT cliff makes tackle the higher-urgency pick.

3. **Preserve $10-15M in rollover** for the 2027 war chest. Cap's dead money cliff math is compelling — NE could have $85M+ next year. Don't blow it all now.

4. **Use picks 31+ for depth** — WR, TE, or edge depth depending on what #4 and FA solve.

**The synthesis call:** Cap is right that the rookie surplus demands spending, but Offense is right that protection matters more than a flashy WR name. Split the difference: spend meaningfully on one receiver, invest the premium draft pick in the offensive line, and trust the dead money cliff to fund a second wave in 2027 if needed.

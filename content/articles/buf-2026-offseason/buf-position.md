## What Beane Actually Did

The original discussion prompt framed this offseason as a cap crisis requiring agonizing triage. Beane answered it before the panel sat down. He restructured Allen (2026 hit now ~$44.2M), extended Knox on a reduced deal (~$8.55M, not the $9.7M cut everyone projected), re-signed McGovern (4yr), traded a 2026 R2 to Chicago for D.J. Moore plus a R5, signed Bradley Chubb (3yr/$43.5M), added C.J. Gardner-Johnson (1yr/$6M) and Sam Franklin Jr., and let the old defensive core walk. The cap deficit shrank from the projected ~$11M to roughly ~$5M Top-51 — tight but workable with normal roster-trimming.

This wasn't one of the four hypothetical paths. It was a fifth: **surgical retool with a scheme change as cover.**

**Milano is the only genuinely irreplaceable loss.** His 492 snaps at 73.1% came as the defensive signal-caller with sideline-to-sideline range. White played 75.6% of snaps but was post-prime after his second ACL. Taron Johnson had real slot value, but Beane extracted a draft pick from Las Vegas rather than losing him for nothing.

The front office read the room: the 2025 defense was McDermott's defense, and McDermott is gone.

## The Scheme Reset: Leonhard's 3-4

Jim Leonhard's **3-4 base** recasts the personnel puzzle entirely. Ed Oliver (LDE), Deone Walker (NT, 464 snaps in 2025 at age 22 — 6'7", 331 lbs), and Chubb (OLB/RDE) fit a three-down front better than they fit McDermott's 4-3. Rousseau (629 snaps, 67.4%) shifts to a stand-up SLB/EDGE role that maximizes his 6'7" frame and closing speed.

The risk is Leonhard — a first-time NFL DC installing a new system with a secondary that has **one proven outside corner** (Benford, 796 snaps, 95.7%) and a massive question mark at LCB (Dorian Strong, 22, five career starts). That's the genuine vulnerability, and it's why **CB at pick #26 is non-negotiable.**

EDGE has a higher solid+ rate (20.6% vs. 12.1%), but Chubb's arrival makes that a depth need, not a structural one.

## The Offensive Upgrade

The Moore trade is a buy-low. His 2025 in Chicago was dismal — 85 targets, 50 receptions, 682 yards, ranked #66 among WRs (nflverse) — but that's a Bears offense problem, not a Moore problem. Paired with Allen, Moore slots into a WR room of Shakir (635 off snaps, 59.8%), Coleman (576, 65.2%), and Palmer (393, 48.3%). He fills the contested-catch alpha role Buffalo lacked since Diggs left.

The offensive line stabilized: McGovern re-signed (1,037 snaps, 98.1% in 2025), Torrence (1,129, 99.7%) is locked in, Brown (846, 91.2%) holds RT. The vulnerability is Dawkins at 31 (957 snaps, 97.4%) with no obvious successor — OT depth remains a Day 2 draft target.

## The Window Verdict

Allen at 30 with +0.133 offensive EPA/play, a true WR1 addition, an elite TE tandem (Knox 653 snaps + Hawes 487 developing), and a retooled defensive front is a **legitimate conference-championship roster** — if the secondary holds.

Realistic 2026 range: **10-7 to 12-5.** Ceiling is a deep playoff run. Floor is a Wild Card exit if LCB becomes a weekly liability in AFC East passing matchups.

The division helps: Miami is in full teardown (lost Tua, Hill, Chubb), New England is building around Maye but 1-2 years away, and the Jets carry $91M in dead money. Buffalo should win this division — the question is what happens in January.

**The hardest tradeoff:** Beane bet that a scheme change plus Chubb's upside could replace four departing defenders at a fraction of the cost. But Chubb's knee history (ACL) and Strong's inexperience mean the margin for error on defense is razor-thin. If either breaks down, Allen is carrying a roster that can't stop anyone in the playoffs — and that's been the story before.

# Defense Position: Buffalo's Scheme Survives the Exodus — But Only If Milano Stays

**By:** Defense — Defensive Scheme Expert
**Article:** BUF 2026 Offseason — Buffalo's Last Best Window

## The Scheme Doesn't Collapse — It Degrades Unevenly

People are framing this as "lose four starters, defense craters." That's too simple. Jim Leonhard's attacking 3-4 was already a Year 1 install in 2025 — the scheme itself is young, and not all four departures hit equally hard. Let me rank them by scheme damage, not talent level:

1. **Matt Milano (catastrophic).** Leonhard's pattern-match zone coverage requires a linebacker who can execute coverage checks at the second level, read route combinations, and handle TEs/RBs in man-match. Milano is one of maybe five linebackers in the NFL who can do all three. Terrel Bernard is a high-motor thumper but cannot replicate Milano's coverage processing. Lose Milano and Leonhard's ~35% blitz rate becomes a liability — every time you send five, you're exposing Bernard or a replacement-level LB in space against Tyreek Hill, Garrett Wilson, or Hunter Henry.

2. **Joey Bosa (significant but replaceable).** Bosa gave Buffalo a plug-and-play 3-4 OLB from his Chargers experience. But Greg Rousseau is the foundational edge rusher — 6'7", elite length, ascending. The defense can survive with Rousseau as the primary edge threat if the second rusher can hold up on base downs and provide 5-6 sacks. That's a Day 2 EDGE profile, not a $17-20M free agent.

3. **A.J. Epenesa (moderate).** Epenesa played 5-tech end in base — a run-setting anchor, not a pass-rush weapon. His 6 sacks were scheme-generated (stunts with Ed Oliver). This is the easiest role to fill: a 275-280 lb defensive end who can hold the C-gap and set the edge. Round 3-4 pick or a $3-4M veteran.

4. **Tre'Davious White (manageable).** White at 31 was post-prime CB2. Christian Benford is the CB1 and ascending. The real question is who plays opposite him: Maxwell Hairston, Dorian Strong, or a Day 2 corner. Leonhard's press-bail scheme needs corners who can jam and transition to zone — that's a learnable technique. A second-round corner with 6'0"+ frame and 4.45 speed can be functional by midseason.

## Replacement Archetypes — What Buffalo Actually Needs

**EDGE (Bosa replacement):** 6'3"-6'5", 255-270 lb, stand-up OLB who can convert to hand-down DE in sub packages. Must have a speed-to-power rush plan — pure speed rushers get eaten alive in 3-4 OLB alignments because the wider set demands a counter move. Think: long arms, explosive first step, functional anchor against the run. This is a Round 2 pick (picks 55-64 range typically produce 5-7 sack EDGEs by Year 2) or a $6-8M veteran on a two-year deal.

**CB2 (White replacement):** 6'0"+, 190-200 lb, press-bail technician. Leonhard's system asks corners to jam at the line then open hips into zone drops with eyes on the QB. Pure man-press corners who stare at their receiver won't work. Route-anticipation instincts matter more than straight-line speed. Day 2 CB with experience in a zone-match system is the ideal profile.

**ILB (Milano replacement):** This is the position where there is no clean replacement archetype — because Milano's skill set barely exists. You need a 230-240 lb linebacker with safety-level coverage instincts, sideline-to-sideline range, and the processing speed to execute pattern-match drops. If this player existed on Day 2 of the draft, he'd be a first-rounder. The realistic answer: sign Milano on a team-friendly 2-year deal (his age-30 market should be $8-9M AAV, not $12M) and accept a slight decline.

## AFC East Survival Check

Can a retooled defense survive the division? Miami's speed (Hill, Waddle) punishes busted zone assignments — exactly the risk when you have new CBs learning Leonhard's press-bail technique. The Jets' contested-catch offense (Garrett Wilson, Allen Lazard in the red zone) targets the CB2 and linebacker coverage spots — precisely where Buffalo is weakest post-exodus. New England under Drake Maye is the one matchup where a developmental secondary gets time to learn.

The division schedule isn't forgiving. Buffalo faces six games against offenses designed to exploit the exact positions they're losing.

## Recommended Path

**Path 4 (Youth Movement Defense) with one critical modification: re-sign Milano.** Let Bosa, White, and Epenesa walk. Spend the ~$30M in saved cap on Milano (2-yr/$18M), McGovern (C, 3-yr/$30M), and one mid-tier WR. Use Pick 25-26 on a CB with press-bail traits. Use Round 2 on a stand-up EDGE. Use Round 3 on an interior defensive lineman who can rotate with Oliver.

The logic: Milano is the one defender whose loss changes the *scheme*, not just the depth chart. Without him, Leonhard's blitz rate drops or his coverage breaks. With him, the defense can be bottom-half in 2026 but schematically coherent — and trending up as the young pieces develop around a proven signal-caller.

## The Non-Obvious Insight

Here's what the other panelists will miss: **Leonhard's scheme transition actually makes this exodus less devastating than it would have been under McDermott's old 4-3.** The prior system needed four quality starters at every front-seven position because the low blitz rate (~20%) meant the four-man rush had to generate pressure alone — if any one rusher was replacement-level, the entire pass defense collapsed. Leonhard's 35% blitz rate and simulated-pressure packages generate pressure through *scheme*, not just talent. He can manufacture edge pressure with safety fire blitzes and A-gap mugs even if the second EDGE is a Day 2 rookie running a 70% snap count. The scheme is designed to make average pieces dangerous through disguise and aggression. That's why Milano — the coverage brain that makes the blitzes possible — matters more than any individual rusher.

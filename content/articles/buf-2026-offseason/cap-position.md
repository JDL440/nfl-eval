## Where Buffalo Stands Now: ~$12.5M Under the Cap

The "$11M over the cap" storyline is already history. Through a wave of restructures, pay cuts, and veteran releases, Beane cleared the overage *and* executed an aggressive roster reload. Buffalo's effective cap space sits at roughly **$12.5M under the $301.2M ceiling** (per Spotrac/SI, mid-March). That sounds like breathing room — until you account for a projected **~$9.6M rookie-class obligation** and in-season reserve needs. Real remaining flexibility: **~$3M.**

The question has shifted: it's no longer *can Beane get compliant?* It's *what did compliance cost in 2027 and beyond?*

The ~$12.5M remaining has to cover the draft class and in-season emergencies with essentially zero margin for another mid-tier signing.

## The 2027 Invoice: Where the Real Pressure Lives

Every restructure moves dollars forward. Here is the downstream cost of Beane's 2026 engineering:

| Player | 2026 Cap Hit | Projected 2027 Cap Hit | Dead Money (2027) |
|--------|------------:|----------------------:|------------------:|
| Josh Allen | ~$44.2M | ~$51–53M (est.) | ~$138M |
| Ed Oliver | ~$13.7M | ~$28.4M | ~$34M |
| Spencer Brown | restructured | elevated (est. +$2.6M/yr) | TBD |
| DJ Moore | ~$6.75M | ~$24.5M (pre-restructure base returns) | elevated via void year |

⚠️ *Oliver's 2027 figure ($28.4M for an interior DT) and Allen's escalating hit create a structural squeeze. The Bills may need another round of restructures or a difficult Oliver decision by spring 2027 — the void-year trick only defers; it doesn't erase.*

Combined with ~$46.2M in total dead money already on the 2026 books, Buffalo's cap sheet is leveraged. The current roster is functional *because* Beane borrowed from 2027–2030. If the 2026 cap was a crisis, the 2027 cap is a reckoning.

## Draft Capital: The Hidden Cap Constraint

Buffalo traded its **2026 2nd-round pick** to acquire DJ Moore. The remaining draft board:

| Round | Pick | Notes |
|------:|-----:|-------|
| 1st | #26 | Late first — only high-value selection |
| 3rd | #91 | First pick after a 60-selection gap |
| 4th | #126 | |
| 5th | #165 | From CHI (Moore trade) |
| 5th | #168 | Own |
| 6th | #182 | From LV (Taron Johnson trade) |
| 7th | #220 | |

Seven picks, but **no 2nd-rounder** and a 65-pick gap between #26 and #91. Why does this matter for the cap? Because cheap rookie contracts are the primary escape valve for teams in cap distress. Without a Day 2 pick, Buffalo has fewer shots at finding immediate-impact starters on cost-controlled deals.

nflverse draft-value data (since 2015) quantifies the damage:

| Round | DE Starter+ Hit Rate | CB Starter+ Hit Rate |
|------:|--------------------:|--------------------:|
| 1 | 29.4% | 39.4% |
| 2 | 13.3% | 20.0% |
| 3 | 14.3% | 7.9% |
| 4 | 23.3% | 2.9% |
| 5+ | 3–6% | 0–2% |

*(Starter+ = career AV ≥ 30; Source: nflverse draft_picks dataset, 2015–present)*

The gap between R1 and R3 is not gradual — it's a cliff, especially at CB. Buffalo's secondary lost Taron Johnson (released/traded), Dane Jackson (released), and Tre'Davious White (UFA). Replacing that depth through R3+ picks is a low-probability bet. The DJ Moore trade bought a proven WR1 but cost a 13.3% (DE) / 20.0% (CB) shot at a cheap defensive starter.

If Allen's offense had a top-10 defense beside it, the Bills might have drafted conservatively and built space. Instead, the +0.024 defensive EPA forced Beane's hand into buying now.

## The Cap Verdict: Compliant Today, Leveraged Tomorrow

Beane did not break even — he went all-in. The Bills are cap-compliant with a genuinely improved 2026 roster: Moore gives Allen a WR1, Chubb replaces edge production, McGovern anchors the interior, and Gardner-Johnson fills the safety void. That's a real win-now push.

But the financial structure is leveraged in ways that constrain 2027–2028:

- **~$3M** in true remaining 2026 flexibility after the draft class
- **~$46.2M** in 2026 dead money already absorbed
- **$173M** in Allen dead money (functionally untouchable through 2029)
- **$28.4M** Ed Oliver 2027 cap hit (a decision point that's already approaching)
- **No 2nd-round pick** to cheaply develop the next wave of starters

The 2026 Bills will field a better roster than the old cap-position draft suggested. The trade-off is that Beane has almost no cap maneuverability until 2028 at the earliest, and the 2027 cap math will require *another* wave of restructures or difficult cuts.

Whether that's a championship gambit or a Saints-style spiral depends on one thing Cap can measure but not predict: how many of these 2026 bets actually produce playoff wins.

## The $3M Line Item on an $11M Problem

Let me walk through the math, because the math is why this trade works — and the math is the *only* reason it works.

Kansas City enters 2026 roughly $11M over the cap after converting $54.45M of Mahomes' salary into signing bonus, dropping his 2026 hit from $78.2M to $34.65M. That restructure bought breathing room in 2027–2028 but created a present-day squeeze. The Chiefs still need to clear space through veteran restructures (Chris Jones, Jawaan Taylor) or outright releases. Adding Fields at a $3M cap number doesn't blow the budget — it's roughly the cost of a mid-level special teamer. But it also means every other roster move has to be surgically precise. There's no margin.

## Market Comps: $3M Is Below Floor

The 2026 backup QB market established a clear range. Capable veterans — Jacoby Brissett, Sam Darnold, Gardner Minshew (KC's own departed backup) — commanded $5M–$12M AAV. Minshew's Arizona deal reportedly came in around $7M annually. Brissett-tier options settle in the $5M–$6M range.

Fields at $3M isn't just cheap for a former first-round pick. It's cheaper than what most teams pay for a QB3 with starting experience. KC is getting starting-caliber insurance at practice-squad pricing because the Jets are subsidizing $7M of the tab. That subsidy is the entire story.

## The Jets' Side: $7M for a 2027 Sixth

New York is absorbing roughly $7M in dead cap — the remaining prorated signing bonus from the $15M bonus on Fields' original two-year, $40M deal. They're essentially paying $7M to get a 2027 sixth-round pick and clear the roster spot. That's an expensive exit for a player signed five months ago, and it signals a front office that evaluated Fields for one offseason and decided the sunk cost wasn't worth protecting. For their cap, it stings in 2026 but clears cleanly — no residual drag into 2027.

## The Creative Angle: Extend-and-Restructure

Here's where it gets interesting. If Fields starts four-to-six games and plays well enough to generate trade interest or extension conversations, KC has a unique leverage position. They could sign Fields to a two-year, $18M–$22M extension with a substantial signing bonus, converting most of the money into prorated bonus to keep the 2026 and 2027 cap hits manageable. Void years could push dead money into 2028–2029 when the projected salary cap ($280M+) makes it trivial to absorb.

More realistically, if Fields plays well, KC doesn't extend him — they let him walk or franchise-tag him in 2027 (projected QB tag: ~$38M), then flip him via tag-and-trade for a second- or third-round pick. That turns a $3M investment into meaningful draft capital.

## The Non-Obvious Insight

Everyone's focused on the $3M cap hit. Here's what they'll miss: **Kansas City's real savings are in opportunity cost.** If KC had signed a free-agent backup at market rate ($7M–$10M), they'd have needed an additional $4M–$7M in cap space — which means *another* veteran restructure or cut. Every restructure pushes guaranteed money into future years, compressing the window. By getting Fields at $3M, the Chiefs avoided a cascading series of cap moves that would have mortgaged 2027 flexibility. The Jets aren't just subsidizing Fields' salary. They're subsidizing Kansas City's entire cap architecture for the next two years.

The $3M price tag doesn't just make this move defensible. At this price, with this cap environment, it's indefensible *not* to make it.

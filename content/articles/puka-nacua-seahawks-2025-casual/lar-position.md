# LAR Position: What the Rams Kept Going Back To

Here's the thing about Sean McVay against divisional opponents: he doesn't just call plays, he keeps a journal. In Week 11, the Rams tested Seattle with Puka on simple stuff—quick slants, comebacks, possession routes. They won 21-19, but Puka only got 75 yards. McVay took notes.

Week 16 was the payoff. McVay started attacking Seattle's split-safety coverage with two specific concepts: motion to create confusion pre-snap, and deep crossers that forced their linebackers into impossible coverage decisions. Puka's not just fast—he's *huge* at 6'2" and runs routes like a slot guy. When Seattle tried to stick a linebacker on him or rotate a safety late, McVay already had Stafford pulling the trigger. Twelve catches, 225 yards, two touchdowns. The Seahawks won the game 38-37, but they couldn't stop what the Rams were doing.

Here's the part nobody talks about: Puka's target share against Seattle was 31%, but his *yard share* was 51%. That means half of the Rams' passing yards in those two games went to one guy. That's not just scheme—that's Stafford and McVay saying "we're riding with Puka until you prove you can stop it." Seattle never did.

The blueprint is on tape now. Division games are about adjustments, and the Rams just won the chess match—even in a loss.

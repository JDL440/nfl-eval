# SEA Position: What Happened from Seattle's Perspective

Look, I'm not going to sugarcoat it — Puka cooked us in Week 16. But here's the thing Seahawks fans need to understand: we didn't exactly *lose* that game defensively. Final score was 38-37 and we won. The defense made enough stops when it mattered.

What happened? The Rams found the one matchup they could live with all game long: Puka against our corner rotation in space. McVay kept running the same concepts — quick releases, rub routes, motion to create separation before the snap — and we just couldn't consistently win those one-on-ones. Week 11 we held him to 75 yards because we were able to get pressure on Stafford early. Week 16? He had all day, and Puka's too good when a receiver has a clean pocket and time for the route to develop.

Here's the non-obvious part: we *chose* this. Mike Macdonald's defense is built on limiting explosive plays and forcing methodical drives. We'll give you the underneath stuff if it means we're not getting beat deep. Puka feasted on exactly the areas we're willing to concede — short and intermediate targets where he could rack up YAC. That's why we still generated 47 sacks and 18 interceptions on the season.

Is it fixable? Depends what you mean by "fix." Could we bracket Puka every snap and take him away? Sure. But then someone else eats, and McVay's offense is designed for exactly that. The real question is whether we can get more consistent pressure so Stafford doesn't have clean pockets to feed him all game. That's a pass rush problem, not a coverage problem.

Bottom line: facing Puka twice a year isn't fun, but we're 1-1 against him and won the game that sent us to the playoffs. I'll live with that.

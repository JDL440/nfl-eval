## Areas of Agreement

Both panelists agree on the core facts and several key interpretations:

1. **Week 16 was the outlier, not Week 11.** Both sides acknowledge that Week 11 (75 yards) was a manageable performance and Week 16 (225 yards, 2 TD) was the explosion. The question is *why* it flipped.

2. **Seattle's defense was genuinely elite overall.** Neither side disputes 47 sacks, 18 INTs, and a strong season. This isn't a story about a bad defense — it's about how a good defense got exposed in one specific matchup.

3. **Puka is a problem you have to pick your poison on.** Both agree that bracketing Puka full-time just shifts the damage elsewhere. The Rams offense is designed to punish over-commitment to one receiver.

4. **Pressure on Stafford is the key variable.** SEA says it directly: pressure early = contained Puka (Week 11). LAR implies it: clean pockets let McVay's designed concepts develop.

## Key Tensions

These are the disagreements that power the article:

1. **Was it Puka or McVay?**
   - SEA frames this as "we gave up the underneath stuff on purpose" — implying any good receiver would have eaten there.
   - LAR frames this as McVay specifically diagnosing Seattle's weaknesses between Week 11 and Week 16, running motion and deep crossers to attack their structure.
   - **This is the article's central question:** Was the 300-yard split about a generational receiver doing what generational receivers do, or about a coaching staff finding a repeatable exploit?

2. **How worried should Seattle be?**
   - SEA's posture is calm: "We're 1-1 and won the game that mattered. I'll live with that."
   - LAR's posture is ominous: "The blueprint is on tape now. The Rams won the chess match — even in a loss."
   - The article needs to navigate this tension without fully siding with either framing.

3. **Is this fixable?**
   - SEA says it's a pass-rush issue (get to Stafford faster), not a coverage scheme problem.
   - LAR implies the structural issue is deeper — McVay's motion concepts create pre-snap advantages that pressure alone can't solve.

## Recommended Path

**Path 2 (The Rams Found Seattle's Weak Spot) as the primary frame, softened by Path 1 (Puka Is Just That Guy).**

Here's the reasoning for a Casual Fan piece:

- Path 3 (dismiss it as one crazy game) is too deflationary. A casual fan clicked because 300 yards against a great defense *sounds* like a story, and telling them "nah, ignore it" doesn't pay off the headline.
- Path 2 gives the article a *narrative engine*: McVay studied the tape, adjusted between games, and unleashed a plan. That's a football story casual fans can follow — it has a beginning (Week 11), a middle (McVay's adjustments), and a climax (Week 16).
- Path 1 layers on top naturally: the scheme worked *because* Puka is elite. A lesser receiver doesn't turn McVay's adjustments into 225 yards and 2 TDs.

**The article should tell the two-game story as a chess match**, with Puka as the piece McVay kept deploying until Seattle couldn't answer. End with the open question: can Seattle solve it before they meet again?

## Open Questions for the Writer

1. **Can you tell this as a two-act story?** Act 1 is Week 11 (Seattle's pressure contains Puka), Act 2 is Week 16 (McVay adjusts, Puka explodes). This structure makes the adjustment the story, not just the stats.

2. **How do you explain "motion and crossers" without getting into X's-and-O's jargon?** The casual fan doesn't need route tree breakdowns. Think: "The Rams kept moving Puka around before the snap to confuse Seattle's defenders about who was covering him."

3. **What's the right emotional register for the ending?** SEA fans read this too. The piece shouldn't dunk on the Seahawks — it should frame this as a respect-the-opponent situation where the Rams found something clever and Puka was good enough to cash it in.

4. **Where does the 51% yard share stat land?** This is the single most accessible number in the piece. "Half of everything the Rams threw for against Seattle went to one guy." Make sure it hits.

5. **How much of the 1,715-yard season context do you need?** Enough for the casual fan to know Puka was the best receiver in football — but don't let the season stats overwhelm the Seattle-specific story.

# Defense Position: Scheme Fit and Defensive Projection
**Agent:** Defense (Defensive Scheme Expert)
**Article:** DAL 2026 Offseason — Parsons Trade Analysis

---

Parker's 3-4 multiple is executable with this roster — barely. The front holds up. Everything behind it is a liability he's being asked to scheme around.

## The Front Is Real, But It's Not Philadelphia

Parker's PHI defense ran a true two-gapping nose at 0-tech to anchor A-gap control, letting ILBs run free to the football. Quinnen Williams is a one-gapping penetrator — interior collapse is his entire value, not two-gap discipline. Clark is a more traditional nose who can anchor, but at 30 on a short deal, he's a transition piece. Ogbonnia has no track record in a two-gap scheme.

The Gary signing is what actually makes Parker's scheme viable. In Philly, edge OLBs set the edge and created containment while the interior ate blocks. Gary does exactly that — disciplined, versatile, rushes from 5-tech or 7-tech depending on the package. He's closer to a 3-4 OLB fit than Parsons ever was. But this front is better in pass rush sub-packages than in base, which is a problem when your LBs can't hold gap integrity without help ahead of them.

## What the Rookie Picks Must Do on Day 1

**Pick #12 (CB):** Parker's Cover 1 Robber and Cover 3 Sky demand assignment-sound technique over athleticism. The #12 pick needs to execute press-bail — show press at the line, bail to cushion at the snap to avoid vertical routes over the top. Thompson will play robber underneath, not deep help. The corner is alone on his man more than in almost any other scheme. No gambling for picks; technique discipline absorbs the early reps.

**Pick #20 (EDGE):** Must function as the SAM OLB in 4-man sub-package rushes from Day 1, not a stand-up 3-4 OLB. Parker runs 4-3 sub-packages heavily — this player will be rushing from a 5-tech on passing downs immediately. The job: contain the C-gap on run downs, win one-on-one in speed-to-power against NFL tackles. He doesn't need to be a scheme master in Week 1; he needs to stay clean in run fits while Gary operates as the primary edge threat opposite.

## The Non-Obvious Hole That Draft Picks Can't Fill

The structural problem in Parker's system is **ILB-to-safety communication on zone blitz disguise**. In Philadelphia, Jordan Davis created enough pre-snap confusion that ILBs only needed to execute one of two post-snap calls. Overshown and James must identify the same pre-snap looks, make coverage adjustments, and communicate them to Thompson and Hooker — in their first year in this system. Disguised pressures fail when a second-level player declares too early or the safety doesn't rotate into the correct shell after a blitz drop-off.

This is a quarterback's dream scenario. The breakdowns won't show up as obvious blown coverage — they'll be seam completions and crossing routes between 12 and 20 yards where assignment fault is ambiguous. Parker will simplify his call sheet early to compensate, which blunts the disguised pressure packages that make this scheme functional in the first place.

That's not something you draft out of. It's a reps problem — and it costs 3–4 games before it gets better.

**Scheme verdict:** Functional 22nd–25th range in 2026 if the rookie CB holds up and Gary stays healthy. Ceiling is 18th–20th with a faster-than-expected LB communication leap. Floor is 28th–30th if the CB gets isolated and Overshown/James can't execute the disguise calls by midseason. Parker understands this. He's installing for 2027.

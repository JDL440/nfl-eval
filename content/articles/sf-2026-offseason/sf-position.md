# SF Position: The 49ers' Two-Front Rebuild

**By:** SF — San Francisco 49ers Expert
**Discussion:** 49ers 2026 Offseason — Pass Rush Rebuild + WR Corps Overhaul

---

## This Isn't 2019. It Isn't 2023. It's Something We Haven't Seen From Shanahan.

The 2019 breakout was a ground-up construction project — Shanahan and Lynch inherited a 4-12 roster, traded for Dee Ford, drafted Nick Bosa at #2, and added Kwon Alexander. They turned nothing into a Super Bowl appearance in one offseason. The 2023 run was the *peak* — a fully stacked roster where every position group had depth, and Purdy's emergence on a rookie deal let them hoard talent everywhere else. Both of those builds had a coherent theory: add elite pieces to a foundation already in place.

This 2026 offseason is neither. It's a *two-front renovation* of a house that's still standing but leaking from the roof and the basement. Rebuilding the league-worst pass rush AND replacing your entire WR1/WR2 tandem in the same offseason isn't the kind of move a dynasty makes at full strength. It's what happens when the bill comes due all at once — Bosa's ACL, Huff's retirement, Aiyuk's voided contract, Jennings walking. Four core contributors gone from two different position groups in the span of three months. That's not team-building. That's triage.

## The Locker Room Lost Its Glue

Here's what the national media will miss: Jauan Jennings wasn't just a complementary receiver. He was the embodiment of the Shanahan-era 49ers identity — the undrafted, undersized grinder who played bigger than his body. Losing Aiyuk was contentious and expected. Losing Jennings rips out the blue-collar DNA of the receiving room. Mike Evans brings a championship pedigree from Tampa and a legitimate red zone presence Purdy has never had, but he's joining a locker room in transition, not one at full confidence. Pearsall is the cultural bridge now — a second-year player asked to anchor a room that turned over completely around him. That's a lot of weight for a guy with 14 career starts.

## The NFC West Picture Is Shifting

Seattle just beat us 41-6 in the Divisional Round. That number lives in the building whether anyone says it out loud or not. The Seahawks lost Mafe, Walker, and Bryant this offseason, but Macdonald's defense humiliated a Shanahan offense on national television. The Rams stabilized with McVay's extension and added Jaylen Watson to a secondary that already gave Purdy trouble. Arizona is rebuilding — Murray released, new coach — so they're not a 2026 threat. But the 49ers are no longer the clear division alpha. We're competing for a Wild Card or division title from a position of parity, not dominance.

## My Recommended Path: Path 2 — One Bosa Plus Draft Insurance

Sign Joey. The talent upside of both Bosas healthy is a top-three EDGE tandem in football, and the Bosa restructure was clearly designed for this reunion. But you cannot bet the entire defensive rebuild on two players with a combined 30+ missed games. Draft an EDGE at #27 — someone like a mid-first pass rusher who can rotate as a rookie and develop into insurance if either Bosa misses time. The 2025 season proved what happens when Nick goes down with no viable complement: 20 sacks, league-worst, season functionally over. You can't run that risk back with older, more expensive players and no safety net.

Evans at WR1 is fine for 2026 — not exciting, but functional. The real WR investment needs to happen in the 2027 draft when you have a clearer picture of Pearsall's ceiling.

## The Non-Obvious Insight: 2026 Is the Last Clean Cap Year

Everyone focuses on Purdy's deal as the structural advantage, but the real window is narrower than people think. The $36.3M in dead money — Hargrave ($16.2M), Jennings ($8M), Gross-Matos, Collins — clears almost entirely after 2026. But 2027 is projected $13.7M *over the cap* before any moves, with Bosa ballooning to $52M, Aiyuk's dead cap at $41.4M if moved, and Warner at $29.6M. This is the last year where the front office can make aggressive additions without triggering a full restructure cascade. If the Bosa reunion and Evans don't produce a deep playoff run in 2026, the Shanahan window doesn't gently close — it slams shut under the weight of its own cap commitments.

The dynasty isn't cracking yet. But the margin for error just went from "comfortable" to "nonexistent."

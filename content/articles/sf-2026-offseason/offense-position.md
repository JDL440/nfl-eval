# Offense Position: The 49ers' WR Corps Overhaul

**By:** Offense — Offensive Scheme Expert
**Focus:** Scheme fit, offensive identity, production projections

---

## Evans in Shanahan's System: Not a Perfect Fit, But a Different Weapon

Let's be direct: Mike Evans is not a Shanahan archetype receiver. Kyle's system prizes YAC monsters — guys who catch at 12 yards and turn it into 22. Deebo Samuel, Brandon Aiyuk, Jauan Jennings — all of them created value *after* the catch within a motion-heavy architecture that manufactures space pre-snap. Evans has never been that player. His career YAC rate sits well below what Shanahan's WR1s have historically produced.

But here's where it gets interesting. What Evans *does* bring is something Shanahan has never truly had in San Francisco: a catch-point winner who can go up and take the ball away from defenders in traffic. Aiyuk was a separation-and-schemed-open receiver. Evans is a contested-catch alpha who has posted 50/50 ball win rates that rank among the best in NFL history. That's a fundamentally different offensive tool, and it forces Shanahan to evolve his concepts — not abandon them.

The real adaptation challenge is motion compliance. Shanahan uses pre-snap motion on 70%+ of snaps. Evans ran from more static alignments in Tampa. At 33, learning to process assignments while in motion — jet sweeps, orbit motions, trade routes off shifts — is genuinely difficult. Expect Shanahan to deploy Evans more from set positions as the designated X-receiver while the remaining WRs and Kittle handle the motion workload. That's a schematic concession, but a manageable one.

## Red Zone Transformation: Purdy's Missing Dimension

This is where the Evans signing actually makes offensive sense. Purdy has *never* had a true above-the-rim red zone target. Inside the 20, field compression eliminates the YAC-after-schemed-catch advantage that Shanahan's offense relies on in the open field. The 49ers have historically leaned on Kittle and the run game in scoring situations — effective, but predictable.

Evans changes the math. He's a 6'5" back-shoulder and fade specialist who has scored 100+ career touchdowns. Put him on a back-shoulder fade at the pylon against a 5'11" corner, and Purdy suddenly has a throw he's never been able to make — not because he couldn't execute it, but because no one on the roster could win it. This alone could add 6–8 touchdowns to the scoring profile, and it opens up everything else. Defenses that bracket Evans in the red zone leave Kittle working against linebackers underneath.

## Purdy Year 4: A Step Sideways Before a Step Forward

I won't sugarcoat it — the timing is rough. Purdy built his Year 2–3 production on chemistry with Aiyuk and Jennings. Those timing relationships, the trust on scramble-drill freelance routes, the anticipation throws to spots only they knew — that's gone. Replacing an entire WR corps in a quarterback's development window typically costs 2–4 games of offensive rhythm while new route timing calibrates.

The saving grace is Kittle. He's the one constant in the passing game, and Purdy's connection with him is the strongest on the roster. Kittle becomes the de facto WR1 in terms of volume and trust through at least the first month. Evans will need time to earn those anticipation reps where Purdy throws before the break.

Long-term, this could actually be fine. Evans gives Purdy a dimension he's never had, and if the remaining WR room fills with young, scheme-fit receivers through the draft, Purdy in Year 4 could be more complete — just not immediately.

## Williams OL Succession: The Quiet Emergency

Trent Williams at 38 with a $38M+ cap hit is the single biggest structural risk to this offense — more than Evans' age, more than the WR depth. Williams is the reason Shanahan's outside zone scheme works at an elite level. His ability to reach-block on the front side of zone runs and anchor against power rushers in pass protection is irreplaceable in the literal sense. There is no free agent or mid-round pick who does what he does.

If Williams declines mid-season or retires, the left side of the line collapses, and with it, the entire run-game architecture. Purdy's time in the pocket shrinks. The play-action game loses its teeth because the run game stops threatening. This is a cascading failure that makes every other offensive investment — Evans, Kittle's usage, the whole motion system — less effective. Pick #27 *must* be in the conversation for an OT prospect. Succession planning here isn't optional; it's existential.

## My Recommended Path

**Path 1 — Full Bosa Reunion + Evans — but with a twist.** From a pure offensive standpoint, the offense is set enough to compete *if* they use pick #27 on offensive line succession rather than EDGE. The offense doesn't need another pass rusher. It needs insurance that the scheme survives beyond Trent Williams. Draft the best available OT at 27, develop him behind Williams for one year, and let the defensive side of the ball handle its own EDGE investment.

The Purdy window is real. The offense has enough weapons with Evans, Kittle, and a healthy McCaffrey/Mason backfield to contend — but only if the line holds.

## Non-Obvious Insight: The Jennings Loss Hurts More Than the Aiyuk Loss

Everyone is fixating on the Aiyuk departure, but Jauan Jennings was the chain-mover who kept drives alive on third-and-7. He was Purdy's security blanket on broken plays — the guy who found the soft spot in zone coverage when the first two reads were covered. Evans replaces Aiyuk's production profile (big-play, downfield). *Nobody on this roster replaces what Jennings did.* The 49ers need to find a slot/possession receiver in the draft or bargain free agency, because without that role filled, Purdy's third-down conversion rate is going to crater — and that kills drives before Evans ever gets his red zone opportunities.

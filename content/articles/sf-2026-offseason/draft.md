# The 49ers Lost Their Pass Rush, Their Receivers, and Their Margin for Error. They Have One Year to Get All of It Back.

*Our SF, cap, defense, and offense experts unanimously want the Bosa brothers reunion — but they can't agree on what to sacrifice at pick #27, and the cap math says there's no second chance.*

Twenty sacks. That's what the San Francisco 49ers got from their entire defensive front in 2025. League-worst. Not middle-of-the-pack. Not "underperforming relative to talent." Dead last, by the kind of margin that makes the stat look like a typo.

It wasn't a mystery. Nick Bosa tore his ACL in Week 2. Bryce Huff retired out of nowhere. And the pass rush that once defined Shanahan-era San Francisco — the unit that carried them to Super Bowls and NFC Championships — disappeared so completely that the secondary couldn't compensate, the offense couldn't stay off the field, and the season died before Halloween.

The fix, apparently, is a family reunion and a $60 million bet on a 33-year-old wide receiver.

That's the elevator pitch for SF's 2026 offseason. Restructure Nick Bosa's deal to clear $17.2M in cap space. Use it to sign his brother Joey, who had 5 sacks and a league-leading 5 forced fumbles in Buffalo last year. Replace Brandon Aiyuk and Jauan Jennings with Mike Evans, fresh from Tampa on a 3-year, $60.4M deal. And then — if the cap gods cooperate — figure out what to do with pick #27 before 2027 turns the entire salary structure into a bonfire.

We asked four experts whether this two-front rebuild can actually work in a single offseason. They agreed on more than they expected. They disagreed where it mattered most.

## Two-Front Triage: This Isn't 2019, and It Isn't 2023

Our **SF** expert sets the historical frame immediately: this offseason doesn't compare to either of the franchise's recent glory builds. The 2019 breakout was a ground-up project — inherited a 4-12 roster, traded for Dee Ford, drafted Nick Bosa at #2. The 2023 Super Bowl run was the *peak* — every position group had depth, and Purdy's rookie deal let them hoard talent everywhere.

This is something else entirely.

> *"This is a two-front renovation of a house that's still standing but leaking from the roof and the basement. Four core contributors gone from two different position groups in the span of three months. That's not team-building. That's triage."* — **SF**

The damage ledger is striking:

| Lost Player | Position Group | How It Happened | Replacement |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Nick Bosa | EDGE | ACL tear, Week 2 | Returning from rehab — 75-80% burst expected early |
| Bryce Huff | EDGE | Sudden retirement | Joey Bosa (projected) |
| Brandon Aiyuk | WR1 | Contract voided | Mike Evans (33, FA signing) |
| Jauan Jennings | WR2/Slot | Free agency (projected SEA) | Nobody — this is the problem |

That's simultaneous reconstruction of the two position groups that most directly affect whether Brock Purdy can win games. The pass rush keeps the defense off the field. The receivers give Purdy targets. Without both, the 49ers are asking a fourth-year quarterback to carry a team that can't protect leads or sustain drives.

And the NFC West isn't standing still. Seattle just beat San Francisco 41-6 in the Divisional Round. That number lives in the building whether anyone says it out loud or not.

## The Financial Tightrope: Cap Space as a One-Year Asset

Our **Cap** expert doesn't bury the lede. San Francisco enters 2026 with roughly $33M in effective space, $36.2M in dead money, and a 2027 projection of **negative $13.7M**. Every dollar committed this offseason is borrowed against a reckoning that's already on the calendar.

The Bosa restructure tells the whole story. Nick's March 14 deal converted ~$17.2M of 2026 base salary into signing bonus, prorated across remaining years. His 2026 hit dropped from $41.6M to approximately $24.4M — the room universally understood as "Joey money." But that $17.2M didn't vanish. It shifted into 2027 and 2028 at roughly $5.7M per year. Add the restructure's deferred costs and you're looking at **negative $19M+ for 2027** before Joey even signs.

> *"Every dollar committed to Joey today is borrowed against a 2027 reckoning that's coming regardless."* — **Cap**

That makes 2026 the last year the front office can make aggressive additions without triggering a full restructure cascade. The math:

| Year | Cap Outlook | What's Happening |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| 2026 | ~$33M effective space | Last "clean" spending year; Purdy at ~$24.4M, dead money absorbs $36.2M |
| 2027 | Projected **-$13.7M to -$19M+** | Dead money clears (~$33M relief), but Bosa balloons to $52M, Warner at $29.6M |
| 2028 | Extension escalation | Purdy's deal climbs toward $50M+; every other investment gets squeezed |

But Cap's non-obvious insight reframes the doom narrative: the $36.2M in dead money (Hargrave $16.2M, Jennings $8M, Gross-Matos, Collins) almost entirely **falls off the books in 2027**. Without that dead money rolling off, SF would be negative $47M. The dead money isn't the problem — it's the *solution's timing*. SF needs every 2026 signing structured with minimal 2027 cap impact, because that relief is the only thing keeping the math theoretically possible.

And then there's the Evans deal — which Cap calls the smartest contract of the offseason. The headline number is $60.4M over three years. The actual base deal is $42.4M ($14.1M AAV) with **$0 guaranteed at signing**. The remaining ~$18M is incentive-based. San Francisco can walk away after any season with negligible dead cap. At $14.1M AAV base, Evans costs less than Alec Pierce ($28.5M AAV) and Rashid Shaheed ($17M AAV).

> *"For a team in cap crisis, this is exactly the kind of structure you need — high upside, minimal risk. The age-33 concern is real, but the money insulates SF completely."* — **Cap**

## The Bosa Brothers: Scheme Fit, ACL Math, and the Hidden Variable

Here's the part that most national coverage will miss, and it's our **Defense** expert's best contribution to this panel: the Saleh-to-Morris scheme change may actually *help* Nick Bosa's ACL recovery.

Saleh's wide-9 alignment demanded full-stride speed rushes — the hardest movement pattern for a recovering knee. Raheem Morris is installing a 3-4 odd front with roughly 34% blitz rate (more than double Saleh's system). Nick moves from a hand-in-dirt wide-9 alignment to a stand-up OLB role. Shorter rushes. Inside stunts. Redirection rather than sprinting. The new scheme may let Bosa be effective at 80% burst in ways the old system never could.

And Joey is the sneaky-perfect fit. He played stand-up OLB in the Chargers' 3-4 for years and ran a similar role in Buffalo. No adjustment period. Nick faces a double adjustment — new scheme plus ACL rehab. Joey faces neither.

The projection model:

| Scenario | Nick Bosa | Joey Bosa | Combined | Context |
|----------|-----------|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Ceiling** (both healthy 14+ games) | 10–12 sacks | 6–8 sacks | **16–20 sacks** | Matches the *entire team's* 2025 output from EDGE alone |
| **Realistic** (Nick ramp-up, Joey durable) | 8–10 sacks | 5–7 sacks | **13–17 sacks** | Joey carries primary load through October |
| **Floor** (one misses 5+ games) | 5–7 sacks | 3–5 sacks | **8–10 sacks** | Back near 2025 levels without depth insurance |

The secondary impact is massive. Twenty sacks in 2025 meant the secondary was exposed on every snap. Morris demands man-coverage corners on islands — a big ask unless the pass rush shortens routes. Elite EDGE duos push time-to-pressure under 2.5 seconds; at that speed, Cover 1 works because receivers can't separate. At 3.5 seconds — what 2025 gave — the same coverage gets torched.

> *"The Bosas make Lenoir and Green look like better corners than they are."* — **Defense**

But Defense isn't finished. The remaining holes are real: CB depth behind Lenoir and Green is thin for a 34% blitz-rate scheme, and the 3-4 nose tackle spot needs a legitimate two-gap anchor. Collins is a placeholder. If the nose gets pushed, edge rush lanes get muddied — and a real nose tackle would do more for the pass rush than people realize.

## Evans, Purdy, and the Offensive Identity Question

Our **Offense** expert is the most honest voice in the room about the Mike Evans fit. Evans is not a Shanahan archetype receiver. Shanahan's system prizes YAC monsters — guys who catch at 12 yards and turn it into 22. Deebo Samuel, Brandon Aiyuk, Jauan Jennings all created value *after* the catch within a motion-heavy scheme that manufactures space pre-snap. Evans has never been that player.

But what Evans brings is something Purdy has never had: a catch-point winner who goes up and takes the ball away in traffic. A contested-catch alpha who has posted 50/50 ball win rates among the best in NFL history. That's a fundamentally different weapon, and it forces Shanahan to evolve his concepts rather than abandon them.

The red zone math is where it clicks. Inside the 20, field compression eliminates the YAC-after-schemed-catch advantage Shanahan's offense relies on in open space. The 49ers have historically leaned on Kittle and the run game in scoring situations — effective, but predictable. Evans is a 6'5" back-shoulder and fade specialist with 100+ career touchdowns. Put him on a pylon fade against a 5'11" corner, and Purdy has a throw he's never been able to make. That alone could add 6-8 touchdowns to the scoring profile.

The schematic concession is real but manageable: expect Shanahan to deploy Evans from more static X-receiver alignments while Kittle and remaining WRs handle the motion workload. Shanahan uses pre-snap motion on 70%+ of snaps. At 33, learning to process assignments while in motion is genuinely difficult.

But Offense's sharpest insight isn't about Evans. It's about the player nobody in the national media is talking about.

> *"Everyone is fixating on the Aiyuk departure, but Jauan Jennings was the chain-mover who kept drives alive on third-and-7. Evans replaces Aiyuk's production profile. Nobody on this roster replaces what Jennings did."* — **Offense**

Both **SF** and **Offense** independently flagged this. Jennings was Purdy's security blanket on broken plays — the guy who found the soft spot in zone when the first two reads were covered. Without a slot/possession receiver filling that role, third-down conversion craters. And if drives stall before reaching the red zone, Evans never gets his scoring opportunities.

## The Pick #27 Fight: Where the Panel Actually Breaks

This is not a polite panel disagreement. It's a genuine resource-allocation collision between two emergency-level needs, and the experts split cleanly.

**Defense, SF, and Cap** want EDGE or IDL at #27. The logic is direct: Nick Bosa's ACL and Joey Bosa's durability history (30+ combined career missed games) demand a third pass rusher who can absorb 400+ snaps. The 2025 lesson was unambiguous — without a viable Bosa complement, the defense produces 20 sacks and the season is functionally over.

**Offense** insists the pick must be an OT. Trent Williams is 38 with a $38M+ cap hit. His ability to reach-block on outside zone runs and anchor against power rushers is irreplaceable in the literal sense. If Williams declines mid-season or retires, the left side collapses, the run game dies, play-action loses its teeth, and every other offensive investment — Evans, Kittle's usage, the whole motion system — becomes less effective.

> *"Williams succession isn't optional — it's existential."* — **Offense**

| Pick #27 Case | Who Wants It | Core Argument | Risk If Wrong |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| **EDGE/IDL** | Defense, SF, Cap | Bosa brothers have 30+ combined missed games; a third rusher is mandatory insurance | Williams (38) has no succession plan; mid-season decline tanks the entire offense |
| **OT** | Offense | Williams is irreplaceable; the scheme collapses without elite LT play | Nick's ACL + Joey's durability = real chance both miss time with no depth behind them |

The Lead's synthesis — and the panel's majority — leans EDGE at #27, with OT succession addressed at picks #91–#130 on Day 2/3. The reasoning: Williams is 38 but still elite, and one more year of his protection is survivable. The EDGE depth cannot wait, because 2025 proved what happens when it doesn't exist.

Cap adds the financial kicker: Joey's deal should be structured as a 2-year, $32–36M contract with Year 1 guarantees only, giving SF an out before the 2027 cliff. That's the responsible version of the reunion — the one that keeps the dream alive without making 2027 impossible.

## The Verdict: One Window, Two Gambles, Zero Margin

Here's what the panel agrees on, which is more than you'd expect from four experts examining a team rebuilding two position groups at once:

**Sign Joey Bosa. The Evans deal is already smart money. 2026 is the last real window. And the Jennings loss is the problem nobody is solving.**

The disagreement — EDGE vs. OT at #27 — is legitimate and unresolved. But even there, the panel converges on the underlying truth: San Francisco cannot afford to get the allocation wrong, because the cap math guarantees there is no follow-up spending spree. Whatever the 49ers build this spring is what they ride into January.

| Expert | Bottom Line | Risk They're Most Worried About |
|--------|------------|-------------------------------|
| **SF** | Two-front triage is survivable, but 2026 is the last clean year — the window slams shut, it doesn't gently close | NFC West parity + fan base confidence if both Bosas miss time |
| **Cap** | Evans deal is brilliant; Bosa reunion is affordable *if* structured as 2yr, Year 1 guarantees only | 2027 projected at -$19M+ — every dollar committed now is borrowed |
| **Defense** | Morris' 3-4 is the hidden variable — Joey is the better scheme fit, and the system may help Nick's ACL rehab | CB depth and nose tackle remain unfixed behind the Bosa headlines |
| **Offense** | Evans adds a dimension Purdy has never had, but Jennings' chain-mover role is the real hole | Williams succession at 38 — if the LT goes, the entire architecture collapses |

The Shanahan dynasty isn't cracking. But it's being stress-tested in a way it has never been, and the answer depends on whether two enormous gambles — Bosa health and Evans fit — both hit in the same 17-game window.

If they do, the 49ers have a top-3 pass rush, a red zone weapon Purdy has never had, and the last season of true roster flexibility before the cap reckoning arrives. If either bet misses, 2026 becomes the year the window closed — not because the franchise made the wrong moves, but because the margin for error in a two-front rebuild was always this thin.

The dynasty isn't dead. But for the first time in the Shanahan era, there's no room to be wrong.

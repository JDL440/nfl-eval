## Areas of Agreement

1. **Sign Joey Bosa — unanimously.** All four panelists want the reunion. The Bosa restructure was designed for this. The upside of both brothers healthy (16-20+ combined sacks) is too significant to pass on. The disagreement is about *how much* to hedge, not *whether* to do it.

2. **The Evans deal is smart money.** Cap's breakdown is definitive: $14.1M AAV base, $0 guaranteed at signing, essentially a one-year audition disguised as a three-year deal. At that price, the age-33 risk is fully insulated. The 49ers can walk away after 2026 with negligible dead cap. This is not a "sunk cost" signing — it's a low-risk bet on upside.

3. **2026 is the last real window.** SF, Cap, and Defense all converge on the same conclusion: the cap math turns catastrophic in 2027 (-$13.7M to -$19M+ depending on adjustments). The Purdy rookie-deal surplus is 2026 only before his extension escalates. Dead money clears after this year but gets replaced by escalating commitments. Every 2026 investment must be evaluated against this one-year reality.

4. **The Jennings loss is underrated.** Both SF and Offense independently flag that losing Jauan Jennings hurts more than losing Brandon Aiyuk. Evans replaces Aiyuk's production profile (downfield alpha). Nobody replaces what Jennings did (chain-mover, scramble-drill safety blanket, blue-collar identity). Third-down conversion is at risk.

## Key Tensions

### Tension 1: Pick #27 — EDGE/IDL Insurance vs. OT Succession
The panel's primary disagreement. Defense, SF, and Cap want EDGE or IDL insurance at #27 to hedge the Bosa brothers' combined 30+ career missed games. Offense insists the pick must be an OT to begin the Trent Williams succession — calling it "existential" because if Williams (38, $38M+) declines mid-season, the entire offensive architecture collapses. **The writer should frame this as a competing-emergencies problem: the defense can't afford another 20-sack season, but the offense can't afford to lose its LT without a plan.**

### Tension 2: Nick Bosa's ACL Timeline — When Is He Really Back?
Defense projects Nick at 75-80% burst in September, climbing to 90%+ by midseason. That means Joey Bosa must carry the primary rusher role through at least October. But Joey has never played a full 17-game season. **The writer should model the "realistic" scenario: Nick on a snap count through Week 6, Joey as primary rusher, with the depth piece (drafted or signed) absorbing 400+ snaps.** Defense offers a silver lining: Morris' 3-4 scheme may actually ease the ACL recovery by replacing pure speed rushes with positional versatility.

### Tension 3: Offensive Identity — Evolution or Erosion?
Offense is honest: Evans isn't a Shanahan archetype. He's a contested-catch alpha, not a YAC monster. Shanahan will need to deploy Evans from more static X alignments while Kittle and the remaining WRs handle the motion workload. That's a schematic concession. **The article's offensive question is whether Evans adds a new dimension (red zone alpha, 6-8 TDs) or forces Shanahan to compromise the system that made him.** Offense and SF both note that Kittle becomes the de facto WR1 in trust and volume for at least the first month while Evans timing calibrates.

### Tension 4: Dynasty Narrative — Cracking or Evolving?
SF frames this as "unprecedented two-front triage" — not the 2019 ground-up build or the 2023 peak roster. The 49ers lost four core contributors from two position groups in three months. But Cap shows the Evans deal is smartly structured, and Defense argues Morris' scheme may actually be a better fit for the Bosa reunion than the old system. **The writer should resist the "dynasty is over" narrative. The better frame: the Shanahan dynasty is being stress-tested in a way it never has been, and the answer depends on whether two risky bets (Bosa health, Evans fit) both hit.**

## Recommended Path — Lead's Synthesis

**Path 2: Sign Joey Bosa on a 2-year deal (Year 1 guarantees only), draft EDGE at #27, address OT succession in Day 2/3.**

Reasoning:

1. **Joey Bosa is the move — but the contract structure is everything.** Cap's recommendation is precise: 2yr/$32-36M, Year 1 guarantees only, giving SF an out before the 2027 cliff. This keeps the reunion alive without committing future capital the team can't afford.

2. **EDGE at #27 over OT.** This is close — Offense makes a compelling case for OT succession. But the 2025 lesson is unambiguous: without a viable Bosa complement, the defense produces 20 sacks (league-worst) and the season is effectively over. The Bosa brothers' combined injury history (30+ career missed games) demands a third rusher who can absorb 400+ snaps. Williams is 38 but still elite — the succession can wait one more offseason. The EDGE depth can't.

3. **Address Williams succession at picks #91-#130 (Day 2/3).** The 2026 OT class should have developmental tackles available in the mid-rounds. A Day 2 pick behind Williams for one year is the ideal timeline, per Cap's analysis.

4. **The Jennings replacement is the hidden must-do.** The chain-mover/slot role needs to be filled before the season. Target a veteran slot receiver in the $4-6M range or a Day 3 draft pick. Without this, third-down conversion craters and the offense never gets Evans his red zone opportunities.

5. **Morris' scheme change is the article's underrated angle.** Defense's insight that the new 3-4 system may actually ease Bosa's ACL return (replacing pure speed rushes with positional versatility) is the kind of non-obvious finding that makes the article worth reading. The writer should develop this fully.

## Follow the Money: The Free Agency Verdict Is Already In

Tennessee's 2026 free agency spending tells the story of organizational priority better than any press conference soundbite. Let's run the numbers:

**Defensive spending (new money):**
- John Franklin-Myers: 3yr/$63M ($21M AAV, $42M GTD) — DL, Saleh reunion
- Alontae Taylor: 3yr/$58M ($19.3M AAV, $42M GTD) — CB/Nickel, Tennessee native
- Cor'Dale Flott: 3yr/$45M ($15M AAV, $32M GTD) — Outside CB
- Jermaine Johnson II: Trade for T'Vondre Sweat + 5th-yr option ($13.4M) — EDGE
- Solomon Thomas: Trade for 7th-rd pick ($2.47M) — DT depth
- **Total defensive investment: $179M in new contracts, $116M guaranteed**

**Offensive spending (new money):**
- Wan'Dale Robinson: 4yr/$70M ($17.5M AAV, $38M GTD) — Slot WR, Daboll reunion
- Daniel Bellinger: 3yr/$24M ($8M AAV, $14M GTD) — Blocking TE, Daboll connection
- **Total offensive investment: $94M in new contracts, $52M guaranteed**

The ratio is **1.9-to-1 in favor of defense** on total contract value, **2.2-to-1 on guaranteed money**. And that's before you factor in what we *didn't* do: We didn't replace Chig Okonkwo (3yr/$30M to WAS) with a true pass-catching TE. We didn't add a WR1 to pair with Ward. We didn't upgrade the interior offensive line after releasing Cushenberry ($9M dead cap).

**The organizational tell:** Mike Borgonzi is building a Saleh defense, not a Daboll offense. This isn't speculation — it's cap allocation arithmetic.

## The Power Structure: Who Owns the #4 Pick?

Here's how the decision-making triangle actually works in Nashville:

**Mike Borgonzi (GM):** Final say on all personnel moves. Hired Jan 2025 after 17 years in Kansas City's front office (scouting director, assistant GM). Borgonzi's KC pedigree is defense-heavy evaluation — the Chiefs built around Spagnuolo's defense and asked Mahomes to operate efficiently, not carry a barren roster. Borgonzi's first major trade as Titans GM? Swapping T'Vondre Sweat (a promising young NT) for Jermaine Johnson II (a Saleh-scheme EDGE on a 5th-year option). That's a win-now defensive move, not a patient roster-building trade.

**Robert Saleh (Head Coach):** Defensive-minded HC with final say on scheme and game planning. Saleh's entire NFL reputation is built on the 2019–2020 49ers defense (ranked #2 and #5 DVOA). His Jets tenure (2021–2024) failed because Zach Wilson couldn't develop, but his defenses consistently ranked top-12 in pressure rate. Saleh didn't get fired for defensive failures — he got fired for offensive incompetence. The lesson he learned: **control what you can control**. And what Saleh can control is the defensive front seven.

**Brian Daboll (Offensive Coordinator):** Hired explicitly to develop Cam Ward, but he doesn't have GM authority. Daboll got two offensive free agents: Wan'Dale Robinson (a slot receiver he coached in New York) and Daniel Bellinger (a blocking TE, not a pass-catching weapon). Compare that to what Daboll had in New York when developing Daniel Jones: Saquon Barkley, Isaiah Hodgins, Darius Slayton, and a healthy offensive line. Ward's supporting cast is significantly worse, and Daboll has no power to fix it via free agency.

**Who drives the #4 pick decision?** Borgonzi makes the final call, but Saleh has his ear. The FA spending pattern proves it. If Daboll were winning internal debates, we'd have signed a proven WR1 (Stefon Diggs was available, Joey Galloway was available) or traded for George Pickens. Instead, we spent $179M on defense and told Daboll to "make it work" with Wan'Dale Robinson and Calvin Ridley's expiring contract.

**The tell:** Borgonzi's brother, Dave Borgonzi, is the linebackers coach. That's not nepotism — it's alignment. The GM and the defensive staff are family, literally. Daboll is the hired consultant brought in to fix the QB problem, but he's not in the inner circle.

## The Calvin Ridley Dilemma and What It Reveals

Calvin Ridley's $26.45M cap hit is the single largest on Tennessee's roster. He's 32 years old, coming off a down year (58 rec, 822 yards, 3 TD), and every credible cap analyst in the league has him as a post-June-1 cut candidate. Cutting Ridley saves $13.4M in 2026 cap space and clears $26.45M in 2027.

**Here's what hasn't happened:** Borgonzi hasn't cut Ridley yet. It's March 15. The post-June-1 designation window is open. The money is sitting there, ready to be reallocated. And we're not pulling the trigger.

**Why?** Because cutting Ridley before the draft would signal organizational intent. If we cut Ridley in March, the entire league knows we're drafting a WR at #4. Trade-down leverage evaporates. Draft rooms adjust their boards. We lose optionality.

But here's the deeper read: **Borgonzi is waiting to see who falls to #4.** If Mykel Williams or Ashton Jeanty is sitting there at #4, we take the EDGE and keep Ridley through 2026 (even at $26.5M). If Williams and Jeanty are gone and Carnell Tate is the BPA, we have a decision to make. And even then, the smart money says we trade down, take Tate at #7–11, and use the extra pick to address interior OL or safety depth.

**The Ridley decision is the offseason's Rosetta Stone.** When he gets cut (and he will), it won't be because Daboll won the internal debate. It'll be because the draft board forced Borgonzi's hand.

## The Division Context: We're Not Playing Houston's Game

Let's talk about what the AFC South looks like in 2026:

**Houston Texans:** C.J. Stroud, a loaded roster, $21.8M in cap space, and a playoff-tested coaching staff. They're the 1-seed favorite.

**Indianapolis Colts:** Daniel Jones (2yr/$88M extension), Alec Pierce (4yr/$114M WR1 contract), and Arden Key (2yr/$20M — our former pass rusher). They're swinging for a Wild Card.

**Jacksonville Jaguars:** Cap-strapped ($3.6M over), lost Travis Etienne and Devin Lloyd, and in rebuild mode. Not a threat.

**Tennessee Titans:** 3–14 in 2025, franchise QB on a rookie deal, and a new coaching staff installing new schemes on both sides of the ball.

**The gap between TEN and HOU is enormous.** Houston went to the playoffs. We got the #4 pick. We're not one EDGE rusher away from competing with them — we're 2–3 years away from being a legit division contender.

**So why is Borgonzi spending like a win-now team on defense?** Because Saleh's defensive vision *is* the 2–3 year plan. Build an elite defense that can keep games close (think 2019 49ers, 2021 Broncos, 2022 Jets). Give Ward a clean pocket by shortening games with time of possession. Ask the offense to be efficient, not explosive. And hope that by Year 3 (2027), Ward has developed enough that we can flip the resource allocation back toward offense.

**The philosophical bet:** Elite defenses can carry mediocre offenses further than elite offenses can carry mediocre defenses. That's the Saleh doctrine, and Borgonzi is buying in.

## The Uncomfortable Truth: Daboll's Mandate vs. Organizational Incentives

Brian Daboll was hired to develop Cam Ward. That's the explicit mandate. But developing a QB requires *weapons, protection, and time*. Ward was sacked 52 times in 2025. He had the 9th-longest time-to-throw in the NFL (2.91 seconds) because no one was getting open. His WR1 is 32 years old and declining. His best pass-catching TE left in free agency.

**What Daboll got in free agency:**
- Wan'Dale Robinson (slot WR, 5'8", not a perimeter threat)
- Daniel Bellinger (blocking TE, 23 receptions in 2025)
- Cordell Volson (backup guard)

**What Daboll didn't get:**
- A true WR1 to replace Ridley
- A starting-caliber center to replace Cushenberry
- A pass-catching TE to replace Okonkwo

**The organizational incentive structure is upside-down.** Saleh's job security is tied to defensive performance — if the defense is top-10, he's safe even if Ward struggles. Daboll's job security is tied to Ward's development — but he doesn't control the resources needed to develop Ward. If Ward stalls in Year 2 because the supporting cast is barren, does Daboll get blamed? Absolutely. Does he have the power to prevent it? No.

**The #4 pick is Daboll's only leverage point.** If Tennessee takes an EDGE at #4, Daboll is coaching Ward with Wan'Dale Robinson, Calvin Ridley, and Elic Ayomanor (a rookie) as his top-3 receivers. That's not a development environment — that's a survival test.

## The Recommendation: EDGE at #4, But the Trade-Down Path Is Smarter

If I'm in the draft room on April 23, here's my read of the organizational decision tree:

**Path 1: Mykel Williams (EDGE, Georgia) at #4**
- **Why it happens:** Borgonzi and Saleh get their premium pass rusher. Williams pairs with JFM and Jermaine Johnson to form a top-10 edge rotation. The defense becomes the team identity.
- **Why it's defensible:** Positional value, BPA alignment, scheme fit. Williams in a wide-9 with Gus Bradley's Cover-3 backend is a 10+ sack-per-year player by Year 2.
- **The cost:** Ward enters Year 2 with a bottom-5 WR room and no proven pass-catching TE. If he regresses (or just stalls), Daboll becomes the scapegoat by Week 12.

**Path 2: Trade down to #7–11, take Carnell Tate (WR) or Malaki Starks (S)**
- **Why it happens:** Borgonzi gets extra draft capital (R2 pick or future 1st), stays in the top-12, and can still land a premium talent. Tate at #7–9 is better value than Tate at #4. Starks at #11 fills a long-term need at safety (Amani Hooker is 30).
- **Why it's defensible:** More picks = more roster flexibility. Address EDGE in Round 2 (plenty of Day 2 talent) or via 2027 free agency when the cap space is even larger.
- **The cost:** You're banking on Tate being available at #7+. If he's not, you're stuck taking Starks (a need pick, not BPA) or reaching for a WR in Round 2.

**Path 3: Carnell Tate (WR, Ohio State) at #4**
- **Why it happens:** Borgonzi decides Ward's development is the #1 organizational priority, overrules Saleh, and takes the WR1 who can transform the offense.
- **Why it's unlikely:** This would require Daboll to win an internal power struggle he's currently losing 2-to-1 on FA spending. It would also require Tate to grade as a top-5 prospect on Tennessee's board, which most evaluators don't have him.
- **The cost:** You're passing on Williams/Jeanty (better pure talent) for a need pick. If Tate busts or Ward still struggles, the entire front office looks incompetent.

**My recommendation as TEN:** Trade down to #7–9, add a Round 2 pick, and take **Malaki Starks (S, Georgia)** if available or **Carnell Tate (WR)** if Starks is gone. Use the extra R2 capital to grab **Emeka Egbuka (WR, Ohio State)** or **Jalon Walker (EDGE, Georgia)**. This gives Borgonzi optionality, Saleh gets defensive talent either way, and Daboll gets a WR in the first two rounds.

But here's what I *think* will happen: **Mykel Williams at #4.** Borgonzi takes the EDGE, Saleh gets his cornerstone pass rusher, and Daboll is told to develop Ward with what he's got. Calvin Ridley gets cut on June 2 (saving $13.4M), that money goes toward a veteran WR3 (think Robert Woods-tier), and the offense is asked to be "good enough" while the defense carries the team.

**The hardest tradeoff:** Choosing EDGE at #4 means accepting that Cam Ward's Year 2 supporting cast will be bottom-10 in the NFL. If Ward's development stalls because he has no one to throw to, the organization will have chosen Saleh's vision over Daboll's mandate. And that choice will define the next 3–5 years of Titans football.

---

**Core recommendation:** Trade down to #7–11, take Malaki Starks or Carnell Tate, and use the extra capital to address WR/EDGE depth in Round 2. If no trade partner exists, take Mykel Williams at #4 and commit fully to the defense-first identity.

**Hardest organizational tradeoff:** The power structure favors Saleh and Borgonzi (defense-first) over Daboll (QB development). The #4 pick will reveal whether that imbalance is sustainable — or whether Ward's Year 2 struggles force a course correction the front office isn't ready to make.

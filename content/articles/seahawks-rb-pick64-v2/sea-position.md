## Bottom Line

**Pick #64 is too expensive for running back. Seattle should not use it on Jadarian Price — or any back — when the roster is bleeding at cornerback and edge rusher.**

The v1 article was written before Seattle lost Riq Woolen, Coby Bryant, and Boye Mafe in free agency. That changes everything. The Seahawks went from "RB is our most urgent hole" to "RB is our third-most urgent hole" in the span of one week. With only four total draft picks — the fewest in the NFL — every selection has to address a position where the fallback plan is worse than what exists at running back.

RB is still a real need. It is not a #64 need.

|--------|----------|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| Kenneth Walker III | RB | KC Chiefs | 3yr/$45M | 🟡 Charbonnet (ACL), Wilson, Holani |
| Boye Mafe | EDGE | CIN Bengals | 3yr/$60M | 🔴 No replacement signed |

| Coby Bryant | S/CB | CHI Bears | 3yr/$40M | 🔴 Thomas II signed but different role |

The v1 article treated Walker's departure as the defining offseason event. It isn't. **Seattle lost its CB2, its best young pass rusher, and its most versatile defensive back — all in the same week.** Walker's loss is real, but the defensive bleeding makes the RB hole look manageable by comparison.

### DeMarcus Lawrence: The Wild Card Nobody's Discussing

Here's the detail that makes EDGE even more urgent: DeMarcus Lawrence, who turns 34 this offseason, is openly considering retirement. Multiple outlets (mynorthwest.com, Pro Football Rumors) have reported he hasn't informed the team of his decision. If Lawrence walks away, Seattle's EDGE room is:

- **Uchenna Nwosu** — $20M cap hit, injury history, missed significant time
- **Derick Hall** — Led SEA edge rushers in PFF pass-rush grade in 2025, but has never been a full-time starter
- **Mike Morris, Rylie Mills** — developmental pieces, not starters
- **Nobody else**

That is a crisis. You cannot defend a Super Bowl title with one proven edge rusher and a prayer. **If Lawrence retires and Seattle didn't draft EDGE, there is no Plan B.**

### The CB Situation Is Worse Than the v1 Assumed

The v1 article barely mentioned cornerback because Josh Jobe's re-signing ($24M/3yr) looked like it stabilized CB2. It didn't. Here's the actual depth chart behind Witherspoon and Jobe:

- **Nehemiah Pritchett** — practice squad level, expanded role by default
- **Noah Igbinoghene** — former MIA first-rounder, has bounced across four teams
- **Shemar Jean-Charles** — practice squad re-sign

If Jobe or Witherspoon misses time, Seattle is starting a journeyman castoff opposite one of the best WR corps in the NFC West (SF added Mike Evans, LAR added Jaylen Watson at CB to unlock their receivers). A CB injury doesn't just hurt Seattle's defense — it hands division games to the Rams and 49ers.

## Why #64 on RB Is a Luxury Seattle Can't Afford

### The Four-Pick Constraint

This is the part the v1 article glossed over. Seattle doesn't have Rounds 4 or 5. Every pick is precious.

| Pick | Best Use (SEA View) | Why Not RB? |
|------|---------------------|-------------|
| #32 (R1) | CB (Cissé, Hood, Terrell) | RB in R1 is indefensible with CB depth this thin |
| #64 (R2) | EDGE (Zion Young, L.T. Overton) | This is where the EDGE class has starter-quality talent; RB has alternatives |
| #96 (R3) | RB *or* IOL (BPA) | **This** is where RB value lives — committee backs available here |
| ~#188 (R6) | IOL or depth BPA | Day 3 dart throw |

If Seattle spends #64 on Price, it means one of CB or EDGE goes unaddressed until Round 3 — and by #96, the starter-quality EDGE and CB prospects are gone. **You're choosing a running back committee piece over a potential starting pass rusher or cornerback. That's not roster building; that's fantasy football.**

### The RB Room Has a Floor — CB and EDGE Don't

Here's the honest comparison of fallback plans:

| Position | If Seattle Doesn't Draft in Top 2 Rounds | Floor |
|----------|------------------------------------------|-------|
| **RB** | Charbonnet (back by midseason), Wilson, Holani, McIntosh (ACL recovery) + $3-4M veteran FA signing | 🟡 Functional — committee can survive |
| **CB** | Witherspoon + Jobe + Igbinoghene + Pritchett. No FA market left. | 🔴 One injury from disaster |
| **EDGE** | Nwosu + Hall + Morris/Mills. Lawrence may retire. No FA market left at impact level. | 🔴 One injury from disaster |

The RB room has a floor because Charbonnet exists. He tore his ACL on January 17 in the playoff game and had surgery in February. Typical RB ACL recovery is 8-12 months. That puts him on track for a midseason return — aggressive but realistic if rehab goes well. GM John Schneider called the outlook "very positive" (mynorthwest.com).

That means Seattle needs a bridge for 6-8 games, not a long-term replacement. **You don't burn a Round 2 pick to cover a 6-8 game gap when your defense has permanent holes.**

## The Veteran Bridge Solves This Without Draft Capital

The v1 article recommended Brian Robinson Jr. ($3-4M) as the veteran bridge. That recommendation still holds — and it becomes *more* important when RB drops off the draft board.

Seattle has ~$40-44M in cap space. A veteran RB signing costs $3-5M. A Round 3 rookie costs nothing against the cap. Together, they cover the RB room until Charbonnet returns.

**The October depth chart without a Day 2 RB pick:**

```
RB1: Zach Charbonnet (back from ACL, midseason)  
RB2: Veteran FA signing (Robinson, or market equivalent)  
RB3: Emanuel Wilson (committee piece, Green Bay experience)  
RB4: George Holani / Kenny McIntosh  
R3 pick: Developmental zone runner (if BPA cooperates at #96)  
```

That's not a championship backfield on Day 1. But it's a functional one — and it lets Seattle spend its premium picks fixing the positions where no veteran bridge exists.

## Where I'll Likely Disagree With the Panel

### vs. Offense
Offense will argue that Fleury's wide-zone scheme *specifically needs* a one-cut explosive back, and that Price is a perfect scheme fit. I don't disagree on the fit. **I disagree on the price.** Fleury's scheme can function with Wilson/Holani in Weeks 1-8 while Charbonnet recovers. It cannot function without a pass rush or with journeyman corners against Kupp-level receivers in the NFC West. Scheme fit doesn't override roster triage.

### vs. CollegeScout
CollegeScout may argue Price is a genuine value at #64 — an Achilles discount on a 6.0 YPC All-American. Fine. But "value" at RB in Round 2 is less valuable than "adequate starter" at EDGE or CB in Round 2. The positional hierarchy matters more than the player grade. An 85th-percentile EDGE in Round 2 protects Darnold. An 85th-percentile RB in Round 2 gains you 200 more rushing yards than the next guy. The defensive pick has more win-probability impact.

### vs. Injury
I expect Injury to confirm that Charbonnet's midseason return is realistic but not guaranteed. That uncertainty is real. But the answer to "what if Charbonnet isn't ready by Week 8?" is a veteran contract, not a Round 2 pick. The downside of a late Charbonnet return is a slightly worse ground game for 4-6 extra weeks. The downside of skipping EDGE or CB is a structural defensive weakness for 17 games.

## Final Verdict

**RB at #64 was defensible in the v1 world. It is not defensible now.**

The Seahawks won the Super Bowl by being elite on defense. They lost Mafe, Woolen, and Bryant in one week. Lawrence might not come back. The defensive depth chart behind the starters is held together with practice squad duct tape.

Seattle has the cap space to solve RB through free agency. It does not have the cap space — or the remaining free agent market — to solve CB2 depth or a starting-caliber edge rusher without the draft. **The draft is the only tool that can fix the defense. Use it there.**

If Price falls to #96, take him and celebrate. If he doesn't, let someone else pay the Round 2 tax on a running back in 2026. Seattle's championship defense can't wait.

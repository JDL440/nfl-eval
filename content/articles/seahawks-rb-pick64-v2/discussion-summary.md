## Faithful Summaries

### SEA — "RB at #64 was defensible in v1. It is not defensible now."

SEA's position is unambiguous: the Seahawks' offseason losses at CB (Woolen, Bryant) and EDGE (Mafe, possible Lawrence retirement) turned the need hierarchy upside down since v1. With only four draft picks — the league's fewest — Seattle cannot afford to burn its second-rounder on a position that has a viable fallback plan (Charbonnet's eventual return + a $3-5M veteran bridge). CB and EDGE have no fallback: the depth behind Witherspoon/Jobe and Nwosu/Hall is practice-squad caliber. SEA recommends CB at #32, EDGE at #64, and RB only if value falls at #96. The Lawrence retirement wildcard makes the EDGE case even more urgent.

**Strongest line:** *"If DeMarcus Lawrence retires and Seattle spent #64 on a running back, there is no Plan B. That's not a draft strategy — it's a trust fall."*

### Injury — "Charbonnet is a coin flip for Week 1. Price's Achilles is a fading footnote."

Injury anchors the two medical pivots. Charbonnet's late-season ACL puts him at **~35-45% for Week 1**, with realistic full return in the Weeks 6-8 range. The 25% subsequent ACL re-injury rate means Seattle permanently needs depth it trusts. Price's 2022 Achilles, after four healthy years and 41 games, has migrated from real medical risk to market-perception discount — and even that discount is compressing as teams run their own physicals. Injury rates the RB medical urgency at 🟠 MODERATE-HIGH: not an emergency, but a coin-flip Week 1 is too volatile for a defending champion to ignore. Robinson Jr. is the clear veteran bridge pick (🟢 durable); Mostert carries too much age/injury risk (🟠-🔴).

**Strongest line:** *"Mahomes at 80% can still beat you from the pocket. Charbonnet at 80% is a running back who can't hit the hole. That's not a running back — that's a decoy."*

### CollegeScout — "Price is a mid-Round 2 talent, not a first-round steal. Recalibrate."

CollegeScout's sharpest contribution is correcting the v1 narrative. Price's consensus ADP has **risen** to ~#53-58 since v1 was written. He was never a late-first-round talent sliding on medical fear; he's a mid-second-round committee back with elite character and return value, but only 15 career catches, 280 career carries, and 3 fumbles in his final year. The *real* finding: the RB dropoff from #64 to #96 is gentle (Coleman, Johnson both available), while the EDGE/CB dropoff is a cliff. Emmett Johnson — 46 catches in 2025, strong zone vision — is a legitimate #96 alternative who better fits the receiving demands of Fleury's system.

**Strongest line:** *"The dropoff from Pick #64 to Pick #96 at running back is a gentle slope. At EDGE and corner, it's a cliff."*

### Offense — "The scheme wants Price. The scheme doesn't need Price at #64 — but the cost of skipping RB is higher than people think."

Offense is the lone panelist pulling toward #64. The argument is structural: Fleury's wide-zone system demands a three-back committee, and Seattle currently has one starter plus a gap runner (Wilson) who is schematically misaligned. Wilson as the primary zone back in Weeks 1-8 is, in Offense's words, "a schematic catastrophe" that suppresses the run game, erodes play-action credibility, and craters Darnold's efficiency. Price's Notre Dame zone experience translates immediately — no scheme adjustment period. A veteran bridge alone doesn't solve the zone-fit gap because Robinson Jr. is also a downhill runner, not a zone primary. Offense rates the pull at 7/10 but acknowledges the defensive needs are real.

**Strongest line:** *"Fleury's run game is a three-back buffet, and Seattle's cupboard has one plate and two plastic forks."*

## The Disagreement — Explicitly Surfaced

### Primary Fault Line: Need Severity vs. Scheme Survival

**SEA says:** The defense is on fire. CB and EDGE have no Plan B. RB has Charbonnet, Wilson, and the veteran market. You don't burn premium capital on the least-broken position.

**Offense says:** The offense's "Plan B" (Wilson as primary zone back) isn't really a plan — it's a structural mismatch that drags down the entire offensive machine. The defensive roster is worse, but the offensive cost of skipping RB is underrated.

**Resolution:** Both are right about their lane. The question is which catastrophe is worse: a compromised run game for 6-8 weeks, or a compromised secondary/pass rush for 17 games. SEA wins this argument because *duration of damage* favors addressing defense.

### Secondary Fault Line: v1 Value Thesis

**v1 claimed:** Price is a late-first-round talent falling to Round 2 on Achilles fear — a steal at #64.

**CollegeScout says:** That was promotional, not analytical. Price is a mid-R2 talent at fair market value. The Achilles discount has compressed. He may not even be there at #64.

**Injury confirms:** The medical discount is shrinking. By draft day it may be 5-10 picks, not 15-30.

**Resolution:** v2 must abandon the "steal" framing entirely. Price at #64 is paying retail for a good player, not buying low on a great one. The article is more honest — and more interesting — for it.

### Tertiary Fault Line: Are #96 RB Alternatives Real?

**CollegeScout says:** Coleman and Johnson are 70-80% of Price and available a round later. Johnson's 46 catches make him arguably a *better* Shanahan-system fit.

**Offense says:** Scheme-fit backs who can run NFL zone concepts from Day 1 are rare. The quality gap from #64 to #96 at RB is bigger than CollegeScout admits.

**Resolution:** This is a genuine 50/50 disagreement. The article should present it as such. Neither panelist has enough certainty to declare victory, and the reader benefits from seeing the tension.

## Lead's Final Call

**The article should take Path 2 with a lean toward Path 4 — a hybrid I'll call "the honest pivot."**

Here is the recommendation:

### What the v2 Article Should Argue

1. **The v1 thesis no longer holds as written.** RB at #64 was defensible before Seattle's defensive losses. It is not the optimal use of that pick now. Say this clearly.

2. **Price remains a quality scheme fit — the article should respect the player while redirecting the pick.** This is not a hit piece on Price. It's a roster-building argument that #64 is more impactful on defense.

3. **The recommended draft sequence is CB at #32, EDGE at #64, RB at #96 (or veteran bridge + #96 BPA).** This reflects the panel consensus among three of four agents and respects Offense's concern by still addressing RB — just one round later.

4. **The Charbonnet timeline is the article's emotional engine.** A coin-flip Week 1 for your RB1 creates real urgency that the article should feel, even as it argues that urgency is best addressed through the veteran market, not the draft.

5. **Price's Achilles discount is smaller than v1 claimed.** If the article mentions it, frame it as a "market perception that's fading" — not as a buying opportunity.

6. **If Price falls to #96, the calculus changes completely.** The article should acknowledge this explicitly. The argument is about the *price tag*, not the player.

### Why Not Full Path 4 (Deprioritize RB Entirely)?

Because Offense's structural argument is real. Wilson as the primary zone back *is* a schematic problem. The article is stronger if it takes that seriously rather than hand-waving it away. "Address RB, but not with your second-round pick" is more nuanced — and more defensible — than "don't worry about RB at all."

### Why Not Path 1 (Reaffirm Price at #64)?

Because three of four panelists — including the prospect expert — say the value math doesn't work. The defensive losses are too severe, the RB alternatives at #96 are too close in quality, and the "steal" narrative was oversold. Offense's 7/10 pull isn't strong enough to override a 3-agent consensus in the opposite direction.

## Writer Handoff

### Headline Direction

The headline should capture the *pivot* — v1 said one thing, v2 says another, and the reason is the Seahawks' defensive meltdown. Candidate angles:

- **"We Were Wrong About Pick #64. Seattle's Defense Changed Everything."**
- **"The Seahawks Still Need a Running Back. They Just Can't Afford One at #64."**
- **"Jadarian Price Is the Right Player at the Wrong Price."**

Lean into the tension between "the scheme wants him" and "the roster can't afford him." That is the article's core drama.

### Narrative Tension

The article's engine is a three-way pull:
1. **Offense pulls toward Price** — the scheme genuinely needs another zone back.
2. **SEA and CollegeScout pull away** — the defense is bleeding, the draft alternatives are close, and the "steal" narrative is dead.
3. **Injury sits in the middle** — the medical urgency is real but solvable without #64.

Frame the article as "the experts argued, the evidence shifted, and here's where we landed." The disagreement IS the content.

### What Not to Overclaim

- ❌ Do NOT call Price a "first-round talent falling to Round 2." CollegeScout debunked this. He's a mid-R2 prospect at fair value.
- ❌ Do NOT assume Charbonnet is ready Week 1. Injury says 35-45%. Treat his return as mid-season baseline.
- ❌ Do NOT dismiss Price as a bad pick. He's a strong scheme fit. The argument is about opportunity cost, not player quality.
- ❌ Do NOT present the veteran bridge (Robinson Jr.) as a complete solution. Offense rightly notes Robinson is a downhill runner, not a zone primary.
- ❌ Do NOT treat Lawrence's retirement as confirmed. It's a risk factor, not a fact.

### 3-5 Key Takeaways for the Article

1. **Seattle's defensive losses (Woolen, Bryant, Mafe) flipped the need hierarchy since v1.** RB went from Need #1 to Need #3. The article's core argument is that the world changed.

2. **Charbonnet's ACL makes him a coin flip for Week 1 (~35-45%).** That's real urgency — but it's 6-8 week urgency, not 17-game urgency. A veteran bridge (Robinson Jr., $3-5M) covers the gap.

3. **Price is a quality player whose "discount" has evaporated.** ADP has risen to ~#53-58. At #64, Seattle is paying retail. The v1 "steal" narrative is dead — the v2 story is about whether retail at RB beats retail at EDGE/CB.

4. **The RB dropoff from #64 to #96 is gentle; the EDGE/CB dropoff is a cliff.** This is CollegeScout's strongest finding and should be the article's analytical backbone. Coleman and Johnson are real alternatives at #96.

5. **Fleury's scheme needs a third back — but the defense needs a starter more.** The article's resolution honors both truths: address RB through the veteran market and #96, spend #64 where the positional scarcity is greatest.

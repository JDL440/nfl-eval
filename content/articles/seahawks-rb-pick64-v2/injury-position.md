| Scenario | Probability | What It Looks Like |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Full clearance, full snap count Week 1 | **~15-20%** | Aggressive rehab, no setbacks, team confidence. Rare for RBs. |

**Composite Week 1 availability estimate: ~35-45%**  
**Composite "back to form" estimate: Weeks 6-8 (mid-to-late October)**

### Why RBs Take Longer Than Other Positions

ACL return data across positions shows a clear hierarchy. Running backs consistently sit at the long end of the window because the position demands:

- **Lateral cutting at full speed** — the exact movement that stresses a reconstructed ACL
- **Absorbing direct contact** — defenders target lower bodies; a recovering knee takes hits
- **Explosive burst from a standstill** — the first thing that returns is straight-line speed; the last thing is that zero-to-sixty burst through traffic
- **Psychological trust** — RBs who don't trust the knee hesitate at the hole, which defeats the purpose

The medical literature is consistent: **ACL return for RBs averages 10.5-11.5 months when the goal is full performance, not just "cleared for practice."**

### The Patrick Mahomes Comparison v. the Charbonnet Reality

The v1 article was written when Mahomes's ACL (also late-season, Week 15) was the most visible parallel. Mahomes is now projected for Week 1-3 return. But QBs and RBs are not the same recovery:

| Factor | Mahomes (QB) | Charbonnet (RB) |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Movement demands | Pocket movement, occasional scramble | Constant cutting, contact absorption |
| Snap intensity | High but controlled | Maximum physical stress every carry |
| Return threshold | Can function at 80% and still play | Needs ~90%+ or he's a liability |
| Re-injury risk | Lower — protected by rules and pocket | Higher — 25% subsequent ACL rate for RBs with prior ACLR |

**Quotable:** *"Mahomes at 80% can still beat you from the pocket. Charbonnet at 80% is a running back who can't hit the hole. That's not a running back — that's a decoy."*

### The 25% Number

This matters for Seattle's planning: NFL players with a prior ACL reconstruction face a **~25% rate of subsequent ACL injury** (same or contralateral knee). That doesn't mean Charbonnet will re-tear — it means the base rate for his knee going forward is roughly 3x the general population. This is a permanent adjustment to his risk profile, not a temporary one.

For a team building around Charbonnet as RB1, this means **you always need depth you trust.** Not special-teams RB4 depth. Real "start if he goes down again" depth.

### The Real Question: Is v1 Overstating the Risk, or the Value?

The discussion prompt asks whether the v1 framing *overstates* the risk/value combination. My honest answer:

**v1 got the risk RIGHT but may have underemphasized how far the discount has faded.**

Here's the nuance. When I assessed Price originally, I estimated his Achilles history was suppressing his draft stock by 15-30 picks. That was a reasonable estimate in early March. But as we move through the draft process and teams complete their own medicals, the discount compresses:

| Draft Process Stage | Likely Achilles Discount |
|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Pre-Combine (Jan-Feb) | 20-30 picks — Achilles stigma at maximum |
| Post-Combine, pre-medicals (Mar) | 15-25 picks — Combine performance showed functional health |
| Post-team medicals (Apr) | 10-15 picks — teams with clean physicals stop discounting |
| Draft day | 5-15 picks — only teams WITHOUT their own medicals still discount |

**By draft day, the Achilles "discount" may be smaller than v1 suggested.** A 6.0 YPC All-American returner with clean medicals might not fall to #64 at all. That's not a medical concern — that's a board-position concern for CollegeScout to weigh.

### What I'm NOT Backing Off

The pre-draft physical remains **non-negotiable.** Clean MRI, bilateral calf measurement within 5%, plantarflexion strength at 85%+ of the uninjured side. If there's ANY red flag on the physical — thickening, atrophy, compensatory patterns — Price at #64 becomes an overpay and should drop to Round 3 or later.

**Quotable:** *"Price's Achilles is a 2022 story with a 2025 ending. Four years, 41 games, three kick-return touchdowns. At some point, the evidence outweighs the scar. We're past that point."*

## Section 3: Veteran Bridge — Health/Risk Lens

### The Case for a Bridge (From a Medical Perspective)

Even without the Charbonnet ACL, I would recommend Seattle carry a veteran bridge. The reason is structural:

1. **Charbonnet's 25% subsequent ACL risk** is a permanent adjustment, not a temporary recovery concern
2. **Price (if drafted) is a rookie** who will carry a full NFL workload for the first time in his career — load management matters in Year 1
3. **RB is the most injury-prone position per snap** (5.2% game-miss rate) — any backfield built on only two healthy bodies is one play away from crisis

### Brian Robinson Jr. — Medical Profile

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Age | 26 — squarely in the RB prime window (24-27) |
| Injury history | Survived a gunshot wound (2022, preseason). Returned and played a full career. No major soft-tissue or structural football injuries. |
| Durability | Started 43 of 48 possible regular-season games over 3 seasons (2022-2024). Excellent. |
| 2025 production | ~900 rushing yards, 4.2 YPC — steady, not explosive |
| Wear concern | Moderate-to-low. Physical runner but hasn't exceeded 230 carries in a season. |
| Risk tier | 🟢 LOW — one of the healthier veteran RB options on the market |

**Medical verdict on Robinson:** This is exactly the profile you want for a bridge — durable, available, no structural history, young enough that age-related decline isn't a factor. The gunshot wound is a non-football event with no football-relevant medical sequela at this point.

### Raheem Mostert — Medical Profile

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Age | 34 — well past the RB cliff (29-30), deep into decline territory |
| Injury history | Extensive: torn ACL (2020), multiple knee injuries, knee procedure (2024), various soft-tissue issues |
| Durability | Missed significant time in multiple seasons. Only 2 seasons above 200 carries in entire career. |
| 2025 production | Limited by age and accumulated wear |
| Wear concern | 🔴 HIGH — 34-year-old RB with ACL history and repeated knee procedures |
| Risk tier | 🟠 ELEVATED to 🔴 HIGH — age + injury history + position = maximum risk profile |

**Medical verdict on Mostert:** The scheme-fit argument is real (literal Shanahan-tree back), but the body is 34 years old with an ACL and multiple knee procedures. If Seattle needs Mostert to carry 12-15 touches per game for 6 weeks while Charbonnet recovers, the probability of Mostert himself getting hurt during that stretch is **non-trivial.** You'd be stacking injury risk on top of injury risk.

**Quotable:** *"Brian Robinson is the Tylenol — reliable, available, does the job. Mostert is the expired prescription with your name on it — might still work, but you shouldn't count on it when the stakes are high."*

## Section 4: The Medical Urgency Score

**How much medical urgency should Seattle assign to RB?**

| Factor | Urgency Contribution |
|--------|---------------------|
| Charbonnet Week 1 probability (~35-45%) | HIGH — coin flip availability for your RB1 |
| Charbonnet long-term ACL re-injury risk (25%) | MODERATE — permanent roster-planning factor |
| Wilson/Holani as fallback options | LOW insurance value — neither is a proven starter |
| Price Achilles risk (if medicals clean) | LOW — manageable, not a medical obstacle |
| Veteran bridge availability on current market | MODERATE — Robinson still unsigned, window closing |

### Composite Medical Urgency: 🟠 MODERATE-HIGH

Seattle doesn't face a medical *emergency* — Charbonnet will be back, and when healthy he's a legitimate RB1. But the gap between "he'll be back" and "he'll be back *Week 1 at full capacity*" is where the urgency lives. A defending champion cannot enter September with a 55-65% chance that its RB1 isn't ready and a prayer that Emanuel Wilson can handle a Shanahan-tree workload.

**The medical case alone doesn't demand #64 go to RB.** But it demands that Seattle address RB with *something meaningful* — whether that's #64 on a rookie, #96 on a different profile, or a veteran bridge, or some combination. The worst outcome is doing nothing and hoping the coin lands heads.

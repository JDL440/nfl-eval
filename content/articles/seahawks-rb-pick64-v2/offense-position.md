## What Fleury's Offense Actually Requires from the RB Room

Brian Fleury came from San Francisco's run game coordinator chair. He's installing what he told reporters himself: "fast, violent, aggressive" football built on the same wide zone foundation Klint Kubiak put in during the Super Bowl run. The bones don't change — outside zone is still the heartbeat, play-action still rides off the run game, motion and misdirection still generate free yards before contact. What Fleury adds is SF-specific layering: more condensed formations, heavier 12-personnel looks (his TE coaching background guarantees this), and a red zone philosophy that leans on the horizontal run game rather than throwing the ball.

Here's what that means for running backs in this system:

| Scheme Demand | Why It Matters |
|---------------|----------------|
| One-cut decisiveness in wide zone | The blocking creates a lane for one beat. Hesitation is death. |
| Burst through the hole, not around it | Speed to the edge matters less than acceleration through a crease |
| Receiving ability — not optional | Fleury asks backs to run routes, not just leak out of protection |
| Pass protection willingness | Non-negotiable. Sam Darnold's play-action timing depends on trusting the back's pickup |
| Committee tolerance | This is a 3-back rotation, not a bell-cow system. Ego kills roster fit. |
| Durability across 12-15 touches | Not 25-carry workloads — but consistent, efficient contributions every week |

Shanahan-tree offenses have *always* been multi-back systems. Mostert/Mitchell/Mason in SF. Jones/Dillon in Green Bay. Mattison/Cook in Minnesota. The 49ers have used a different lead back seemingly every 8-10 weeks for the last five years. **You do not need one elite running back. You need three competent ones who fit the scheme.**

That's the framework. Now let's test Seattle's current room against it.

## How Jadarian Price Fits the Scheme

I'm not going to repeat the v1 article's full scouting breakdown. CollegeScout owns that lane. What I own is scheme translation.

**Price's profile maps to this system at a 🟢 STRONG level.** Here's why:

Notre Dame ran a zone + gap hybrid under Mike Denbrock — outside zone, inside zone, counter, power, all from heavy TE formations with play-action integration. That is structurally the closest college approximation to what Fleury is installing. Price has been running one-cut zone concepts for three years. He reads combo blocks, makes decisive cuts, and gets north-south without dancing. His 6.0 career YPC held steady across three seasons against escalating competition (including playoff defenses). His 4.49 speed is right at the threshold Fleury needs — fast enough to threaten the edge on stretch, not so fast that he outruns his blocks.

Where Price needs development:

- **Receiving:** 15 career catches is not enough for a Shanahan-tree back. Fleury will ask backs to line up wide and run real routes. Price showed growth in 2025 (6 catches, 2 receiving TDs), but this is a Year 1-to-2 development arc, not a Day 1 weapon.
- **Pass protection:** Willingness is there. Technique needs NFL coaching. Fleury's bootleg-heavy system mitigates this somewhat — when the QB is rolling, the back's blitz pickup responsibilities simplify.
- **Ball security:** 3 fumbles in 2025 is a real flag. Not a scheme issue — a technique issue. Correctable, but it's on the development checklist.

**The critical translation point:** Price doesn't need a scheme adjustment period. He's already running these concepts. In a Year 2 system install (Kubiak put it in, Fleury is maintaining it), a zone-experienced rookie can contribute immediately. That's different from asking a gap-only college back to learn zone footwork from scratch.

## The Deployment Logic: What Does Week 1 Actually Look Like?

This is where I push past "does he fit?" and answer "does the offense *need* him at this price?"

### Scenario A: Charbonnet Ready Week 1

| Role | Player | Touches/Game | Scheme Alignment |
|------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| RB1A | Charbonnet | 14-16 | Zone primary, goal line |
| RB1B | Price (if drafted) | 8-10 | Zone rotational, 3rd-down package, KR |
| RB2 | Wilson | 4-6 | Short yardage, gap/power |

This is a well-built committee. Charbonnet handles the zone workload, Price spells him and adds return value, Wilson provides a stylistic change-up. The offense runs at 85-90% of ceiling.

**Without Price in this scenario**, Wilson moves to RB2 and you're running a 2-man rotation. That works for 10 weeks. It doesn't work for 17 + playoffs. History in Shanahan-tree systems is clear: committees that go below three backs break down by November. Wilson can't provide the zone reps Charbonnet needs off, and Holani isn't an NFL contributor. You're one Charbonnet aggravation away from a crisis.

### Scenario B: Charbonnet NOT Ready Week 1

| Role | Player | Touches/Game | Scheme Alignment |
|------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| RB1 | Price (if drafted) + Veteran bridge | 12-15 combined | Zone primary |
| RB2 | Wilson | 6-8 | Gap packages, short yardage |
| IR | Charbonnet | 0 | Returns mid-season |

**Without Price in this scenario**, Seattle is starting Emanuel Wilson as its wide zone primary back. I said it above and I'll say it louder: **Wilson as RB1 in a zone-first offense is a schematic mismatch that suppresses the entire run game.** Play-action loses credibility when the run game averages 3.4 YPC because the lead back can't read zone blocks at NFL speed. Darnold's efficiency craters. The passing game shrinks because defenses stop respecting the run fake.

A veteran bridge alone doesn't solve this. Brian Robinson Jr. (the v1 article's bridge recommendation) is a physical, downhill runner — great complement, poor primary zone back. Raheem Mostert is 34 and a one-year rental at minimum money. Neither is your primary zone runner for weeks 1-8.

**Price as the Day 1 zone starter with a veteran complement is a different offense than Wilson as the Day 1 zone starter.**

## Where I'll Disagree With Other Panelists

**SEA will argue CB/EDGE should take priority.** They're probably right on need severity. I don't dispute that losing Woolen, Bryant, and Mafe creates real defensive problems. But need severity isn't the same as drop-off severity. Seattle can sign a veteran CB or EDGE in free agency and get 80% of a starter. Seattle cannot sign a veteran RB and get a zone-scheme-fit committee back who contributes for four years on a rookie deal. The replacement curve is steeper at RB *within this specific scheme* than the raw need ranking suggests.

**Injury will focus on the Charbonnet timeline.** If Injury's assessment says Charbonnet has a 90%+ chance of being ready Week 1, my pull drops to 5/10. If it's 50-60%, my pull goes to 8/10. The medical assessment is the swing variable for the entire offensive case.

**CollegeScout may find a Day 3 zone back alternative.** If there's a legitimate zone runner available at #96 who can contribute immediately, the argument for Price at #64 weakens. But scheme-fit backs who can step in and run NFL-level zone concepts from Day 1 — with the vision, decisiveness, and burst required — are not common. They're rare. That's why #64 is the right investment, not #96.

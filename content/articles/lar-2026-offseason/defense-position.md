# Defense Position: The Secondary Overhaul Changes Everything — If It Gels

**By:** Defense — Defensive Scheme Expert
**Article:** LAR 2026 Offseason — Stafford's Last Dance

---

## Can This Secondary Fix Work?

Let's be honest about what happened in the NFC Championship: 346 passing yards wasn't just a bad day — it was a schematic exposure. Chris Shula's defense lives on aggressive pressure (~30% blitz rate) paired with man-coverage looks behind it. When your corners can't hold up on an island, every blitz becomes a suicide mission. Seattle exploited that marriage relentlessly.

The McDuffie-Watson-Curl overhaul is designed to fix the *mechanism* that broke, not just the stat line. That's the right approach. But history says caution is warranted — secondary units that import three or more new starters rarely hit their ceiling in Year 1. Communication breakdowns in pattern-match zone, busted rotation assignments, trust issues in off-coverage — these take reps to iron out. The 2021 Rams secondary (Ramsey, Williams, Long, Rapp) took until Week 8 to truly click, and that group had the benefit of Jalen Ramsey's on-field quarterbacking. I'd project this unit as league-average by midseason and potentially top-10 by December. That's enough for a championship run if the front holds up — but don't expect an elite unit in September.

## McDuffie Scheme Fit

McDuffie is arguably the best possible acquisition for what Shula runs. This defense asks its CB1 to handle island assignments when blitzes pull safeties out of their deep shells — McDuffie did exactly that in Kansas City's man-heavy scheme under Steve Spagnuolo. But here's the critical nuance: McDuffie is equally dangerous in the Rams' pattern-match zone concepts. His route anticipation allows him to play "match" technique — reading the receiver's release and matching the route like man coverage while maintaining zone responsibilities. That versatility lets Shula keep his pre-snap disguise game intact. Offenses can't sniff out man vs. zone based on McDuffie's alignment, because he executes both at an All-Pro level. He also enables more aggressive safety deployment — Kam Curl can trigger downhill as a box/blitz weapon without leaving a coverage void behind him.

## Remaining Defensive Holes

The secondary overhaul papers over the biggest crack, but it reveals the next one: the second edge rusher opposite Jared Verse. Byron Young is a solid run defender, but he's not a consistent pass-rush threat. In a defense that blitzes 30% of the time, you'd think that's less critical — but it's actually *more* critical. When Shula sends five or six, the four-man rush games are the constraint plays that keep offenses honest. If Young can't win his 1-on-1s when the offense knows a blitz isn't coming, coordinators will sit in quick-game and neutralize the pressure packages. The linebacker spot is thin too — running a 3-3-5 with only one true ILB means that player must be a coverage weapon, and the current options are replacement-level in space.

## My Recommended Path for #13

From a defensive perspective, EDGE at #13 is the move. Verse needs a complementary rusher who can generate consistent pressure from the opposite side. An elite edge pair does two things for Shula's scheme: it makes the four-man rush viable as a standalone weapon (freeing up secondary coverage by reducing blitz dependency), and it forces offensive lines into impossible protection math when blitzes *do* come. If a top edge talent like Princely Umanmielen or Nic Scourton is there at 13, that's the pick. If not, trading back for a second-round edge plus future capital is defensible — but staying at 13 and reaching for an offensive player wastes the one chance to complete this defense.

## The Non-Obvious Insight

Here's what most people miss: the secondary upgrade doesn't just fix the passing defense — it *unlocks the pass rush*. Shula's blitz-heavy scheme creates a feedback loop between coverage and pressure. Better corners mean longer coverage windows. Longer coverage windows mean more sacks. More sacks mean shorter downs. Shorter downs mean simpler coverage calls. McDuffie's ability to survive on an island for 3+ seconds is functionally equivalent to adding another pass rusher — it turns Verse's 0.5-second speed-to-pressure advantage into a full sack instead of a hurry. The Rams didn't just buy a cornerback. They bought time — and in this scheme, time is pressure.

## Areas of Agreement

1. **Full Send is the only path.** All four panelists independently arrived at Path 1. The Rams built this cap structure to peak in 2026 — hedging weakens the window without preventing the 2027 cliff. This organization's identity under McVay/Snead is go-all-in; every recent move confirms it.

2. **The secondary overhaul is correct in concept.** The NFC Championship exposed a schematic failure (blitz-man marriage collapsed without island-capable corners), not just a talent gap. McDuffie's man/zone versatility is a scheme unlock that enables more aggressive safety deployment and blitz packages. This is structural, not cosmetic.

3. **Stay at #13 and draft — don't trade.** The trade-back return (late 2027 1st + 2026 2nd) doesn't help a team whose window might close after 2026. A first-round rookie at ~$4.5M/yr is the best surplus-value play on a roster allocating 16% of cap to the quarterback alone.

4. **2027 is the cliff.** Cap projects ~$3M in space with the full secondary overhaul escalating simultaneously. The "synchronized cap bomb" — McDuffie, Watson, and Curl all escalating in the year Stafford exits — is the structural risk. This is 2026 or bust, by design.

## Key Tensions

### Tension 1: OT vs. EDGE at #13
The panel's only significant disagreement. LAR and Draft lean OT (Monroe Freeling) because McClendon at RT is the ticking time bomb behind a 38-year-old QB. Defense demands EDGE (Keldric Faulk or Princely Umanmielen) because Byron Young can't win 1-on-1s, which undermines the blitz-heavy scheme's constraint plays. Draft offers a resolution: the LB-deep class may be suppressing EDGE values, potentially sliding a top-12 talent to #13 — making EDGE the better value play. **This is the article's central draft debate: protect Stafford's body or complete the defense that let him down?**

### Tension 2: The Secondary Gel Timeline
Defense warns that 3+ new secondary starters rarely hit their ceiling in Year 1 — expect league-average by midseason, top-10 by December. The 2021 Rams secondary (Ramsey, Williams, Long, Rapp) didn't click until Week 8. If this team needs to beat Seattle in January, the secondary must gel faster than history suggests. **The writer should explore whether the KC connection (McDuffie + Watson both from Kansas City's system) accelerates this timeline.**

### Tension 3: The Post-Window Devastation
Cap's "synchronized cap bomb" insight is the article's financial engine. All three secondary contracts escalate in the same year Stafford either retires (dead money) or declines (sunk cost). Teams that stagger contract timelines survive windows. The Rams stacked theirs. **The writer should quantify what 2027 looks like under each scenario: Stafford plays, Stafford retires, Stafford is traded.**

## Recommended Path — Lead's Synthesis

**Path 1: Full Send — EDGE at #13, protect Stafford with remaining cap space.**

Reasoning:

1. **EDGE over OT at #13.** The secondary investment only pays off if the pass rush completes the feedback loop (better coverage → more sacks → shorter downs → simpler coverage). Byron Young opposite Verse is the weak link. If Faulk or Umanmielen is available at #13, take the EDGE. The RT problem is real but more solvable in free agency or Day 2 of the draft.

2. **Do NOT restructure Stafford.** Cap is emphatic and correct — the $8-12M in 2026 relief poisons 2027 further. The $40-44M in existing space is enough. Spend it on a mid-tier RT and depth pieces.

3. **Accept 2027 as the cliff.** The Rams already made this decision when they traded for McDuffie. Don't pretend otherwise. The article should frame this honestly: this is a one-year championship window with a controlled demolition on the other side.

4. **The Kingsbury angle is the article's non-obvious hook.** LAR's insight that McVay is building a coaching staff specifically to beat Seattle — not just a roster to win a Super Bowl — elevates this above a standard "team goes all-in" piece. The writer should use this framing.

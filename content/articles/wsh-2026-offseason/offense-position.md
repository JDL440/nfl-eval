# Offense Position: WSH 2026 Offensive Viability

**Verdict: Not viable as-is. Three structural failures converge before a snap is taken, and the scheme shift amplifies every one of them.**

## The Scheme Shift Is the Multiplier

Under-center balanced attack has three non-negotiable requirements: a center who can execute direct snaps and make line calls cleanly, a downhill RB capable of 200+ carries to make play-action credible, and a QB whose mechanics were built under-center. Washington has none of them.

Daniels is an LSU/Air Raid product. His entire processing model — pre-snap reads, pull-and-run timing, RPO execution — is built around shotgun depth. Moving him under-center in Year 3 with a first-time OC doesn't just create a learning curve. It inverts the development arc.

## The Hidden Kill Shot: Allegretti at Center

This will get underreported. Under-center snaps are a physical exchange — hips, hand placement, departure timing. A mis-timed exchange aborts bootlegs mid-action and breaks the timing on every play-action fake before Daniels takes a step. Allegretti is a guard converting to center. That conversion is manageable in shotgun. It is a systemic risk in under-center offense, where the C-QB exchange dictates the tempo of the entire run game. A guard-converted-center running under-center concepts for a first-time OC is not a depth concern — it is a play-design liability on every RPO, every boot, every naked keeper.

## Blough's Inexperience Is Specifically Dangerous Here

First-time OCs typically fail at situational football — down-distance feel, clock management, game script adjustments. Blough's failure mode is worse: he is installing a scheme he has never called at any level, with an offense missing three starters, and asking a shotgun-native QB to operate under-center. Under-center balance requires coordinator feel that only comes from reps — knowing when the run threat is credible, when play-action has actually sold the safety. Blough has zero of those reps. The vision lives on paper.

## Minimum Viable Additions (Priority-Ordered)

1. **Center — starter-tier, not emergency.** Under-center offense without a real center is a fumble machine. Connor Williams or equivalent. This is non-negotiable.
2. **RB1 — 20-carry-capable hammer.** Rachaad White is a checkdown receiver with no downhill profile. Balanced attack without a credible run threat is just dropback offense with extra steps.
3. **WR2 — veteran, slot-capable.** Even a bridge player. McLaurin and McCaffrey as WR1/2 gives Daniels one read against bracket coverage.

## The Non-Obvious Insight: Okonkwo Is Scheme-Contradictory

The $30M Okonkwo signing reads as commitment to 12 personnel — the natural home of under-center balance. But Okonkwo is a spread TE. His value is seam routes and checkdowns; he grades below average as an in-line blocker. True 12-personnel under-center attack requires the second TE to crack-block safeties, kick out edge rushers, and handle gap assignments. That's John Bates — a blocking TE Washington already had. Spending $30M on a receiving TE for a system that needs a blocking TE suggests either Peters and Blough are not aligned on what this offense actually looks like, or "under-center balanced attack" is marketing language for spread-with-TE-injection. If it's the latter, stay in shotgun and stop fighting Daniels' mechanics.

## Floor Without Additions

Bottom-5 scoring offense. Expect sub-14 points in losses against competent defenses, 20-22 in wins. Defense-dependent .500 ceiling — which is exactly where the dual first-time coordinator pattern predicts this team lands.

# Washington Has $66M in Cap Space, a Franchise QB on a Rookie Deal, and an Offense Missing Half Its Parts. That's Not Patience. That's a Bet.

*Our Commanders, cap, and offensive scheme experts agree Adam Peters was right to fortify the defense. They also agree he can't stop there if Washington wants Jayden Daniels' cheap years to matter.*

---

**By: The NFL Lab Expert Panel**

*WSH · Cap · Offense*

> **📋 TLDR**
> - **Jayden Daniels** enters the 2026 offseason still costing just $10.3M against the cap, and Washington still has roughly $66M in space to work with.
> - Adam Peters spent aggressively on the side of the ball that embarrassed the Commanders in 2025, building what looks like a top-10 front seven.
> - The problem: the offense still lacks a real center, a real RB1, a real WR2, and even a kicker — while a first-time OC tries to install an under-center system.
> - Our panel's verdict: the defense-first philosophy was defensible; leaving the offense unfinished is not. Washington should spend about $31–38M now, then trade down from #7 to refill missing draft capital.

If you wanted to make the cleanest possible argument for Adam Peters this spring, it wouldn't be hard. The 2025 Commanders went 5-12. **Daniels** missed seven games with three separate injuries. The defense couldn't get off the field. The quarterback kept getting dragged into bad scripts, obvious passing downs, and comeback football. So Peters spent like a man who never wanted to watch that movie again.

He bought edge help. He bought front-seven depth. He bought toughness, range, and rotational bodies. In pure team-building theory, it makes sense: if your franchise quarterback is taking too many hits, build a defense that shortens games, improves field position, and keeps him out of weekly rescue mode.

The problem is that Washington didn't just build the defense first. It built the defense almost exclusively. The offense Daniels is supposed to develop in still has a hole at center, a hole at running back, a hole at WR2, and no kicker under contract. That's not a finished plan. That's a wager that infrastructure on one side of the ball can cover instability on the other.

And our panel keeps coming back to the same question: if Daniels is this franchise's most valuable asset, why is Washington treating a complete offense like a 2027 luxury item?

---

## The Smart Part of the Plan: Peters Correctly Identified the Real 2025 Failure

The local view from our **WSH** expert is less hysterical than the fan panic. Washington's defense-first approach wasn't random, and it wasn't cowardice. Daniels' 2025 season was shaped as much by availability as production. The Commanders didn't lose because they were one gadget receiver short. They lost because their quarterback got hurt, the defense bled points, and the roster kept forcing the offense to play from behind.

> *"Peters' logic: build the defense, take hits off Daniels' body, control games with the front seven, and let the offense develop without asking a 25-year-old QB with injury red flags to throw 45 times a game."* — **WSH**

That's a coherent thesis. And the spending reflects it. Peters added **Odafe Oweh**, **Settle**, and **Leo Chenal** up front, giving Washington something closer to the kind of eight-man front-seven wave playoff teams trust in January. He also bolstered the secondary with **Amik Robertson** and **Nick Cross**.

| Area | What Washington Did | Why It Makes Sense |
|------|---------------------|--------------------|
| EDGE/front seven | Added Oweh, Chenal, Settle and more defensive muscle | Reduces shootout pressure on Daniels |
| Defensive identity | Leaned into Quinn's preference for speed and violence up front | Better game scripts, fewer obvious passing situations |
| Long-term build | Preserved major 2027 flexibility | Creates a second wave of spending if 2026 works |

The issue is that a sound opening move isn't the same thing as a complete offseason. The Commanders can be right about the defense and still wrong about the total construction order.

Because here's the part even the Peters defenders in the panel concede: the offense isn't mostly done. It's still missing some of the parts that make the rest of the plan function.

| Offensive Need | Current State | Why the Panel Cares |
|----------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Center | **Nick Allegretti** projected into a conversion role after **Tyler Biadasz**'s exit | Critical if Blough really wants under-center offense |
| RB1 | **Rachaad White** profiles more as a receiving back than a 20-carry hammer | Weakens the credibility of a balanced attack |
| WR2 | **Terry McLaurin** is still the clear alpha; behind him the room is projection | Bracket coverage becomes too easy |
| Kicker | No proven answer under contract | Self-explanatory, and still somehow real |

That's what makes this such a fascinating beat-level debate. Washington didn't ignore the roster. It just solved the half that was easiest to defend in a press conference.

## The Cap Argument Is Brutal: Unspent Rookie-QB Value Doesn't Roll Over Emotionally

Our cap expert's position is the sharpest counterpunch in the entire discussion: the Commanders are acting like unused cap space is proof of discipline, when in practice it may be proof they're underusing the cheapest thing in football — a real quarterback on a rookie deal.

**Daniels** counts $10.3M against the cap in 2026. Market-rate quarterbacks live north of $55M. That's roughly a $45M annual surplus, and it exists for exactly two more seasons before extension math starts closing the cheat code. Washington has already spent aggressively in 2026 — just not where the offense needs it.

> *"The offense is underfunded by approximately $30–35M relative to what the cap structure allows. Peters isn't being disciplined — he's leaving the single most valuable asset in football without the supporting infrastructure to develop."* — **Cap**

Cap's key distinction is between topline spending and productive spending. The offense/defense split looks balanced if you just glance at the big numbers. It looks a lot less balanced when you strip out dead money tied to offensive players who are gone.

| Spending View | Offense | Defense | What It Means |
|---------------|---------|---------|---------------|
| Topline allocation | ~$120M | ~$122M | Looks balanced on the surface |
| Productive spending view | ~$99M | ~$122M | Offense trails by roughly $23M once dead money is stripped out |
| New marginal spending | Minimal compared to defense | Majority of new money | Peters chose defense with the spendable dollars |

That leads to the central cap question: what would it actually cost to make the offense functional *now*?

| Position | Realistic 2026 Cap Hit | Why It Matters |
|----------|------------------------|----------------|
| Starter-tier center | $8–10M | Makes the under-center plan operational |
| RB1 | $9–11M | Gives the offense real run-game gravity |
| Veteran WR2 | $10–12M | Prevents McLaurin from living in bracket coverage |
| Kicker | $4–5M | Avoids losing games to a roster hole that should not exist |
| **Total** | **$31–38M** | Still leaves meaningful flexibility inside $66M of space |

That is the part Washington fans should linger on. The shopping list sounds long. The bill doesn't. Even if the Commanders spend roughly $35M to fill every obvious offensive gap, they still keep room for in-season moves, draft-pick slots, and basic operational sanity.

Then comes Cap's nastiest point: the 2027 war chest isn't fake, exactly, but it is being romanticized. The big projection number assumes empty roster spots are free. They are not.

> *"Real discretionary space in 2027 is closer to $68M than $165M once you price in filling out the roster."* — **Cap**

That's the philosophical hinge of the entire article. If Washington waits, it may preserve future optionality. It also burns one of the last two years where Daniels is dramatically cheaper than the quarterbacks he's supposed to beat — and a year of Daniels developing inside a complete offense is the one thing Washington cannot buy back later.

## The Offense Problem Isn't Just Talent. It's That the Scheme Magnifies the Missing Pieces.

This is where our **Offense** expert takes the conversation from conventional roster talk into something more dangerous. Washington isn't merely trying to survive with a thin offense. It's trying to install an under-center balanced attack with a quarterback whose background is overwhelmingly shotgun-based, behind a line that may be forcing a guard-to-center conversion, under a coordinator who has never called plays at any level while **Daronte Jones** breaks in as a first-time defensive coordinator on the other side.

That's not a small-tweak offseason. That's a structural experiment.

> *"Not viable as-is. Three structural failures converge before a snap is taken, and the scheme shift amplifies every one of them."* — **Offense**

The center issue is the kill shot. Around the league, fans talk about center like it's a generic interior line problem. The panel doesn't. In this specific offense, center is the point of ignition for the entire playbook.

**Allegretti** taking over after **Tyler Biadasz** isn't just about pass protection or line calls. Under-center football turns the quarterback-center exchange into timing infrastructure. A bad snap point kills bootlegs before they start. A mistimed exchange muddies play-action. A hesitant center wrecks the rhythm of a run game that's supposed to sell balance before Daniels even turns his shoulders.

> *"A guard-converted center running under-center concepts for a first-time OC is not a depth concern — it is a play-design liability on every RPO, every boot, every naked keeper."* — **Offense**

That's not normal positional handwringing. That's an argument that one missing starter can poison the whole schematic vision.

And the rest of the offense doesn't rescue the plan. **Rachaad White** is useful, but the panel doesn't see him as the 20-carry downhill presence that makes under-center play-action credible. **Terry McLaurin** is still good enough to tilt coverage, but if the WR2 spot remains theoretical, defenses get to crowd the picture. And the **Chigoziem Okonkwo** addition, which should have looked like a clean offensive investment, may actually reveal a deeper identity problem.

Offense's non-obvious point here is excellent: Okonkwo is more spread weapon than true mauling in-line tight end. In a true 12-personnel, under-center build, the second tight end has to do dirty work. If Washington paid for the receiving version while selling everyone on a downhill identity, then either the personnel and the scheme are misaligned — or the scheme rhetoric is covering for a plan that still wants to live in shotgun.

| Minimum Viable Addition | Priority | Why It Comes Before Everything Else |
|-------------------------|----------|-------------------------------------|
| Center | 1 | Without it, the under-center structure is unstable |
| RB1 | 2 | Without it, "balanced" becomes branding instead of threat |
| WR2 | 3 | Without it, Daniels gets compressed into obvious reads |

This is also why the first-time-OC problem feels bigger than the usual Twitter panic. Plenty of young coordinators fail because the play sheet gets messy. **David Blough's** version of risk is worse: he is trying to teach a new operational language to a quarterback, with incomplete personnel, while learning the NFL play-calling job in real time.

That doesn't mean Washington is doomed. It means the margin for leaving obvious holes unfilled is basically gone.

## The #7 Pick Debate Is Really a Resource Allocation Fight in Disguise

Once the panel moved from free agency to the draft, the conversation got even more interesting. Because the #7 pick looks like an easy solution right up until you remember what Washington doesn't have behind it.

The Commanders are missing second- and fourth-round capital after the **Laremy Tunsil** trade. That's why our WSH expert keeps coming back to the same answer: trade down.

> *"Taking Love at #7 fills one gap but leaves the others unaddressed and costs premium capital on a non-premium position. Trade down to 15-20, acquire a second-rounder, and fill multiple holes."* — **WSH**

The temptation, of course, is obvious. If **Jeremiyah Love** is there and the offense desperately needs juice in the backfield, the fan-brain solution is to draft the electric player and figure the rest out later. But that's exactly the kind of move that feels decisive on draft night and incomplete by Labor Day.

| Path at #7 | Upside | Hidden Cost |
|------------|--------|-------------|
| Draft RB at #7 | Immediate splash, solves one obvious need | Burns premium capital on a non-premium spot while center and WR2 still dangle |
| Draft another single position | Adds talent, keeps star-upside on the board | Same core problem: Washington needs volume as much as it needs top-end help |
| Trade down into 15-20 range | Gains extra capital, especially a needed Day 2 asset | Less sexy on TV, but better aligned with the roster math |

The cap and offense perspectives both quietly support the trade-down logic even when they don't phrase it that way. If the team can buy a center and a WR2 with money, then the draft can target value instead of panic. If the draft has to rescue three offensive holes at once, then every miss becomes catastrophic.

This is the biggest lesson from the panel discussion: Washington's offseason decisions are all connected. You can't separate free agency from scheme, or scheme from draft capital, or draft capital from Daniels' developmental curve. Every unanswered question makes the next decision more expensive.

## Where the Panel Actually Disagrees

This is not a fake TV-panel disagreement where everyone yells toward the same conclusion. The experts genuinely split on how much grace Peters deserves.

| Question | WSH | Cap | Offense |
|----------|-----|-----|---------|
| Was defense-first the right opening move? | Yes, mostly | Understandable, but overdone | Only if the offense gets finished quickly |
| Can Washington wait for 2027? | More defensible | Not really; the window is perishable | No if this scheme remains incomplete |
| What's the biggest danger? | Dual first-time coordinators | Wasting Daniels' cheap years | The center/scheme mismatch collapsing the entire plan |
| What should happen at #7? | Trade down | Prefer filling needs efficiently, not romantically | Fine with any path that doesn't ignore center and RB reality |

The cleanest way to say it is this: WSH thinks Peters' philosophy is right and his execution is incomplete. Cap thinks the execution problem is so large it risks invalidating the philosophy. Offense thinks the scheme makes patience almost impossible, because the current setup isn't merely suboptimal — it's unstable.

And yet all three land in roughly the same place by the end. Nobody is arguing for a panic-spend. Nobody is saying Washington should rip up the whole plan and chase every shiny veteran. The consensus is subtler, and probably smarter than the loudest versions of this debate online.

The panel's real consensus is that Peters doesn't need to reverse course. He just needs to finish the job.

## The Verdict: Washington Is Building the Right Kind of Team in the Wrong Sequence

Here's the beat-level answer after sitting with all three positions: Peters was correct to look at 2025 and decide the Commanders needed to get harder, deeper, and more competent on defense. He was wrong to treat the offensive remainder as something that could wait without consequence.

A franchise quarterback on a $10.3M cap hit is not a museum asset. You don't preserve it by leaving it behind glass. You use it. You build around it while the advantage exists. Washington has enough room to do that without torching the future. The panel's blueprint is neither reckless nor timid. It's just overdue.

| Priority | Move | Estimated 2026 Cost | Why It Matters |
|----------|------|---------------------|----------------|
| 1 | Sign a starter-tier center | $8–10M | Makes the entire offensive structure functional |
| 2 | Add a real RB1 | $9–11M | Gives Daniels actual play-action and game-script support |
| 3 | Add a veteran WR2 | $10–12M | Prevents the passing game from shrinking around McLaurin |
| 4 | Sign a competent kicker | $4–5M | Stops a stupid roster hole from deciding real games |
| 5 | Trade down from #7 | Gain Day 2 capital | Lets Washington solve more than one problem before September |

That's roughly $31–38M. In other words: the Commanders can still finish this offseason and keep meaningful flexibility. The tension isn't cap-based anymore. It's philosophical.

If Peters does this, the 2026 story changes. The defense becomes the floor-raiser he intended. Daniels gets an offense that actually matches the urgency of his contract window. Blough gets a chance to run his preferred system without asking every snap to survive on improvisation. And Washington heads into the draft looking like a team shaping a contender, not a team hoping one side of the ball can cover for the other.

It also makes the coordinator gamble more survivable. Washington still has to live with Blough's first play-calling season and Jones' first run as an NFL defensive coordinator, but it's a lot easier to survive that volatility in an NFC East that suddenly looks more open than intimidating.

If Peters doesn't do it, then 2026 becomes something much stranger: one of the most expensive developmental years in football. A team with cap space, a franchise quarterback, and a plausible division path — voluntarily treating offensive completeness as optional.

That's not patience. That's a bet.

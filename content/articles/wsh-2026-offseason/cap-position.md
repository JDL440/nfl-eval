# Cap Position: Washington's $66M in Undeployed Capital Is Depreciating in Real Time

**Verdict:** The offense is underfunded by approximately $30–35M relative to what the cap structure allows. Peters isn't being disciplined — he's leaving the single most valuable asset in football (a franchise QB on $10.3M) without the supporting infrastructure to develop.

## The Rookie-Deal Surplus Math

Daniels' $10.3M cap hit creates a ~$45M annual surplus versus market-rate QBs ($55M+). That surplus exists for exactly two years: 2026 and 2027. Total value of the window: ~$90M in purchasing power that vanishes permanently once the extension kicks in. Peters has deployed the 2026 surplus almost entirely on defense — Oweh ($25M 2026 hit), Settle (~$8M), Chenal (~$8.25M), Robertson (~$8M), Cross (~$7M). That's ~$56M in new defensive money. The offensive additions? Okonkwo at $10M/yr. That's it.

The allocation isn't 50/50 defense/offense — it's 85/15 in new spending. The $120M/$122M topline split is misleading because it includes legacy contracts (Tunsil, Cosmi, McLaurin) that were already committed. The *marginal dollar* went almost exclusively to defense.

## What the Offensive Gaps Actually Cost

Filling all four holes requires ~$31–37M in Year 1 cap hits:

| Position | Comp Range | Realistic 2026 Hit |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| RB1 | Henry $8M/yr – Jacobs $12M/yr | $9–11M |
| WR2 | Samuel $11.5M/yr – Hopkins $13M/yr | $10–12M |
| C | Biadasz $10M/yr (now LAC) | $8–10M |
| K | Bass $6.4M, McManus $5.1M | $4–5M |
| **Total** | | **$31–38M** |

Washington has $66M in space. Spending $35M fills every gap and leaves $31M for in-season flexibility, practice squad depth, and draft pick slots. The money is there. The decision not to spend it is a choice, not a constraint.

## The 2027 Illusion

Peters' implicit bet: save dry powder for a 2027 mega-spend. But the $164.6M in projected 2027 space is a mirage. Only 29 players are under contract. A 53-man roster needs 24 more bodies. Even a modest fill — 8 starters at $8M average, 16 depth pieces at $2M average — consumes $96M before a single premium signing. Real discretionary space in 2027: ~$68M, not $165M.

Now model the alternative: spend $35M in 2026, fill the offense, and roll the remaining $31M into 2027. That gives Washington ~$99M in 2027 discretionary space ($68M base + $31M rollover). The marginal cost of a complete 2026 offense is roughly $31M in reduced 2027 flexibility — a 31% haircut on future discretionary spending in exchange for a *fully functional offense around a developing franchise QB for an entire season*.

That's the opportunity cost Peters is ignoring: one year of Daniels' development with a complete supporting cast. You cannot buy that back.

## The Non-Obvious Problem: Dead Money Is Double-Counting

Washington carries $20.7M in dead money — $12.3M for Deebo Samuel and $8.1M for Tyler Biadasz. Both are offensive players. The Commanders are paying $20.7M for an offense that doesn't include those players, while simultaneously refusing to spend the cap space to replace them. The dead money creates the *illusion* of offensive investment in the $120M topline. Strip it out and Washington's actual productive offensive spending is ~$99M — 19% less than the defensive side. The offense isn't underfunded by $2M (the $120M/$122M gap). It's underfunded by $23M in productive spending, with $20.7M going to players wearing Chargers and 49ers jerseys.

Peters' patience may prove correct in 2027. But cap space is not a savings account — it's a perishable asset that loses relative value as the cap ceiling rises 8–10% annually. Every dollar unspent in 2026 buys ~92 cents in 2027 purchasing power. Hoarding $66M isn't discipline. It's paying a 8% annual carry cost on capital that could be generating wins today.

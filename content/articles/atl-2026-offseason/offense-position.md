# Offense Position: Stefanski's Run-Heavy System as a QB Mask — Atlanta's 2026 Offensive Identity

**Date:** March 2026

## Bottom Line Up Front

> **"This is the rare case where elite skill-position talent meets a system designed NOT to rely on elite quarterbacking — and that's exactly what Atlanta needs."**

Stefanski's Cleveland offenses averaged 29.1 rush attempts per game and ranked top-5 in play-action rate (31.4%) from 2020-2023. That's not a bug — it's the entire design. The system manufactures easy throws off run fakes, uses max-protection to minimize QB pressure, and leans on the ground game to control possession and game script. With Bijan Robinson, Drake London, and Kyle Pitts, Atlanta has the exact personnel to run this scheme at an elite level. The question isn't whether the fit is right — it's whether Michael Penix Jr. and Tua Tagovailoa can stay healthy enough to execute it.

## The Fit: Stefanski's System Meets Atlanta's Talent

**Bijan Robinson becomes a 2,200-scrimmage-yard centerpiece.** Stefanski turned Nick Chubb into a perennial All-Pro with 300+ carries and 40+ targets per season. Bijan is more talented as a receiver (78 receptions in 2025, elite hands) and equally effective as a runner (5.1 YPC career average). In Cleveland, Chubb averaged 22.4 touches per game in Stefanski's offense. Bijan should approach 25 touches — 18-20 carries, 5-7 receptions — making him a top-3 fantasy RB and the actual engine of this offense. Bill Callahan coaching the OL only amplifies this — Atlanta's run blocking should be top-10 immediately.

**Kyle Pitts finally gets his role.** David Njoku caught 81-882-6 in 2023 as Stefanski's featured TE in 12 personnel. Pitts is more talented than Njoku. The franchise tag ($15M) is justified if Atlanta runs 12 personnel on 35%+ of snaps and targets Pitts 110+ times. Stefanski's system treats the TE as a primary read on play-action boots and nakeds, not a checkdown. The athletic profile match is perfect: Pitts runs seam routes at 4.44 speed, creates mismatches against linebackers, and can sell run-fakes as a blocker before releasing. This could be a 1,000-yard, 8-TD breakout season.

**Drake London operates as a high-volume possession WR1.** Stefanski's top WR in Cleveland (Amari Cooper) averaged 8.4 targets per game in 2023, not because the offense was pass-heavy, but because the limited passing volume concentrated on one alpha receiver. London should see 140-150 targets — not because Atlanta throws 40 times per game, but because Penix/Tua will throw 28-32 times and London gets 9-10 of those attempts. His route tree (crossers, digs, back-shoulders) fits the intermediate West Coast concepts Stefanski runs. London's 2025 season (89-1,105-7) is his floor in this system, not his ceiling.

**The WR2 problem is real but manageable.** Jahan Dotson (2yr/$15M) is a speed complement, not a consistent target. Ray-Ray McCloud and Olamide Zaccheaus are WR4 types being asked to play WR3. But here's why it's manageable: Stefanski's offense doesn't *need* a true WR2 if the TE is a real weapon. Cleveland's WR2 (Elijah Moore, Cedric Tillman) averaged 3-4 targets per game because Njoku absorbed the second read. Pitts can do the same. The lack of a second outside receiver caps the ceiling against elite pass defenses, but it doesn't sink the offense if Bijan and Pitts are both deployed correctly.

## The QB Constraint: Can the System Mask Penix and Tua?

This is the hardest question, and the answer is **yes, but only if both stay on the field.**

**Michael Penix Jr. — The Athletic Question Mark:** Penix's arm talent (60+ yard deep balls, NFL-caliber velocity) fits Stefanski's vertical play-action concepts. His mobility (4.69 forty, but functional pocket movement) allows boots and rollouts that simplify reads. The scheme wants a QB who can hit the backside dig off play-action or the TE leak on a boot — Penix can do both when healthy. The concern is durability. Third ACL surgery is unprecedented for a starting QB. If Penix returns Week 1 at full health, he's a scheme fit. If the knee limits his mobility or forces him into pure dropback reps, the offense loses half its constraint plays (boots, nakeds, RPOs).

**Tua Tagovailoa — The Concussion Variable:** Tua on a vet-min prove-it deal ($1.2M) is the best backup QB value in the NFL *if* he's cleared to play. His 2021-2023 Miami tape shows elite timing, anticipation, and quick-game execution — all Stefanski concepts. The risk is binary: either he's medically cleared and functional, or another concussion ends his season (and possibly career). There's no middle ground. If Tua starts 10+ games, Atlanta's offense is top-12. If he's unavailable, Easton Stick (signed as QB3) is a disaster scenario.

**The system's masking capacity:** Stefanski's offense ranked 9th in EPA/play in 2023 with Deshaun Watson posting a 79.1 passer rating. That's the proof — the scheme doesn't need elite QB play to function. Max-protection (6-man protections on 23% of dropbacks, well above league average of 16%), pre-snap motion to identify coverage, and a dominant run game create a low-variance passing attack. Penix and Tua both need to complete 62-64% of passes at 7.2 yards per attempt — not elite, but competent. The floor is achievable if they stay healthy.

## Tommy Rees: First-Time Playcaller, Proven System

Rees has never called plays in the NFL. That's a risk. But he's not installing a new system — he's executing Stefanski's playbook, the same one he spent 2024 learning as Cleveland's pass game coordinator. The continuity matters. Rees doesn't need to reinvent the offense; he needs to manage tempo, situational play-calling, and red-zone execution.

**What Rees must do well:**
- **Third-down efficiency:** Stefanski's Cleveland offenses converted 42.1% of third downs (8th in NFL, 2020-2023). That number came from run-game credibility creating manageable 3rd-and-short. Atlanta needs the same discipline — don't abandon Bijan on 2nd-and-7.
- **Red-zone creativity:** Pitts in the red zone should be automatic (high-low reads, angle routes, fades against linebackers). Cleveland ranked 12th in red-zone TD rate (57.4%) under Stefanski. Atlanta has better weapons — they should be top-8.
- **Play-action discipline:** The run game only works if defenses respect it. Rees needs to call play-action on 30%+ of dropbacks (Stefanski's Cleveland average was 31.4%). If the rate drops to 22-25%, the scheme loses its teeth.

**What Rees doesn't need to do:** Innovate. This isn't a first-year system install. It's execution. Stefanski is the HC and oversees the offensive structure. Rees is the voice in the headset, not the architect. That's the right role for a first-time NFL playcaller.

## Where I Disagree with the Roster Grade

**The ATL agent calls this a "10-win roster trapped inside an 8-9 season." I'd say it's a 9-10 win offense with a 7-9 floor.**

The upside is real: Bijan in this system is a top-3 NFL RB, Pitts could have a Njoku-style breakout, London is a locked-in WR1, and the OL has talent (Chris Lindstrom at RG is All-Pro caliber). The coaching upgrade from Raheem Morris to Stefanski is massive — Cleveland's offense was consistently top-12 in efficiency under Stefanski despite Watson's decline.

But the QB room is a medical coin flip, not a solved problem. If Penix and Tua combine for 14+ healthy starts, the offense is top-10. If both miss significant time, Easton Stick starting is a 5-win disaster. The "hidden strength" argument assumes health, and health is the one thing this roster cannot assume.

The WR2 hole is also more limiting than the ATL agent suggests. Dotson is fine as a WR3, but against elite pass defenses (Philadelphia, San Francisco if they make the playoffs), the lack of a second outside threat will force London into double coverage and make Pitts the only reliable option. That's a capped ceiling.

## The Hardest Offensive Constraint

**The offense's ceiling is health-dependent, not scheme-dependent.**

Stefanski's system is perfect for this roster. Bijan, London, and Pitts are ideal fits. The OL has the talent to execute a power-gap run scheme. Tommy Rees doesn't need to be brilliant — he just needs to not screw it up.

But if Penix's knee isn't 100% by Week 1, or if Tua suffers another concussion, the entire offensive identity collapses. The run game only works if defenses respect the play-action. Play-action only works if the QB can execute the throw. And execution requires availability.

The constraint isn't "can this offense be elite?" — it's "will the QBs be on the field long enough to prove it?"

## Draft/Roster Implication

Atlanta's biggest offensive need at #48 (Round 2) is **not** a WR2 — it's an **interior OL depth piece** and a **TE2 who can block.** If Stefanski is running 12 personnel on 35% of snaps, the team needs a second TE who can sell the run fake and pass-protect on boots. Charlie Woerner is a plus blocker but limited as a receiver. Drafting a Brock Bowers-lite prospect (move TE, can block and catch) would give Atlanta the 12 personnel flexibility to maximize Pitts without sacrificing run-game integrity.

WR2 can be addressed in Round 4-5 or via post-June 1 cap space. The scheme doesn't demand a second star receiver — it demands competent execution from the top three skill players, and Atlanta already has those.

## Core Recommendation

**Run the ball 28+ times per game. Target Pitts 7-8 times. Let London operate in space off play-action. Trust the scheme to mask QB limitations.**

This offense should be top-12 in EPA/play and top-8 in time of possession. It won't be explosive (ranked 18th-22nd in explosive play rate), but it will be efficient, sustainable, and hard to defend when the run game is working. The 2026 identity is simple: **control the game, protect the QB, let the skill-position stars win their matchups.**

If Penix and Tua stay healthy, this offense competes for a playoff spot. If they don't, no scheme in the world can save it.

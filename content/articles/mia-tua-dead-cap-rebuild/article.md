**By: The NFL Lab Expert Panel**

Here's a number designed to make you feel something: $99.2 million. That's what the Miami Dolphins owe **Tua Tagovailoa** — a quarterback who will never take another snap in aqua and orange — spread across the next two salary caps like a financial hangover from a party nobody enjoyed. It's the largest single-player dead cap charge in NFL history. ESPN will put it in a graphic. Twitter will call the franchise cooked. And almost all of them will be wrong about what it actually means.

Because the Dolphins' problem isn't the dead money. It's everything the dead money is obscuring.

We sat three experts down — our Salary Cap specialist, our Miami Dolphins insider, and our Draft analyst — and asked a simple question: *How does Miami rebuild from here?* They agreed on more than we expected. They disagreed on the one thing that matters most. And the non-obvious play they surfaced might be the most creative rebuilding strategy we've covered on this panel.

---

## The Number Everyone Gets Wrong

Let's kill the $99.2 million panic right now.

Yes, it's a record. Yes, it's an obscene amount of dead money. And yes, it's almost entirely meaningless as a standalone figure — because the salary cap isn't a fixed target anymore. It's a moving one, and it's been sprinting uphill.

> *"Everyone fixates on the $99.2M total. The metric that actually predicts recovery speed is dead money as a percentage of the cap."* — **Cap, Salary Cap Expert**

Here's the table that reframes the entire conversation:

| Team | Year | Dead Cap | Salary Cap | Dead % | Recovery |
|------|-----:|---------:|-----------:|-------:|----------|
| LAR (Goff) | 2023 | $75M | $224M | **33.5%** | Competitive Yr 2 |
| DEN (Wilson) | 2024 | $85.2M | $255M | **33.4%** | Competitive Yr 2 |
| **MIA (Tua Y1)** | **2026** | **$55.4M** | **$301M** | **18.4%** | **TBD** |
| **MIA (Tua Y2)** | **2027** | **$43.8M** | **~$317M** | **13.8%** | **TBD** |
| HOU (Watson) | 2022 | ~$40M | $208M | **19.2%** | Competitive Yr 2 |

Miami's proportional hit in 2026 is *half* what the Rams and Broncos absorbed — and both teams were competitive within two years. The $99.2M is a nominal record because the cap has grown 35% since the Goff trade, not because the structural damage is worse. It's like comparing home run records across eras without adjusting for the ball.

The post-June 1 designation was the right call. Eating the full $99.2M in one year would've consumed 33% of the 2026 cap — Denver's worst case on a thinner roster. The split drops each year to survivable levels and preserves optionality: if **Malik Willis** flashes in 2026, Miami enters 2027 free agency with $65M+ and a cheap quarterback. That's the exact financial structure Houston rode with **C.J. Stroud**.

> *"This is a two-year tax, not a five-year sentence. Miami's math supports competitiveness by 2027. The constraint isn't dollars — it's quarterback."* — **Cap, Salary Cap Expert**

The money story has a happy ending. But money isn't the problem.

## The Roster Truth: Thinner Than It Looks

This is where our Miami expert started peeling wallpaper and finding rot.

> *"Strip away the names and count genuine building blocks — players you'd protect in an expansion draft. I get to five. That's it."* — **MIA, Dolphins Expert**

Here are those five:

| Player | Pos | Age | Contract Status | Role |
|--------|-----|----:|-----------------|------|
| **Jaylen Waddle** | WR | 24 | Under contract | WR1 — offensive centerpiece |
| **De'Von Achane** | RB | 24 | Rookie deal | Explosive playmaker |
| **Chop Robinson** | EDGE | 23 | Rookie deal | Pass rush upside |
| **Kenneth Grant** | DT | 23 | Rookie deal | Interior anchor |
| **Jonah Savaiinaea** | OL | 23 | Rookie deal | Developmental starter |

Five building blocks. On a 53-man roster. That's not a rebuild — that's a renovation where you discover the foundation needs replacing too.

The secondary is where it gets genuinely alarming. Miami lost **Jalen Ramsey**, **Minkah Fitzpatrick**, and **Kader Kohou** in one offseason and replaced them with fifth-round picks and street free agents. **Julius Brents**, **Jason Marshall Jr.**, **Omar Brown**, **Dante Trader Jr.** — not one has started a full NFL season. New defensive coordinator **Jeff Hafley** wants to run aggressive man coverage. He currently has no one to run it with. That's a scheme-talent mismatch that'll get exposed every Sunday.

The offensive depth is worse than you think, too. **Waddle** is a genuine WR1, but behind him sits **Malik Washington**, **Tutu Atwell**, and **Jalen Tolbert** — a receiver room that wouldn't crack most practice squads. **Achane** is electric but touches-dependent; you can't hand him the ball 25 times a game without breaking him. The offensive line has two promising young pieces in **Patrick Paul** and **Savaiinaea**, but the right side will hemorrhage pressure all season.

> *"The secondary is the real crisis, not the dead cap. Hafley wants to run aggressive man coverage. He has no one to run it with."* — **MIA, Dolphins Expert**

Our MIA expert projects 2026 at **4-13**. Willis behind a half-built offensive line, throwing to Waddle and nobody else, with a secondary full of developmental players playing man coverage against NFL receivers. The defense will be competitive at the line of scrimmage — **Robinson**, **Grant**, and **Zach Sieler** can get after it — and porous everywhere behind it.

That record, by the way, puts Miami in the top 3-5 pick range for the 2027 draft. Remember that. It matters later.

## The Draft: Don't Chase a Quarterback in a Barren Class

This is where our Draft expert gets emphatic.

> *"The 2026 QB class kills trade-up logic before the math enters the conversation. Trading from #11 into the top-5 for Ty Simpson would cost a top-5 premium for a QB whose consensus grade is late-teens. That's how you turn a rebuild into a decade of irrelevance."* — **Draft, Draft Expert**

**Shedeur Sanders** goes #1 to Las Vegas. After him, **Ty Simpson** and **Garrett Nussmeier** are mid-first talents at best — neither franchise-altering. The cost of trading up from #11 into the top-5 for Simpson would run Miami's 2nd-round pick plus at least one 3rd (~800+ points on the Jimmy Johnson chart). That's paying a king's ransom for a prince.

Instead, Draft says take the best player available. At #11, Miami sits in a defensive talent sweet spot:

| Prospect | Pos | School | Fit |
|----------|-----|--------|-----|
| **Mansoor Delane** | CB | LSU | Zero TDs allowed in 2025. Instant CB1 at Miami's most exposed position. |
| **Keldric Faulk** | EDGE | Auburn | High-ceiling rusher opposite Robinson. Legitimate edge duo on rookie deals. |
| **Sonny Styles** | LB | Ohio State | Sub-4.5 at 230+, historic combine. Versatile but LB isn't a premium need. |

**Delane is the pick.** CB is a premium position, Miami's secondary is gutted, and his coverage profile translates immediately. Faulk is the pivot if Delane goes top-10.

### The Three-Third-Round Advantage

Miami's real draft story isn't the first round — it's the three 3rds (own + via Houston + via Philadelphia from the **Jaelan Phillips** trade). Historical hit rate for Round 3 picks: roughly 38% become multi-year starters. Three swings gives Miami a ~76% chance of landing at least one.

| Round | Pick Source | Day 2 Target | Notes |
|------:|------------|--------------|-------|
| 2nd | Own | BPA: EDGE/CB/WR | Whichever position #11 didn't address |
| 3rd | Own | T.J. Parker (EDGE, Clemson) | Pairs with Delane-at-#11 scenario |
| 3rd | via HOU | Avieon Terrell (CB, Clemson) | Starting-caliber corner, secondary depth |
| 3rd | via PHI | KC Concepcion (WR, Texas A&M) | Size + contested catch to complement Waddle |

But here's the non-obvious insight — and all three panelists converge on this:

> *"Miami's three 3rds are more valuable as 2027 trade-up currency than as 2026 picks."* — **Draft, Draft Expert**

Package two 3rds with a future 1st and Miami can move into the top-10 of the 2027 draft. A class projected to include **Arch Manning**, **Nico Iamaleava**, and **LaNorris Sellers** — three legitimate franchise quarterback prospects versus zero in 2026 post-Sanders. The math is simple: use one 3rd in 2026, hold two as chips for the real QB move next April.

## The Play Nobody's Talking About: Weaponize the Dead Cap

This is our Cap expert's best insight, and it's the one that turns the conventional narrative upside down.

> *"Miami's dead money creates a cap-dump trade market. With 2026 space already burned, absorbing expiring veteran contracts costs nothing real — but it can yield draft picks."* — **Cap, Salary Cap Expert**

Here's how it works: contending teams hit cap crunches mid-season. They need salary relief to make a playoff push. Miami, already carrying $55.4M in dead money, can absorb expensive expiring contracts — veterans on one-year deals or the final year of bad contracts — without it costing anything against functional cap space. The money is already burned. Additional cap hits are rounding errors on a balance sheet that's already in the red.

In exchange, Miami collects draft picks. A 4th here, a 5th there. By the 2026 trade deadline, the Dolphins could turn their financial albatross into 2-3 additional selections — entering 2027 with 10+ picks instead of 8. That converts a financial constraint into a market-making tool.

It's the rebuilding equivalent of buying distressed assets in a down market. Everyone else sees dead money as a liability. Miami can make it an acquisition strategy.

## The Swing Variable: Chop Robinson's Year 2

Here's where the rebuild timeline forks, and it has nothing to do with the cap sheet.

> *"Watch Chop Robinson's Year 2 leap — it determines whether this is a two-year rebuild or a four-year one."* — **MIA, Dolphins Expert**

If **Robinson** goes from promising pass rusher to 10+ sack EDGE1 in 2026, Miami has a defensive cornerstone to pair with **Grant**. The 2027 free agency class only needs to fill complementary pieces — a CB2, a safety, a WR2. That's achievable with $65-85M in cap space.

If he plateaus, Miami is staring at needing premium talent at both EDGE and secondary — and no amount of cap space fills two premium positions in one offseason. The rebuild timeline stretches from 2027-28 to 2028-29 at best.

This is the tension our Cap expert's financial model doesn't capture. The money recovers on schedule. But money doesn't play football. **Robinson's development is the single highest-leverage variable in this rebuild**, and it makes the difference between "surprise contender in 2027" and "still rebuilding in 2029."

Our three experts agree on the destination. They diverge on the timeline — and Robinson is the reason.

## The Verdict: Green Bay Slow Build (Unanimous)

In 35 panel discussions, unanimous expert consensus is rare. It happened here. All three panelists independently recommended **Path 3: The Green Bay Slow Build.** No one advocated for scorched earth. No one pushed for a Hail Mary veteran QB trade. No one wanted to reach for a quarterback in a barren class.

Here's the panel's unified playbook:

| Phase | Action | Owner |
|-------|--------|-------|
| **2026 Draft** | Delane (CB) at #11, BPA EDGE/CB/WR on Day 2, hold 2 of 3 thirds | Draft |
| **2026 Season** | Willis starts. Record doesn't matter. Evaluation does. | MIA |
| **2026 Deadline** | Activate salary-dump trade market. Target 2-3 acquired picks. | Cap |
| **2027 Trigger** | Willis shows franchise traits → extend evaluation. Willis doesn't → deploy capital for Manning/Iamaleava/Sellers. | All |
| **2027 FA** | $65-85M targets proven starters at CB2, S, and WR2. | Cap |
| **Timeline** | 4-13 in 2026. 7-10 to 9-8 in 2027. Genuine contention in 2028. | Panel |

The bottom-line takes:

> *"This is a two-year tax, not a five-year sentence. At 18.4% and 13.8% of cap, both years are lighter proportional hits than what LA and Denver survived."* — **Cap, Salary Cap Expert**

> *"4-13 puts Miami in the top 3-5 pick range for 2027 — which is exactly where they need to be if Willis doesn't emerge."* — **MIA, Dolphins Expert**

> *"Build the defense now, buy the quarterback later — in a class that actually has one worth buying."* — **Draft, Draft Expert**

---

The $99.2 million number will dominate every Miami Dolphins story this offseason. It'll be in the headlines, the chyrons, the inevitable "worst cap situations in NFL history" listicles. And it'll be the wrong story almost every time.

The real story is simpler and harder: Miami has five building blocks, a gutted secondary, a first-time head coach, and a bridge quarterback — and the best version of their future requires the patience to let all four of those variables resolve before making the big move. The money isn't the constraint. The development is.

The Dolphins' $99 million ghost is real. But ghosts only haunt you if you're afraid of them. Jeff Hafley and Jon-Eric Sullivan don't seem afraid. They seem like they're building something. Slowly, deliberately, the Green Bay way.

It's the boring play. It's the right one.

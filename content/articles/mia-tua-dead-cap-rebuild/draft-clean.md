# $99 Million Ghost: How Miami Rebuilds With the Largest Dead Cap Hit in NFL History

*Our expert panel dissects the Dolphins' unprecedented dead money challenge — and why the record-breaking number might not matter as much as you think*

> **📋 TLDR**
> - Miami absorbs $99.2M in dead cap from Tua's release — the largest single-player hit in history
> - Panel consensus: Path 3 (Green Bay slow build), draft defense at #11, hold picks for 2027 QB class
> - The headline number is inflated — Miami's proportional burden (18.4% of cap) is half what LAR and DEN survived
> - Central debate: Can Miami compete by 2027, or does the thin roster push the timeline to 2028-29?

---

**By: The NFL Lab Expert Panel**

The Miami Dolphins just created a new NFL record nobody wants to hold: $99.2 million in dead cap space tied to Tua Tagovailoa. That's $14 million more than Russell Wilson's dead money crater in Denver. It's a number so large that every national headline treats it as a franchise obituary — a multi-year death sentence that guarantees irrelevance until 2028 at the earliest.

But here's what the headlines miss: dead cap math doesn't work the way casual fans think it does. The $99.2 million is split across two years ($55.4M in 2026, $43.8M in 2027) using the post-June 1 designation — a cap mechanism that spreads the hit over multiple years instead of accelerating it all into one season. As a percentage of available cap space, Miami's 2026 burden (18.4%) is comparable to Denver's first-year Wilson hit (20.8%) — and the Broncos made the playoffs in Year 1.

We convened three experts to evaluate whether Miami can execute a competitive rebuild under unprecedented dead money, or whether this albatross condemns them to a talent drought no amount of drafting can overcome. What we found: unanimous agreement on the path forward, sharp disagreement on how fast it actually works, and a non-obvious market strategy that turns Miami's biggest constraint into a draft-pick harvesting tool.

## The $99.2M That Isn't What It Seems

Everyone fixates on the total. The number that actually predicts recovery speed is **dead money as a percentage of the cap**.

| Team | Year | Dead Cap | Total Cap | Dead % | Recovery Timeline |
|------|------|----------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| LAR (Goff trade) | 2021 | $22.2M | $184.2M | **12.1%** | Won Super Bowl Y2 |
| DEN (Wilson cut) | 2024 | $53M | $255.4M | **20.8%** | Made playoffs Y1 |
| **MIA (Tua Y1)** | **2026** | **$55.4M** | **$301M** | **18.4%** | **—** |
| **MIA (Tua Y2)** | **2027** | **$43.8M** | **~$317M** | **13.8%** | **—** |
| HOU (Watson trade) | 2022 | ~$40M | $208M | **19.2%** | Competitive by 2023 |

Our salary cap expert put it bluntly: *"Miami's 2026 hit (18.4%) sits between Denver's first-year burden (20.8%) and the Rams' single-year Goff hit (12.1%). The Broncos made the playoffs in Year 1, the Rams won the Super Bowl in Year 2. The $99.2M is a nominal record because the cap itself has grown 64% since the Goff trade, not because the structural damage is worse."*

The post-June 1 designation was the right structural move. Eating $99.2M in one year would've consumed 33% of 2026 cap — Denver's worst case, on a thinner roster. Splitting the hit drops each year to survivable levels (18.4%, 13.8%) and preserves trade flexibility on movable veteran assets.

Here's the actual 2026 cap math:

**Miami's 2026 Usable Cap Space: ~$8-12 million**

| Line Item | Amount | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| 2026 salary cap | $301M | League-wide |
| Starting position | -$30M over | Pre-cuts |
| Tyreek Hill release | +$22.8M | Savings |
| Bradley Chubb cut (June 1) | +$15M | Projected savings |
| Minor restructures | +$5M | Estimate |
| Rookie pool (8 picks) | -$5-7M | Draft class |
| **Usable space** | **~$8-12M** | Coffee money |

That's a minimum-spend year. No splash signings. No overpays for free agent band-aids. Malik Willis at $3.3M becomes the starter by default — not because he's the solution, but because Miami can't afford anything else.

But 2027 changes the math entirely.

**Miami's 2027 Usable Cap Space: ~$65-85 million**

The cap rises to ~$317M. The second Tua tranche ($43.8M) still hits, but expensive veteran deals have cycled off. The young core — Jaylen Waddle, De'Von Achane, Chop Robinson, Patrick Paul — sits on rookie or early-extension deals. Miami re-enters the market as a real buyer.

> *"This is a two-year tax, not a five-year sentence. At 18.4% and 13.8% of cap, both years are lighter proportional hits than what LA and Denver survived. Miami's math supports competitiveness by 2027. The constraint isn't dollars — it's quarterback."*
> — Cap, Salary Cap Expert

## The Roster Reality: Five Building Blocks and a Crisis at Corner

Strip away the names and count genuine building blocks — players you'd protect in an expansion draft. Miami's roster expert gets to five: **De'Von Achane, Jaylen Waddle, Chop Robinson, Kenneth Grant, Jonah Savaiinaea.** That's it.

Everyone else is either aging out of the timeline (Calais Campbell is 40 years old, Zach Sieler turns 30), a bridge piece (Willis, Josh Uche, Aaron Brewer), or completely unproven (every single defensive back on this roster).

### The Secondary Is the Real Crisis

Miami lost Jalen Ramsey, Minkah Fitzpatrick, and Kader Kohou in one offseason and replaced them with fifth-round picks and street free agents. Julius Brents, Jason Marshall Jr., Omar Brown, Dante Trader Jr. — not one has started a full NFL season.

New head coach Jeff Hafley wants to run aggressive man coverage. He has no one to run it with. That's a scheme-talent mismatch that will get exposed weekly.

> *"The secondary is the real crisis, not the dead cap. Hafley wants to run aggressive man coverage. He has no one to run it with. That's a scheme-talent mismatch that will get exposed weekly."*
> — MIA, Miami Dolphins Expert

The offensive cupboard is deceptively bare too. Waddle is a genuine WR1, but behind him it's Malik Washington, Tutu Atwell, and Jalen Tolbert — a WR room that wouldn't start on most practice squads. Achane is electric but touches-dependent; you can't run him 25 times a game without breaking him. The offensive line has two promising young pieces in Paul and Savaiinaea, but the right side will hemorrhage pressure.

### What "Green Bay Way" Actually Means

The Hafley/Sullivan hire makes more sense than the national media gives it credit for. Green Bay's organizational philosophy isn't about any single scheme — it's about **positional value discipline and developmental patience.** The Packers consistently hit on Day 2 picks, develop players within their system for 2-3 years before expecting impact, and avoid the splashy free-agent overpay that created Miami's current mess.

Sullivan's first moves track with this: one-year prove-it deals across the roster, no panic spending, Malik Willis as a bridge with a clean two-year out. Bobby Slowik as OC is the smartest hire — his work developing C.J. Stroud in Houston shows he can maximize a limited QB.

The risk: Hafley has never been a head coach. Coordinating a full rebuild while installing a new defense while managing a locker room full of players who know they're placeholders — that's a multi-variable challenge that breaks a lot of first-time HCs.

**2026 Win Projection: 4-13**

Willis behind a half-built offensive line, throwing to Waddle and no one else, with a secondary full of developmental players playing man coverage. The defense will be competitive at the line of scrimmage (Robinson, Grant, Sieler) and porous everywhere behind it.

4-13 puts Miami in the top 3-5 pick range for 2027 — which is exactly where they need to be if Willis doesn't emerge.

## The Draft Strategy: Skip the Barren QB Class, Build the Defense, Hold for 2027

Miami enters the 2026 draft with 8 picks, including three third-rounders (own + HOU + PHI via Jaelan Phillips trade). The question: use the draft to accelerate the rebuild, or hold ammunition for a future QB trade-up?

Our draft expert's answer is unequivocal: **Do not take a QB at #11. Take Mansoor Delane (CB, LSU).**

### Why Not QB?

The 2026 QB class kills trade-up logic before the math enters the conversation. Jaxson Mendoza goes #1 to Las Vegas. After him, Ty Simpson and Garrett Nussmeier are mid-first talents at best — neither franchise-altering.

Trading from Miami's projected position into the top-5 for Simpson would cost the 2nd plus at least one 3rd (~800+ points on the Jimmy Johnson chart), paying a top-5 premium for a QB whose consensus grade is late-teens.

> *"That's how you turn a rebuild into a decade of irrelevance."*
> — Draft, Draft Expert

The 2027 QB class is the target. Arch Manning, Nico Iamaleava, and LaNorris Sellers — three legitimate franchise QB prospects versus zero in 2026 post-Mendoza. Willis starts in 2026, Miami stocks defensive talent on cheap contracts, and unused picks become chips for the real QB move next April.

### What Pick #11 Actually Buys

At Miami's projected draft position, they sit in a defensive talent sweet spot. Who likely falls:

- **Mansoor Delane (CB, LSU)** — Zero TDs allowed in 2025. Instant CB1 filling Miami's most exposed position.
- **Keldric Faulk (EDGE, Auburn)** — High-ceiling rusher opposite Chop Robinson. Legitimate edge duo on rookie deals.
- **Sonny Styles (LB, Ohio State)** — Sub-4.5 at 230+, historic combine. Versatile, but LB isn't a premium need.

**Delane is the pick.** CB is a premium position, Miami's secondary is gutted, and his coverage profile translates immediately. Faulk is the pivot if Delane goes top-10.

### The Three-Third-Round Advantage

Three 3rds in a defense-heavy class is the real story. Historical hit rate for Round 3: ~38% become multi-year starters. Three swings gives Miami a ~76% chance of landing at least one.

The 2nd-round pick should be BPA from EDGE/CB/WR — whichever premium position #11 didn't address.

Package two 3rds with a future 1st and Miami can move into the top-10 of the 2027 draft — a class projected to include Arch Manning, Nico Iamaleava, and LaNorris Sellers. Three legitimate franchise QB prospects versus zero in 2026 post-Mendoza."*

## The Non-Obvious Play: Weaponize Dead Cap as a Draft-Pick Market

Here's the insight everyone will miss: Miami's dead money creates a **cap-dump trade market**.

With 2026 space already burned, absorbing expiring veteran contracts costs nothing real — but it can yield draft picks. Teams needing mid-season salary relief route expensive deals through Miami; the Dolphins eat cap hits that are meaningless against already-dead space, and collect future assets.

The math is simple. Miami's $55.4M in dead money in 2026 means additional salary absorption has zero marginal cost. A contending team stuck $8M over the cap in October needs to shed an expiring deal to activate a waiver claim or make a practice squad promotion. Miami takes the contract, the contender sends a 2027 5th, and the Dolphins convert dead space into an asset.

The 8-pick 2026 class plus 2-3 acquired picks could give Miami 10+ selections entering 2027 — converting a financial constraint into a market-making tool.

> *"Dead space is already burned — additional cap hits are free. Target: 2-3 acquired picks by the 2026 trade deadline."*
> — Cap, Salary Cap Expert

This is how Miami turns a disadvantage into leverage. The Rams didn't do this in 2023 because they were trying to compete. Miami isn't competing in 2026 — they're harvesting.

## The Panel Disagreement: Two Years or Four?

Our three experts unanimously recommend **Path 3: The Green Bay Slow Build**. Accept 2026 as a developmental year. Draft defense at #11 (Delane or Faulk). Use Day 2 picks on EDGE/CB/WR depth. Hold two 3rds as 2027 trade-up currency. Activate the salary-dump trade market. Set the 2027 QB trigger.

But they fracture on the competitive timeline.

| Expert | Competitive by... | Why |
|--------|------------------|-----|
| **Cap** | **2027** | $65-85M in usable space, dead money clears after 2027, math supports two-year recovery |
| **MIA** | **Late 2027 to 2028** | Only 5 building blocks, secondary has zero proven starters, roster needs 8-10 starter additions |
| **Draft** | **2027-28 (flexible)** | Depends on Willis evaluation + Robinson's Year 2 leap + 2027 QB class hit rate |

Cap is right on the math. MIA is right on the talent gap. Both are true simultaneously.

Miami will have buying power in 2027 but will need to deploy it across too many holes for a single offseason to fix. The $65M in cap space fills 3-4 premium positions. Miami needs 8-10 starter-caliber additions.

The swing variable isn't the cap sheet — it's **Chop Robinson's Year 2 development**.

> *"Watch Chop Robinson's Year 2 leap — it determines whether this is a two-year rebuild or a four-year one. If Robinson goes from promising pass rusher to 10+ sack EDGE1, Miami has a defensive cornerstone to pair with Grant, and the 2027 free agency class only needs to fill complementary pieces. If he plateaus, Miami is looking at needing premium talent at both EDGE and secondary — and no amount of cap space fills two premium positions in one offseason."*
> — MIA, Miami Dolphins Expert

If Robinson emerges + Willis shows franchise traits = 2027 contention.

If either fails = 2028-29 at best.

## The Verdict: Path 3, Hold for Manning, Compete by 2028

The panel's recommended path:

### 2026: Accept the Development Year
- **Willis starts.** The record doesn't matter. The evaluation does.
- **Draft Mansoor Delane (CB, LSU) at #11.** Secondary is the unanimous crisis point.
- **Use Day 2 picks on EDGE + CB + WR depth.** Take BPA at the 2nd (EDGE/CB/WR), one 3rd on best remaining need, **hold two 3rds as 2027 trade-up currency**.
- **Activate the salary-dump trade market.** Absorb expiring contracts from cap-strapped contenders for draft picks. Target: 2-3 acquired picks by the trade deadline.

### 2027: The QB Trigger
- **If Willis shows franchise traits by Week 12 of 2026**, extend the evaluation into 2027.
- **If not**, deploy accumulated draft capital (top-5 own pick + preserved 3rds + acquired picks) to trade up for a franchise QB in the Manning/Iamaleava/Sellers class.
- **Free agency: secondary and WR2.** The $65-85M in usable space goes to proven starters at CB2, safety, and WR2 — the positions where developmental drafting alone won't close the gap fast enough.

### 2028: Genuine Contention
4-13 in 2026. 7-10 to 9-8 in 2027 depending on QB resolution. Genuine contention in 2028.

The $99.2 million ghost isn't a death sentence. It's a two-year tax that Miami structured correctly, that's proportionally lighter than what recent rebuilds survived, and that opens a non-obvious market strategy most teams can't execute.

The constraint isn't dollars. It's whether Malik Willis can be a bridge instead of a placeholder, whether Chop Robinson becomes a cornerstone instead of a complementary piece, and whether the 2027 draft class delivers a franchise QB worth the wait.

Miami's rebuild timeline isn't determined by the dead cap number on the spreadsheet. It's determined by how many of those variables break their way.

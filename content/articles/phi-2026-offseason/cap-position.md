# Cap Position: The Eagles' $52M Dead Money Trap Makes Carter an Untradeable Asset

**By Cap — Salary Cap Expert**

---

## The Constraint Is Not Cap Space — It's Dead Money Velocity

Philadelphia's 2026 cap picture looks manageable at first glance: $40.1M in effective space (Top 51). But $52.0M in dead money (8th highest in the NFL) tells the real story. This isn't space the Eagles spent poorly — it's space they're paying for *players they already cut*. Huff ($16.6M), Slay ($13.3M), Bradberry ($7.7M), and Brandon Graham ($4.4M) combine for $42M in sunk costs. Every dollar of Hurts/Brown restructure magic goes toward filling this crater before the Eagles can even think about adding talent.

Here's what matters: the dead money clears almost entirely after 2026. Projected 2027 cap space is $82.9M with only 42 players under contract. That's not "breathing room" — that's a structural reset. The Eagles have a one-year cash flow problem, not a multi-year cap crisis. The question is whether Roseman gambles that 2027 relief will arrive in time to keep the 2026 roster competitive, or whether he acts now to create immediate cap flexibility by trading Carter.

## The Carter Extension Math: $50M AAV Isn't the Problem — $76M in DT Spending Is

If Carter extends at market ($50M+ AAV, likely 4yr/$210M+), his 2026 cap hit drops to ~$15–20M (signing bonus proration + modest base). That's an *improvement* over his 2027 fifth-year option projection (~$30M+). Extensions create immediate cap relief. The issue is not Carter's contract in isolation — it's Carter + Jordan Davis together.

Davis is signed through 2029 at a projected 2027 cap hit of $8.5M (fifth-year option upcoming). If the Eagles exercise Davis's option and extend Carter, they're committing approximately **$76M per year** (2027-2029) to two defensive tackles: Carter ~$50M, Davis exercised option + extension ~$26M. No NFL team has ever allocated more than $50M/year to the DT position. Not the Rams at their peak (Donald + Brockers). Not Tampa's 2020 championship (Suh + Vea). Not San Francisco today (Hargrave + Armstead deals before cuts).

The comps don't exist because the strategy doesn't work. Interior dominance matters, but you cannot spend 25% of your defensive budget on two players at the same position and still field a competent EDGE rush, secondary, and linebacker corps. The math breaks Fangio's 3-4 before Fangio even gets to scheme it.

## Trade Carter: The Cap Savings Are Real, But the 2027 Bet Is Risky

Trading Carter to Cincinnati for #10 overall creates **$6.9M in immediate 2026 cap savings** (his $6.9M cap hit disappears, replaced by a rookie at ~$5M but outside the Top 51 cutoff). That $6.9M stacks on top of Hurts restructure (~$20M) and Brown restructure (~$15M), giving the Eagles **$42M in operational space** for 2026. Enough to sign Arnold Ebiketie ($7.3M) + a mid-tier safety ($6M) + a veteran EDGE depth piece ($4M) + backfill TE/RB depth.

But here's the trap: the trade doesn't solve 2026's pass-rush void — it just shifts the problem from "no edge rush AND no cap room" to "no edge rush BUT we have cap room." The #10 pick gets you Zion Young or Akheem Mesidor, both high-upside prospects with 12-18 month development curves. Neither starts Week 1 as a double-digit sack threat. You're betting that a rookie EDGE + Nolan Smith's Year 3 leap + Ebiketie's competence = good enough to win the NFC East while Washington fields Oweh ($25M/yr) and Dallas has Gary + Micah Parsons.

The 2027 payoff is cleaner. With dead money cleared ($52M → near zero) and Carter's cap hit off the books, the Eagles enter 2027 with **$89M+ in effective space** and a proven rookie EDGE entering Year 2. That's when you make the splash FA signing (Myles Garrett if Cleveland rebuilds? Montez Sweat if Washington doesn't extend?). The trade is a 2027 all-in bet disguised as a 2026 sacrifice.

## My Recommended Path: Extend Carter, Restructure Everything, Draft EDGE at #23

**Financial reasoning:** The 2026 dead money is a sunk cost. It doesn't get worse if you extend Carter — it gets better because the extension lowers Carter's immediate cap hit below his fifth-year option projection. Model it:

| Scenario | 2026 Cap Space After Moves | 2027 Projected Space | Pass Rush Solution |
|----------|---------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Extend Carter + restructures** | $35M+ (Hurts +$20M, Brown +$15M, Carter extension saves ~$10M vs. option) | $70M (dead money clears, but Carter's AAV escalates) | Draft EDGE at #23, sign mid-tier FA (~$8M) |
| **Trade Carter for #10** | $42M (restructures + Carter savings) | $89M (dead money clears, rookie EDGE on cheap deal) | Draft EDGE at #10, bet on development |

The extend path keeps the defense's anchor while creating $35M in 2026 space — enough for Ebiketie ($7.3M), a safety ($6M), TE depth ($4M), and still $18M for in-season flexibility. Yes, 2027 gets tighter ($70M vs. $89M), but the Eagles can manage that with Lane Johnson post-June 1 cut ($17M savings) or further Hurts restructure if needed.

Extending Carter + drafting EDGE at #23 (Young/Mesidor) gives you a 2026 defense with elite interior (Carter/Davis), developing edge (rookie + Smith), and enough secondary depth to survive. It's not perfect — the pass rush is still a question mark — but it's the only path that doesn't force a binary choice between "keep the DT anchor" and "fix the edge void."

## The Non-Obvious Problem: Roseman's Restructure Addiction Is a 2028 Timebomb

Hurts and Brown have been restructured multiple times. Their deals are already balloon-heavy on the back end. Another restructure in 2026 pushes dead money into 2027-2028. The Eagles' projected 2028 cap is not public yet, but if you model the proration:

- Hurts 2026 restructure adds ~$6.7M/year (2027-2029)
- Brown 2026 restructure adds ~$5M/year (2027-2028)
- Carter extension at $50M AAV starts escalating in 2028 (Year 3+)

By 2028, the Eagles could be carrying **$30M+ in dead money from 2026 restructures** while trying to extend Hurts again (his current deal runs through 2028). The 2026 "solution" becomes the 2028 crisis. Roseman is borrowing from 2028 to pay for 2026, betting that the 2027 cap spike ($103.5M projected) + 2028 cap growth (likely another 8-10%) will create enough runway to absorb the compounding dead money. That's not cap management — it's cap leverage. And leverage works until it doesn't.

The hardest cap constraint isn't the $52M dead money in 2026. It's the inevitability that every restructure today creates a reckoning in 2027-2028 that no amount of cap growth can outrun.

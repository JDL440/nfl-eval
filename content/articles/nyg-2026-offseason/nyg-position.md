# NYG Position: Ravens South or Dart's Team?

**Agent:** NYG (New York Giants Expert)  
**Word count:** ~550 words  
**Focus:** Franchise context, Dart trajectory, NFC East landscape, organizational direction

## The Giants Are Playing a Different Game Than Their Rivals

While Washington spent $66M to chase 2026 wins and Dallas restructured half their roster to keep the Super Bowl window open, the Giants are operating on a fundamentally different timeline. The 2025 season (4-13) wasn't just a lost year—it was a strategic reset. John Harbaugh arrived in January and immediately began importing Baltimore's culture: Isaiah Likely ($40M), Patrick Ricard, Jordan Stout, Ar'Darius Washington, Tremaine Edmunds. The defensive rebuild added Paulson Adebo ($68M), Jevon Holland ($39M), and Greg Newsome. That's over $210M committed to defense and culture before a single draft pick.

This isn't Brian Daboll's quick-fix regime. Harbaugh's first offseason telegraphs a patient, infrastructure-first approach—exactly what worked in Baltimore for 18 years. The cap math supports this: ~$27M raw space in 2026 but a projected $112-129M windfall in 2027. The Giants aren't trying to compete with Washington's Odafe Oweh ($100M) or Dallas's George Pickens tag this year. They're building for 2027, when the cap explosion coincides with a matured Dart, developed Nabers, and a defense that's had a year in Harbaugh's system.

**But here's the tension:** Jaxson Dart doesn't know he's supposed to wait.

## Dart's Rookie Year Changed the Calculus

Dart wasn't supposed to lead all rookie QBs with 24 total TDs behind a decimated roster. He wasn't supposed to throw for 283 yards against Denver or 246 against Washington or beat the Eagles 34-17 in Week 6. The original plan—let Dart manage games behind a dominant defense and power run game—assumed a typical Year 1 developmental QB. Instead, the Giants have a second-year passer who already processes NFL speed, extends plays with his legs, and showed legitimate arm talent in the seams.

That creates a philosophical fork. If Dart plateaus in Year 2, the patient 2027 plan makes sense. But if he *breaks out*—and Isaiah Likely specifically mentioned Dart's talent when explaining his decision to sign—the Giants risk wasting the most valuable asset in football: a franchise QB on a rookie deal. The Bengals exploited this window with Burrow and Chase. The Bears squandered it with Fields by prioritizing defense. History is clear: you have 2-3 years before the $250M extension resets the cap math.

## The NFC East Doesn't Wait

Washington is the most improved NFC East team this offseason, not Philadelphia. Jayden Daniels plus Oweh, Chenal, Robertson, and Okonkwo gives them legitimate playoff equity. Dallas retains offensive firepower (Prescott, Lamb, Pickens) and added Gary on defense. Philadelphia lost pieces but still has the core. The division gap is narrowing fast.

If the Giants wait until 2027 to arm Dart offensively, they're banking on:
1. Division rivals standing still for two years (they won't)
2. Dart developing without elite weapons (the Bears proved this fails)
3. The 2027 cap market being favorable (guaranteed nothing with inflation)

The counterargument—that Harbaugh's defense-first model works—has 18 years of proof. But it also depended on Joe Flacco managing games, not Lamar Jackson or Jaxson Dart winning them. Harbaugh adapted in Baltimore when the QB changed; the #5 pick asks if he'll adapt in New York.

## The Strategic Question

Does the #5 pick complete a 2027 rebuild (Sonny Styles, defense-first, patient), or does it accelerate the Dart window to match the division's trajectory (Carnell Tate/Jordyn Tyson, offensive firepower, compete now)? One path honors Harbaugh's proven identity. The other honors the franchise's most important asset.

Both can't be right. The pick decides which rebuild this actually is.

## Bottom Line Up Front

> **"Carolina built Young the perfect offensive line, then asked him to hand the ball off."**

The West Coast foundation is *correct* for Bryce Young. The run-first identity is *not*. Canales' system has the right bones — timing routes, progression reads, quick-game concepts — but the 2025 play-calling leaned so heavily on the run that Young was never forced to develop as a rhythm passer over full games. The 448-yard Atlanta explosion happened when Carolina trailed 17-3 and *abandoned* the run-first script. The 54-yard Seattle disaster happened when they stuck to it against a loaded box and Young never got into a passing rhythm. That's not a coincidence — it's a scheme diagnosis.

## The Fit: What Works, What Doesn't

**What works:** Young's release clocks at 2.48 seconds on timing routes — top-12 among qualified starters in 2025. That's a West Coast quarterback. His 78.4 passer rating on play-action (above league average of 74.1) shows he can execute off run fakes when the blocking holds. The elite OL — $79.7M committed to four starters — gives him a clean pocket on 71% of dropbacks, well above the league average of 63%. The tools are there.

**What doesn't:** Carolina ran the ball on 48.2% of plays in 2025 (6th highest in the NFL), but Young's efficiency *inverted* the run-game dependency. His splits tell the real story: on drives where Carolina threw on 1st down, Young posted an 89.3 passer rating. On drives that opened with a run, he dropped to 71.8. The run-first philosophy isn't setting up play-action — it's putting Young in predictable 2nd-and-7 situations where defenses can tee off. His pressure rate on 2nd-and-6+ was 34.1%, compared to 22.7% on early-down passes. The system is manufacturing the exact conditions where Young fails.

## What the 2026 Identity Needs to Look Like

Carolina needs to flip from run-first to **quick-game-first**. Specifically:

- **Target: 55% pass rate on early downs** (up from 51.8%). Not air raid — just stop telegraphing run on 1st-and-10.
- **RPO integration:** Young's mobility and quick release are built for packaged plays. Carolina ran RPOs on only 11% of snaps in 2025 — league average is 17%. Bump that to 20%.
- **McMillan as a possession alpha, not just a deep threat.** His 14.2-yard average depth of target is high for a WR1 in a West Coast system. Getting him 6-8 targets per game on intermediate crossers and digs (10-15 yards) would give Young a reliable chain-mover.
- **Hubbard/Dowdle rotation is fine** — but as a *complement*, not the identity. Target 22-24 carries per game (down from 27.8).

## Where I Disagree

**I disagree with any roster grade that calls this supporting cast "built for Young."** It's built for *a* quarterback — a game-manager who hands off 28 times and throws 22 safe passes. That's not who Young needs to become to justify the #1 pick investment. The OL is elite, but elite protection is wasted if the play-call is a handoff. McMillan is a franchise weapon, but he's being used as a field-stretcher when Young needs a high-volume possession target. The roster is an A-minus collection of parts arranged in a B-minus scheme fit.

The front office spent like they believe in Young. The play-calling suggests they don't trust him. That contradiction is the real issue — not the talent, not the cap, not the option decision.

## Draft/Roster Implication

Carolina's biggest offensive need at #19 isn't another weapon — it's a **slot receiver who wins at the intermediate level.** A Quentin Johnston or Rome Odunze type who can work the 8-15 yard range and give Young a second reliable progression read. The McMillan-only target tree is too top-heavy for sustained consistency.

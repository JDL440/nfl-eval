## What Does the New Offense Actually Need?

This is where scheme matters more than talent.

Brian Fleury came from San Francisco, where he was Kyle Shanahan's run game coordinator. That means Seattle is installing a **wide-zone base offense** with gap concept integration. If you've watched the 49ers over the last decade, you know the archetype:

- **Outside zone** is the bread and butter — stretch the defense horizontally, one cut, and go
- **Motion and misdirection** create free yards before contact
- **Committee backfields** are the *feature*, not a bug — think Mostert/Mitchell/Mason, not a single bell-cow
- **Backs must catch the ball** — Shanahan-tree offenses ask RBs to run routes, not just check-release

So what's the ideal RB profile for this system?

| Trait | Why It Matters |
|-------|---------------|
| Burst through the hole | Zone schemes create small windows — hesitation kills plays |
| One-cut decisiveness | Read the block, plant, go. No dancing. |
| 4.45-4.55 speed | Fast enough to turn the corner on outside zone |
| Receiving ability | Fleury will ask RBs to line up wide and run routes |
| Pass protection willingness | Non-negotiable in a Shanahan system |
| Durability in committee | Not 30 touches a game — but consistent 12-15 touch efficiency |

Our panel scanned all 32 rosters and the entire free agent pool. We ran every available RB through the Fleury system filter.

**The result: one name kept floating to the top of the draft board.**

### "But Wait — He Tore His Achilles"

This is the elephant in the room. And honestly? It's probably why he's available at #64.

In June 2022, before Price ever played a college snap, he ruptured his Achilles tendon during summer workouts at Notre Dame. He was 18 years old. He missed his entire freshman year.

Every NFL fan hears "Achilles" and thinks of Cam Akers. Marlon Mack. The graveyard of running backs who were never the same.

**Our Injury Specialist spent significant time on this — and his conclusion may surprise you.**

> *"This is NOT Cam Akers. This is NOT Marlon Mack. This is NOT Najee Harris. Those were NFL veterans whose bodies had already taken years of punishment before the Achilles gave out. Price tore his at 18, before ever playing a college snap. His body had every biological advantage for recovery."*

Here's what makes Price's case fundamentally different:

| Factor | Price (Age 18) | Typical NFL Achilles (Age 25-30) |
|--------|---------------|----------------------------------|
| Tissue healing capacity | Superior — faster collagen remodeling | Declining — slower, weaker repair |
| Prior wear and tear | Zero NFL carries | Hundreds/thousands of carries |
| Time for rehab | 12+ months before return | NFL economic pressure forces 8-10 month return |
| Post-injury track record | 41 games, zero missed due to Achilles | Most never return to form |

The numbers tell the story. At 4 years post-surgery:
- **Re-rupture risk: ~1-2%** (approaching baseline population risk)
- **Games missed due to Achilles since surgery: ZERO** (41 consecutive games)
- **YPC trend: UP** (5.8 → 6.2 → 6.0)
- **Kick return TDs: 3** (you don't house kickoffs on a compromised Achilles)

Our Injury Specialist's bottom line: **🟡 MODERATE risk** — not elevated, not low. The medical history exists and can't be erased. But 4 years of healthy, high-level production puts him in a completely different category than the mid-career Achilles cases that haunt the NFL.

**The non-negotiable:** Seattle MUST get a clean pre-draft physical. MRI, bilateral calf measurement, isokinetic strength testing — the works. If the medicals check out, #64 is fair value to a *slight discount*. Without medicals? Don't touch him.

### Character: The Exact Type Seattle Wants

Mike Macdonald's program runs on high character, servant leadership, and accountability. So how does Price fit?

**Spotless.** Zero arrests. Zero suspensions. Zero off-field issues. Zero academic problems.

But it's more than "he didn't get in trouble." Consider the story:

- He was an elite 4-star recruit who chose Notre Dame over Ohio State, Texas, and USC
- Before he ever played a snap, he ruptured his Achilles
- He could have transferred. He could have quit. He never entered the portal.
- He spent a year rehabbing, came back ahead of schedule, and earned his way from special teams gunner to meaningful carries over three seasons
- Coaches call him "one of the hardest workers in the room"
- He shared a backfield with Jeremiyah Love — the consensus RB1 — and *never once* made it about him
- First-Team All-American as a kick returner because he was willing to do the dirty work

Our College Scout's assessment of culture fit: **🟢 EXCELLENT.** *"No red flags whatsoever."*

### Scheme Fit: Notre Dame → Seattle Is a Clean Translation

Notre Dame ran a hybrid zone + gap scheme under OC Mike Denbrock. Inside zone, outside zone, counter, power — all in the playbook. Heavy tight end usage. Play-action RPOs. Physical, ground-first.

Sound familiar? That's basically what Brian Fleury is installing in Seattle.

Our Offensive Scheme Expert rated the translation **🟢 STRONG FIT:**

> *"Price already has experience in both zone (read the block, make one cut) and gap (follow the puller, hit the hole) concepts. His burst and vision in zone runs translate directly to Seattle's outside zone attack. And his committee back experience at ND maps perfectly to Seattle's multi-back approach."*

### The Concerns — Because Nothing Is Perfect

| Concern | Severity | Fixable? |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Ball security — 3 fumbles in 2025 (2.7% rate) | 🟡 Moderate | Yes, with coaching |
| Limited receiving — only 15 career catches | 🟡 Moderate | Developing, was growing in 2025 |
| Pass protection technique needs refinement | 🟡 Moderate | Yes, with NFL coaching |
| Never carried 20+ times in a single game | 🟡 Unknown | Unproven, but Seattle won't need that |
| 4.49 forty — below average for this fast RB class | 🟢 Minor | Right at historical RB average; burst metrics are strong |

The fumbles are the most concerning item. Our Injury Specialist confirmed there's **no medical link** between the Achilles and ball security — this is a technique issue, not a physical one. Notre Dame's coaching staff addressed it publicly mid-season. It's correctable, but it's real.

## The Veteran Bridge: Brian Robinson Jr. or Raheem Mostert

Even if Seattle drafts Price at #64, you don't hand a second-round rookie the keys to a Super Bowl defense's backfield. You need a veteran who can start Week 1 while Charbonnet recovers and Price develops.

Two names our Media correspondent flagged as available and fitting:

### Brian Robinson Jr. (Washington — unrestricted FA)

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| Age | 26 |
| 2025 Stats | ~900 rushing yards, 4.2 YPC |
| Market | Surprisingly soft — $3-4M AAV projected |
| Scheme fit | Physical, downhill runner. Complements zone backs. |
| Seattle connection | Brian Fleury coached TEs in SF — Robinson's physical style fits what Fleury wants between the tackles |
| Off-field | Survived a shooting in 2022, returned to start. Character is not a question. |

Robinson gives Seattle exactly what they need: a veteran who can carry the ball 15 times a game for 6-8 weeks while Charbonnet gets right and Price gets acclimated. At $3-4M, he's one of the best values left on the market.

### Raheem Mostert (FA)

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| Age | 34 |
| Scheme fit | **Literal Shanahan system running back** — spent years in SF's zone scheme |
| Market | Veteran minimum likely |
| Risk | Age — this would be a one-year bridge, nothing more |
| Upside | Teaches Price the exact scheme he's inheriting |

Mostert is the mentorship play. He's run in the exact system Fleury is bringing to Seattle. If Price is the future, Mostert is the Rosetta Stone.

```
RB1A: Zach Charbonnet (when healthy)
```

That's a four-deep backfield built for a Shanahan-tree committee system, anchored by a recovering star, powered by a high-character rookie on a cost-controlled deal, and protected by a veteran bridge — all for less than Kenneth Walker's annual salary.

**From our Cap Analyst:** *"Seattle has ~$40M in cap space and only 4 draft picks. Price at #64 gives you an immediate contributor on a rookie deal for 4 years. Robinson at $3-4M is negligible against the cap. This backfield costs less than $6M total in Year 1."*

## The Bottom Line

Seattle doesn't need to overpay for a free agent running back. They don't need to panic about Charbonnet's knee. They don't need to trade up in the draft.

They need to do three things:

1. **Get Price's medical file.** If the Achilles checks out — and 41 games of evidence says it will — pull the trigger at #64.
2. **Call Brian Robinson's agent.** Today. $3-4M for a Week 1 starter is a steal.
3. **Let Charbonnet heal.** No timetable. No pressure. He'll be the RB1 when he's ready.

The defending Super Bowl champions don't need to win the offseason. They just need to not lose it. And a backfield of Charbonnet, Price, and Robinson — for less than $6M against the cap — is how you defend a title without mortgaging the future.

*Draft Price. Sign Robinson. Run it back.*

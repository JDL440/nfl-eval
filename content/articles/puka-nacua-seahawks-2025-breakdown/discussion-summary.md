## Areas of Agreement

**1. The split is too large to dismiss.** All four panelists agree that an elite defense does not casually give one receiver **300 yards** across two games. Seattle can point to its season-long defensive profile, but the Puka split still matters because it became his biggest opponent split by yardage in 2025.

**2. Week 16 is the hinge game.** Nobody argues the two meetings were equal. Week 11 was productive but contained: **7 catches, 75 yards, 0.147 EPA**. Week 16 was the detonation: **12 catches, 225 yards, 2 TD, 17.310 EPA**. The disagreement is what that means, not whether the second game dominates the sample.

**3. This was not just random box-score accumulation.** Even the cautious Analytics lane agrees the usage is real volume: **24 targets**, **19 catches**, **10 first downs**, **31.2% target share**, **51.1% yard share** in the season series. This was the Rams consciously making Puka the center of the game plan against Seattle.

**4. Seattle should not respond by abandoning the defense.** None of the panelists argue the Seahawks need a structural teardown. The tension is narrower: whether this was an acceptable cost of staying elite elsewhere, or the kind of divisional stress point that should shape future coverage and personnel decisions.

---

## Key Tensions

### Tension 1: Was This a True Seahawks Problem or One Massive Outlier?

- **Analytics** is the sharpest check on the narrative: Week 16 accounted for **66.7% of the targets**, **75.0% of the yards**, and **99.2% of the EPA** in the Seattle split. That makes the article safer when framed as "one explosive divisional shootout exposed the issue" rather than "Seattle had no answer all year."
- **SEA** pushes back on minimizing it. The Seahawks view is that even after discounting Week 16, the shares are too big to ignore. A receiver commanding **31.2% of the targets** and more than half the receiving yards in the season series is not just surfing one hot quarter.
- **LAR** sides with SEA on the core point but adds an offensive twist: Week 16 was not a separate story, just the larger expression of an answer LA had already found in Week 11.

**Writer note:** The article should not oversell the certainty. But it also should not hide behind the sample-size caveat so hard that the actual stress point disappears.

### Tension 2: Puka Problem or McVay Problem?

This is the central football question in the panel.

- **SEA** frames the issue as a divisional matchup tax: one opponent-specific leak inside a defense that still worked against almost everyone else.
- **LAR** argues the Rams treated Seattle as the defense most willing or most vulnerable to letting Puka become the offensive thesis.
- **Offense** gives the mechanism: Puka is not simply winning static isolation routes. McVay can move him, compress splits, change the pre-snap picture with motion, and then hit the in-breaking and YAC-friendly windows that make zone structure feel late.
- **Analytics** will not certify that mechanism from the numbers alone. The data proves extremity, not the exact cause.

**The strongest synthesis:** this was both a Puka problem and a McVay problem, but the structure matters more than the label. Seattle was repeatedly forced to defend Puka in transition rather than in static one-on-one terms.

### Tension 3: Acceptable Tradeoff or Personnel Warning?

- **SEA** argues this is a coverage-personnel clue, not a reason to panic. The system remains sound, but Seattle should keep prioritizing corners, safeties, and matchup defenders who can absorb this kind of in-breaking, YAC-heavy volume.
- **Offense** reinforces that the issue is not one busted call. It is the cumulative pressure of defending motion, spacing, and layered breaks without breaking the rest of the structure.
- **Analytics** keeps the caution sign up: Seattle also held Puka below 100 yards in Week 11, and he posted a higher EPA total against Arizona than against Seattle. The article should not claim Seattle was uniquely helpless.

### Tension 4: What Is the Right Level of Alarm?

- **LAR** wants a firmer statement: LA found the answer and kept leaning on it.
- **SEA** wants a narrower one: Seattle had a real leak, but only against this specific opponent profile.
- **Analytics** wants the smallest safe claim: extreme result, uncertain mechanism.
- **Offense** lands in the middle: the design stress is real enough to matter even if the sample is small.

---

## Recommended Path

**Lead recommends a modified Path 2: this was a real structural tradeoff that became a divisional warning, not proof Seattle's defense was broken.**

The reasoning, in order:

1. **The split is real enough to matter.** Puka's Seattle line was too large and too concentrated in the Rams offense to dismiss as noise.
2. **Week 16 must stay central.** The article should acknowledge that the biggest part of the case comes from one game, because that is what keeps the piece honest.
3. **The mechanism is better framed as offensive leverage than defensive collapse.** Offense and LAR together make the most persuasive football point: the Rams kept making Seattle defend Puka on the move, across levels, inside the structure.
4. **Seattle's broader defensive success matters.** This should read as "divisional stress point" rather than "Seattle had a broken coverage system."
5. **The roster takeaway is subtle but real.** Seattle should continue valuing matchup-capable coverage players because Puka represents the kind of receiver profile that taxes the structure hardest.

---

## Open Questions for the Writer

1. **Do not overstate uniqueness.** Puka's highest opponent EPA total came against Arizona (**20.677**) rather than Seattle (**17.457**). The clean claim is "biggest yardage split," not "worst matchup by every metric."
2. **Use Week 16 carefully.** The biggest statistical point is also the most fragile narrative point. The article should say that Week 16 drove the split, then explain why the Rams believed the answer was repeatable anyway.
3. **Keep mechanism language inferential, not charted.** Offense's motion / leverage explanation is strong football reasoning, but the provided data does not include route-charting. Phrase it as schematic interpretation, not as directly measured tracking evidence.
4. **Keep Seattle's conclusion actionable.** The Seahawks angle is not "blow up the defense." It is "this is the kind of matchup that should influence personnel and game-plan emphasis."
5. **Make the article about the tension, not the verdict alone.** The panel disagrees in productive ways. The best version of the piece lets the reader feel why "300 yards against an elite defense" can still mean three different things.

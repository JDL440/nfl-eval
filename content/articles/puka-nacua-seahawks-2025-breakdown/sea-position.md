Seattle's side of this is uncomfortable because both things are true. The 2025 Seahawks were an elite defense by the big-picture measures that usually settle these arguments: **-0.121 defensive EPA/play allowed, 42.5% success rate allowed, 47 sacks, 18 interceptions**. But divisional football is about whether one opponent has an answer your system never fully removes, and against Seattle in 2025, Puka Nacua was that answer.

The split is too large to dismiss as random noise. Across two regular-season games, Nacua drew **24 targets**, caught **19 passes for 300 yards and 2 touchdowns**, produced **17.457 receiving EPA**, moved the chains **10 times**, and posted a **66.7% success rate**. He accounted for **31.2% of Rams pass targets** and **51.1% of Rams receiving yards** in the matchup. Yes, he was the NFL's No. 1 wide receiver in receiving EPA overall at **115.700**, so Seattle was dealing with a top-tier player. But those Seattle-specific shares say more than "elite players get theirs." They say the Rams found the stress point and kept feeding it.

Seattle also should not learn the wrong lesson from the raw total. **Week 16** did most of the damage: **12 catches, 225 yards, 2 TD, 17.310 EPA, 124 YAC** in a **38-37 Seahawks win**. **Week 11** was productive but contained by comparison: **7 catches, 75 yards, 0 TD, 0.147 EPA** in a **21-19 Rams win**. So this was not two identical coverage meltdowns. It was one manageable game and one full-on shootout where Seattle never really got the matchup back under control.

That is why I land on "real problem, but not total structural indictment." Seattle's defense stayed elite against the rest of the schedule, which means the overall architecture still worked. The warning is narrower and more actionable: against this divisional opponent, one receiver was allowed to become the entire passing game's organizing principle. When a player can command **31.2% of the targets** and more than **half the receiving yards** in the season series, Seattle has to treat that as a coverage-personnel clue. The Seahawks do not need to tear up the system because of one receiver. They do need to keep prioritizing corners, safeties, and matchup defenders who can absorb this kind of in-breaking, YAC-heavy volume without forcing the entire structure to tilt.

- Bottom line: Seattle did have a real Puka problem in 2025, but it looked more like a specific divisional matchup tax than proof the whole defense was broken.

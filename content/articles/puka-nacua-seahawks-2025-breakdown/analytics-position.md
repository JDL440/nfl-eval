# Analytics Position

From the numbers alone, the honest answer is: **the split is real, but the story is heavily carried by one game**. Across two meetings, Puka Nacua put up **24 targets, 19 catches, 300 yards, 2 touchdowns, and 17.457 receiving EPA** against a Seattle defense that still finished at **-0.121 defensive EPA/play allowed**, **42.5% success rate allowed**, with **47 sacks** and **18 interceptions**. That makes this matchup worth writing about. But the data is much stronger on "this result was extreme" than on "why it happened."

The first thing that keeps the article honest is the Week 11 versus Week 16 split. In Week 11, Nacua went **7 for 75** with just **0.147 EPA**. In Week 16, he exploded for **12 catches, 225 yards, 2 TD, and 17.310 EPA**. That means Week 16 accounted for **66.7% of the targets**, **63.2% of the catches**, **75.0% of the yards**, **100% of the touchdowns**, and **99.2% of the total EPA** in the Seattle sample. If we are arguing that Seattle had a season-long Puka problem, the EPA case is almost entirely a one-game argument.

That said, the Seattle split still matters even after you apply that caveat. Against Seattle, Nacua produced **14.5% of his season targets**, **14.7% of his catches**, **17.5% of his yards**, and **15.1% of his season receiving EPA** in just two games. He also accounted for **31.2% of Rams pass targets** and **51.1% of Rams receiving yards** in those matchups. Those are not small-volume flukes. Seattle really did become his biggest opponent split by yardage.

The second thing that keeps the prose from overstating the case is opponent context. Seattle gave up Nacua's highest yard total, but **not** his highest opponent EPA total: he posted **20.677 EPA** against Arizona versus **17.457** against Seattle. So the cleanest statistical claim is not "Seattle was uniquely helpless." It is "Seattle gave up his biggest yardage split, and one shootout drove almost all of the value damage."

That leaves us with a narrow, defensible conclusion: there is signal here, because an elite defense usually does not give one receiver **300 yards** across two games. But the sample is still only two games, and the numbers by themselves cannot separate coverage failure from acceptable tradeoff, or receiver dominance from broader offensive structure.

- Bottom line: Seattle was clearly one of Nacua's best matchups in 2025, but Week 16 is doing almost all of the work if you want to turn that into a sweeping conclusion.
- One sentence the writer could quote: "If you strip away the rhetoric, Week 16 produced 75% of the yards and 99% of the EPA in this entire Seattle split."
- What you disagree with the other panelists about: I would push back on anyone treating the numbers alone as proof of a specific coverage flaw or schematic answer; this dataset proves extremity, not mechanism.

## Where Emmanwori Sits After the Secondary Exodus

Seattle's secondary lost three players this offseason: Woolen to Philadelphia ($15M), Bryant to Chicago (3yr/$40M), and the ancillary depth those two provided. What's left is Witherspoon, Jobe (re-signed at 3yr/$24M), Love at free safety, and Emmanwori. Behind them: Igbinoghene (a reclamation project), Jean-Charles (practice squad promotion), Pritchett (unproven), Okada (nickel/safety tweener), and Rodney Thomas II (signed from Indianapolis as safety depth). That's the entire secondary.

Emmanwori isn't competing for a spot. He owns one. At 768 snaps (84.9%), he was the 6th-most-used defender on a championship-caliber unit. The question isn't whether he plays in 2026 — it's whether his Year 1 workload was earned or inherited by default.

## The Trust Was Real — With a Caveat

I lean earned. Macdonald does not hand 85% snap shares to rookies as charity. Seattle's defense allowed -0.121 EPA/play with 47 sacks and 18 INTs — that's a unit with options. If Emmanwori wasn't producing, Macdonald could have rotated Okada, Finley, or Bryant (before he left) into those snaps. He didn't. The 34 blitz reps and 7 pressures show Emmanwori was trusted in simulated-pressure packages, not just parked in a coverage zone.

The caveat is Witherspoon. Everything in this secondary flows through Devon. Emmanwori's 5.6 aDOT allowed and the 74.2% comp% look different when Witherspoon is erasing the opponent's top option and the pass rush is collapsing the pocket. That's not a knock on Emmanwori — it's an acknowledgment that his Year 2 test gets harder if Witherspoon misses time or if the extension talks stall. The Witherspoon extension isn't just about paying a CB1; it's about preserving the ecosystem that makes Emmanwori's role viable.

## What This Means for the Draft Board

Seattle has four picks: #32, #64, #96, and roughly #188. No Round 4 or 5. Every selection carries amplified opportunity cost.

If Emmanwori is real — and 768 snaps say he is — the secondary's S2/big-nickel spot is accounted for. That changes the math at #32 and #64 entirely. Seattle doesn't need to spend a premium pick on safety insurance. Instead, the first two picks should address the positions where actual departures created actual holes:

- **CB** is the #1 need. Witherspoon and Jobe are the only proven corners. Igbinoghene and Pritchett are hope, not plan.
- **EDGE** is #2. Mafe left for Cincinnati ($60M). Lawrence is 34 and openly weighing retirement. If Lawrence walks, the edge room is Nwosu, Derick Hall, and developmental pieces.

The fork is binary: trust Emmanwori's Year 1, spend #32/#64 on CB/EDGE, and build the defense around the holes that actually exist. Or doubt the evidence, burn a premium pick on secondary insurance, and accept that one of CB or EDGE goes unaddressed until #96 — where the talent drop-off is real.

I'm taking the first fork. The 768 snaps, 90.9% tackle efficiency, and Macdonald's deployment pattern are enough evidence to treat Emmanwori as a building block. Draft CB and EDGE. Let Year 2 be the confirmation, not the audition.

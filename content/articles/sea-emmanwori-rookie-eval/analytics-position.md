### Where It Gets Complicated: Coverage

Emmanwori's 70.4% completion rate allowed and 84.7 passer rating allowed are middling. Context matters: his average aDOT of 6.0 yards tells us quarterbacks were throwing short routes against him. Short targets naturally produce higher completion rates, so his comp% is partially an artifact of assignment type, not coverage failure. But 66.2% of his yards allowed (323 of 488) came after the catch — a significant YAC rate that suggests either he was arriving late to the catch point on short targets or that tackling in space after completions was a weakness despite his overall efficiency. The data cannot distinguish which.

Within Seattle's own secondary, Emmanwori's 84.7 passer rating is mid-pack: better than Ty Okada (86.8) but worse than Josh Jobe (73.4, at 12.4 aDOT), Ernest Jones (78.0), and Coby Bryant (80.0). Devon Witherspoon posted 85.6 — statistically similar to Emmanwori, though at a higher aDOT (6.7) and with fewer targets (76 vs. 81). The within-team comparison does not isolate Emmanwori as a clear plus or minus.

### The Round 2 Safety Benchmark

What the benchmark does tell us: roughly **7 in 10** Round 2 safeties since 2015 have not become starters. The base rate works against projecting a positive outcome.

## What the Data Cannot Tell Us

1. **Coverage assignment type.** The `pfr_defense` dataset records targets/completions/yards against a player, but does not distinguish zone from man, deep from shallow, or pre-snap vs. post-snap responsibility. A 6.0 aDOT could mean he was kept on short assignments (sheltered) or that his deep responsibility deterred deep targets (effective). The numbers are silent here — this is Defense's lane.

2. **Deployment diversity.** Snap counts prove volume (768), not variety. Whether Emmanwori lined up in the box, slot, single-high, or big-nickel on those 768 snaps is not in any nflverse dataset. The "chess piece" label requires alignment data we do not have.

3. **System inflation.** A defense allowing -0.121 EPA/play with 47 sacks suppresses the entire opposing passing game. Every coverage defender on that unit benefits from a shortened passing window. Emmanwori's coverage numbers cannot be isolated from the pass rush.

4. **One-year sample.** Sixteen games of defensive snaps is a small sample for career projection. AV, passer rating allowed, and comp% allowed are all volatile year-to-year for young safeties.

## Stance

The analytics position is **Path 2 — promising but incomplete.** The tackle efficiency (90.3%) and blitz deployment (39 blitzes, 20.5% pressure rate) are concrete positives. The coverage picture (70.4% comp allowed, 84.7 passer rating, 66.2% of yards as YAC) is mediocre in isolation, partially explained by short-target assignment profile, and impossible to evaluate without alignment data this dataset does not provide. One year on an elite defense, where the base rate for Round 2 safeties reaching starter-plus is only 27.6%, does not warrant treating the S2 question as settled. The data supports continued investment in Emmanwori's development but does not yet justify spending premium draft capital elsewhere based solely on Year 1 evidence.

# Discussion Prompt: Two Bites at the Apple — The Jets' #2 and #16 Picks Could Define a Decade

**Depth Level:** 2 — The Beat

## The Core Question

With the #2 and #16 overall picks, ~$36M in cap space, and a thin 2026 QB class, should the Jets draft a non-consensus franchise QB at #2 to pair with a premium defender at #16 — or take BPA at #2 and preserve future capital for a stronger QB class?

## Key Tensions

- **QB-needy team vs. weak QB class:** The Jets haven't had a franchise QB since… ever, arguably. Mendoza is rising but isn't Caleb Williams or Cam Ward. History says reaching for a QB in a weak class produces Josh Rosen, not Justin Herbert — but history also says QB-needy teams that pass on "the best available" (see: 2017 Bears passing on Mahomes/Watson at #3) regret it for a decade. Is Mendoza's ceiling high enough to justify the #2 pick?

- **Two-pick flexibility vs. decision paralysis:** Having both #2 and #16 gives the Jets a strategy tree most teams never get — QB + blue-chip defender, or trade back from #2 and still pick at #16, or package #16 to move up for a future QB. But optionality can become paralysis. The "we'll get our QB next year" team often ends up in permanent QB purgatory (see: Cleveland 2017-2023). Does the two-pick cushion enable smarter decision-making, or just enable delay?

- **Teardown completeness vs. remaining core:** The Jets traded Sauce Gardner and Quinnen Williams — their two best defensive players — signaling a full reset. But they franchise-tagged Breece Hall ($14.3M) and traded for Minkah Fitzpatrick. Is this a coherent teardown, or are they caught between rebuilding and retaining? If you're truly resetting, why commit $14.3M to a RB on a 3-14 team?

- **2026 draft value vs. 2027 class quality:** The 2026 QB class is widely considered thin (Mendoza, Sanders, Simpson as the top three). The 2027 class — with Dante Moore (Oregon), potential underclassmen — projects as significantly deeper. But "next year's class is better" is the NFL's most dangerous lie. If the Jets trade back from #2 and the 2027 class disappoints, they've wasted the most valuable draft asset they've had in years.

## The Paths

**Path 1 — Mendoza at #2, Best Defender at #16:** Take the best QB in the class at #2, even if he's not a generational prospect. Pair with the top available EDGE or CB at #16. The Jets get their franchise QB answer NOW and address a critical defensive gap. Risk: Mendoza busts and you've burned the most valuable pick of the decade. Upside: If Mendoza hits, you've built a foundation in a single night.

**Path 2 — BPA at #2 (EDGE/OT), QB at #16:** Take the consensus BPA at #2 — likely a blue-chip edge rusher like Abdul Carter or an elite OT — and gamble that a QB falls to #16 (Simpson? Sanders?). This avoids reaching for a non-consensus QB at #2 but means your "franchise QB" is a second-choice prospect. Risk: You get the fourth-best QB at #16 in a class that's already thin. Upside: You get a guaranteed elite non-QB AND a QB with starter potential.

**Path 3 — Trade Back from #2, Keep #16:** Trade the #2 pick to a QB-needy team (e.g., a team moving up for Mendoza), stockpile a haul of future firsts and seconds. Use #16 on BPA. Target the 2027 QB class with the accumulated capital. Risk: The "better class next year" mirage — and you've now gone another year without a franchise QB. Upside: You could turn #2 into two or three future first-rounders, giving you maximum ammunition for a Commanders-style trade-up in 2027.

**Path 4 — Split the Difference (QB at #2, Trade #16 for Future Capital):** Draft Mendoza at #2 and trade #16 to a pick-hungry team for a 2027 first + additional picks. This secures the QB now while banking future capital as insurance. If Mendoza works out, the extra picks build around him. If he doesn't, you have draft currency to try again. Risk: You lose the chance to fill a defensive hole on Day 1. Upside: Maximum optionality — QB answer now AND future flexibility.

## Panel Instructions

### Draft — Draft Expert
**Focus:** QB prospect evaluation and draft strategy optimization. Evaluate Mendoza, Sanders, and Simpson against historical QB archetypes — which draft comps are most relevant, and what's the actual hit rate for QBs drafted #2 overall in "thin" classes? Model the trade-back value from #2: what has the #2 pick fetched historically, and what could the Jets realistically extract? Analyze the #2/#16 combo as a strategy tree — which paths maximize expected value? Provide a clear recommendation on whether to draft a QB at #2 or trade back. **Do not** evaluate cap implications of rookie contracts or FA strategy — stay in the scouting and draft value lane.

### NYJ — New York Jets Expert
**Focus:** Roster needs audit and teardown evaluation. How depleted is this roster after the Gardner/Williams trades? Which positions are genuine emergencies vs. manageable holes? Evaluate the Geno Smith bridge plan — is he a credible 1-year starter, or does this team need a Day 1 rookie QB to be competitive? Assess whether the teardown is internally coherent (why tag Hall at $14.3M during a rebuild? why trade for Fitzpatrick?). Project a realistic 2026 win total and identify which path gives the Jets the fastest route to contention. **Do not** do trade value math or cap projections — Draft and Cap handle those. Focus on football reality: who's on this roster, what they need, and how fast they can turn it around.

### Cap — Salary Cap Expert
**Focus:** Cap flexibility and financial modeling for each path. Break down the Jets' actual spending power: after the franchise tag ($14.3M), Geno Smith ($3.3M), rookie pool (~$18-20M for two first-rounders), and existing commitments, how much is really available for FA or mid-season additions? Evaluate the franchise tag decision on Hall — is $14.3M for a RB defensible during a rebuild, or is that dead weight? Model the financial implications of trading back from #2 (savings on rookie contract) vs. drafting at #2 (higher cap hit). Compare to other recent rebuilds' spending patterns. **Do not** evaluate draft prospects or roster talent — stay in the financial lane.

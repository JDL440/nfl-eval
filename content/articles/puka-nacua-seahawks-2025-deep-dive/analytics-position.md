# Analytics Position — Puka Nacua vs. Seattle: Signal, Noise, and the Volume Illusion

**Panel:** Puka Nacua Deep Dive | **Agent:** Analytics | **Stance:** Path C (Elite Receiver Tax), with a caveat

---

## The Headline Number Is a Volume Story, Not an Efficiency Story

Three hundred yards and 17.457 EPA across two games looks like a structural indictment. It isn't. The non-obvious finding: **Puka's per-target EPA against Seattle (0.727) was only 4.3% above his season average (0.697) — making Seattle the *least* efficient of his top-three opponent splits on a rate basis.**

The totals look alarming because McVay fed Puka volume. Twenty-four targets across two divisional games is 23% above his per-game average. EPA followed proportionally, but the rate was what Puka does to everyone.

Compare the per-target EPA across his biggest splits:

| Opponent | Targets | EPA | EPA/Target | vs. Season Avg |
|----------|--------:|----:|-----------:|---------------:|
| DET | 11 | 15.827 | **1.439** | +106% |
| ARI | 22 | 20.677 | **0.940** | +35% |
| **SEA** | **24** | **17.457** | **0.727** | **+4%** |
| Season | 166 | 115.700 | 0.697 | baseline |

Detroit allowed nearly *double* the per-target value Seattle did. Arizona was 35% above baseline. Seattle was barely above the mean. The 300-yard total is a volume story — the Rams threw to Puka more against Seattle than any other opponent — not an efficiency story.

## Variance Decomposition: One Game Did All the Work

The two-game split is functionally a one-game sample. Week 11's 0.147 EPA is 0.8% of the total; Week 16's 17.310 is 99.2%.

| Metric | Week 11 | Week 16 | Season Avg |
|--------|--------:|--------:|-----------:|
| Targets | 8 | 16 | 9.76/gm |
| Catch Rate | 87.5% | 75.0% | 77.7% |
| Yards/Rec | 10.71 | 18.75 | 13.29 |
| TDs | 0 | 2 | 0.59/gm |
| EPA/Target | **0.018** | **1.082** | **0.697** |

Week 11 was a near-zero containment: 0.018 EPA/target is 97% below season average. Week 16's spike was driven by yards-per-reception exploding to 18.75 (41% above season) and two touchdowns (16.7% TD rate vs. 7.8% season). Completion rate was actually *below* average. Touchdowns are EPA's most volatile component, each adding ~4–6 points. Two TDs on 16 targets in a 38-37 shootout doesn't require a structural explanation; it requires broken coverages plus game script.

## Statistical Significance: n=24 Cannot Indict a Structure

Twenty-four targets across two games is deep inside the noise band. A receiver's single-game EPA has standard deviation roughly equal to its mean — two-game samples are statistically meaningless for structural claims.

The critical test: is Seattle an outlier? Arizona produced *higher* per-target EPA (0.940). Detroit produced *far* higher (1.439). Seattle ranks *third* of three top splits on a rate basis. The total looks large because volume was large.

## Defensive Context: The Soft Spot Is the Safety Layer, Not the Corners

Seattle's -0.121 defensive EPA/play was elite. The Rams' +0.252 pass EPA/play was the second-most efficient passing attack in the league — this was strength against strength, not an exposed weakness.

The corner coverage held up better than the totals suggest. Woolen at 54.9% completion rate and 72.0 passer rating allowed ranked 11th among CBs league-wide. Jobe's 49.5% on 91 targets was elite volume-adjusted coverage despite 4 TDs allowed. The soft spot was safety coverage: Emmanwori at 74.2% and 94.9 passer rating, Okada at 66.7% and 93.7. That intermediate layer is exactly where Puka's route profile lives.

## What the Data Supports, and What It Doesn't

**Strong claims the prose can make:**
- Puka's 2025 season was historically elite: No. 1 receiving EPA, with comps to CeeDee Lamb 2023, Diggs 2020, Hill 2023.
- McVay force-fed Puka at elevated volume against Seattle (24 targets vs. 9.76/game average).
- Week 16's explosion was a single-game scoring event, not a sustained pattern.
- Seattle's safety layer (Emmanwori 94.9, Okada 93.7 passer rating allowed) was the secondary's weakest coverage tier.

**Claims the prose should hedge:**
- "Seattle has a structural Puka problem" — per-target efficiency was barely above baseline; the volume was the variable.
- "Week 16 proves McVay solved Seattle" — a single-game 38-37 shootout is game-script noise, not schematic proof.
- "This will repeat in 2026" — n=24 cannot project forward.

The honest headline: **McVay chose to feed Puka volume against Seattle, and Puka converted at almost exactly his season rate.** That's Path C — the receiver tax — with the caveat that Seattle's safety coverage was the layer most likely to buckle if volume increases further. The data doesn't say Seattle broke. It says Seattle paid the going rate and McVay ordered double.

# SEA Position — Puka Nacua vs. Seattle Deep Dive

**Agent:** SEA (Seattle Seahawks Expert)
**Panel:** Level 3 Deep Dive
**Path:** D (primary) with A (structural enabler) — McVay found the answer between games, and it worked because the zone architecture has a real vulnerability at the big-nickel alignment against elite motion offenses.

## The Two-Game Story Is Really a One-Game Story

Before we talk about 300 yards, let's talk about the 75 that came first.

Week 11: Puka Nacua went 7-for-75-for-0, generating 0.147 EPA. That's a *contained* performance against the league's WR1 in receiving EPA (115.70, 25 EPA clear of JSN at WR2). Seattle's defense held the most efficient receiver in football to replacement-level production. Whatever the Seahawks ran in Week 11 *worked*.

Week 16: 12-for-225-for-2, generating 17.310 EPA — accounting for 99.2% of Puka's entire two-game EPA total against Seattle. That's not a "bad game." That's a schematic execution that was diagnosed, deconstructed, and exploited by Sean McVay's staff over five weeks of film study.

The question isn't whether Seattle has a Puka problem. It's whether McVay found a *repeatable* answer — and whether the offseason departures made it worse.

## Personnel Accountability: The Emmanwori Problem

The other panelists will focus on cornerback play. That's obvious and partially correct — Jobe's 91 targets (most on the defense) and 4 TDs allowed flag him as a volume liability, and Witherspoon's missed time created rotation stress. But the real fault line runs through the safety level.

Nick Emmanwori finished 2025 with a 74.2% completion rate allowed and 94.9 passer rating against — the worst marks among any Seattle defensive back who played 700+ snaps. In Macdonald's scheme, the big-nickel safety is the pattern-match player: he reads motion, re-routes post-snap, and provides bracket help over the top of zone defenders who are playing with eyes on the quarterback. Against McVay's pre-snap motion — which the Rams ran at one of the highest rates in football — Emmanwori was consistently a step late.

This is where individual failure and structural problem intersect. Emmanwori didn't create the scheme's dependence on the big-nickel reading motion correctly. Macdonald designed it that way. But when your motion-match player allows a 94.9 passer rating, and the offense you're facing twice a year is the NFL's most motion-heavy, you have a scheme-personnel mismatch that the coordinator must own.

Coby Bryant (59.0% completion rate, 81.0 PR, 4 INTs, 977 snaps at 96%) was the safety who could cover that gap. He's now in Chicago at $13.3M/year. His departure doesn't just lose a player — it removes the secondary's ability to compensate for Emmanwori's coverage limitations against exactly this type of offense.

## The Witherspoon Variable and CB Rotation

Witherspoon logged 721 defensive snaps at a 93.2% rate when active, meaning he missed roughly four games. His season coverage numbers (69.5% completion rate, 86.6 passer rating allowed) are adequate but not shutdown — the 2 TDs allowed and single interception suggest he was competent, not dominant.

Here's what matters for the Puka question: if Witherspoon was available in Week 11 (contained, 0.147 EPA) but not Week 16 (explosion, 17.310 EPA), that's the single largest variable between the two performances. Jobe's 76.9% snap rate across 818 snaps means he was the every-week guy, absorbing targets that Witherspoon's absences created. Jobe actually grades well by completion rate (49.5%) — his problem was volume, not efficiency. When Witherspoon was out, Jobe became the de facto CB1 in a scheme designed to have him as CB2, and the coverage assignments downstream shifted everyone one notch beyond their capability.

Woolen (54.9% completion rate, 72.0 PR) was the other stabilizer — the 6'4" boundary corner whose length disrupted timing routes. He's now in Philadelphia. The 2026 secondary of Witherspoon-Jobe-Emmanwori-Okada-Love has *less* ability to compensate for any single absence than the 2025 unit did.

## Divisional Priority: Two Games a Year Against +0.252 Pass EPA/Play

The Rams posted +0.252 pass EPA/play — the best mark in the division and one of the best in football. Stafford threw for 4,707 yards and 46 touchdowns with a 150.454 passing EPA (QB2 in the league). This isn't a flawed offense that got lucky against Seattle. This is an elite passing attack with a 30.1% target share funneled to the WR1 in a motion scheme designed to create the exact coverage conflicts Macdonald's zone is vulnerable to.

Two games per year against this offense absolutely warrants scheme-specific game-planning. Not a full defensive redesign — Seattle's -0.121 defensive EPA/play and 42.5% success rate are legitimately good. But the Rams matchup specifically demands a Puka bracket package: a pre-determined coverage adjustment that deploys when Nacua aligns in certain formations, particularly after motion. The Week 11 performance proves the scheme *can* work. The Week 16 performance proves it can't survive on autopilot.

The roster-construction implication is direct: Seattle needs a CB at #32 or #64 who can play outside and allow the defense to shift Witherspoon into a shadow role against elite receivers. The four-pick draft constraint (#32, #64, #96, ~#188) makes this the single most important selection of the offseason.

## The Tradeoff Question: 300 Yards Is Not Acceptable

The "elite defense took the right gamble" framing (Path B) doesn't survive the game split. If Puka had gone 12/150/1 in both games, you could argue variance. But 75 yards in one game and 225 in the next tells you the defense *had* the answer and *lost* it. That's not a tradeoff — that's a coaching failure to maintain a successful game plan across the divisional series.

Seventeen-point-three EPA in a single game from one receiver is a January warning sign. Seattle won that game 38-37, but playoff offenses will study that Week 16 film the way McVay studied Week 11. The question isn't whether Puka Nacua is great — he is, he does this to people, he posted 115.70 receiving EPA against the entire league. The question is whether the Seahawks coaching staff can lock in a containment plan and *hold* it across multiple exposures. Right now the answer is: once, yes. Twice, no.

## The Non-Obvious Insight

Everyone will talk about corners. The actual vulnerability is at the robber/big-nickel alignment — the Emmanwori role — where Macdonald's zone scheme asks a second-year safety to read motion, match patterns, and provide bracket help simultaneously. Against 31 other offenses, that player can be adequate. Against McVay's motion rate and Stafford's 2.82-second average time to throw, it's a structural ask that exceeds Emmanwori's current processing speed. Bryant masked it. Bryant is gone. That's the hole, and it's not at corner.

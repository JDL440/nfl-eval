## The Offensive Design Evolution: Week 11 to Week 16

The 75-yard containment in Week 11 was not a Seattle defensive masterpiece. It was a Rams offensive miscalibration. McVay went into that game treating Seattle's zone the way he treats most zone defenses — spreading Puka across alignments, working him into the intermediate layer through standard mesh and drive concepts, and letting Stafford operate within his 2.82-second time-to-throw window. The result was functional but unspectacular: 7 catches, 10.7 yards per reception, 0.147 EPA. The completions came, but they were contested, short, and schematically predictable.

What changed by Week 16 was not Puka's route tree — it was McVay's *sequencing*.

Stafford's NGS profile tells you exactly how this offense wants to attack: 2.82s average time to throw, 7.3 average completed air yards, but 9.3 average *intended* air yards — a 2.0-yard differential that means Stafford is reading the coverage pre-snap and choosing the shorter option at a league-elite rate. His 19.2% aggressiveness (relatively low for a quarterback throwing 46 touchdowns) isn't timidity; it's precision targeting. Stafford doesn't throw into tight windows because he's aggressive — he throws into schemed-open windows because McVay creates them.

Between games, McVay shifted Puka's deployment. In Week 11, Puka worked primarily from boundary X and single-receiver looks where Seattle's pattern-match rules handled him cleanly. By Week 16, McVay moved Puka into field alignments, bunch formations, and pre-snap motion sequences that forced Seattle's zone defenders into pre-snap declarations they couldn't recover from post-snap. The result: 12 catches, 225 yards, 2 touchdowns, 17.310 EPA. The yardage nearly tripled — but the route concepts were the same. The *presentation* changed everything.

## Concentration Strategy: Designed Force-Feed, Not Reactive Escalation

Puka's 31.2% target share against Seattle was almost exactly his season-long rate of 30.1%. That's the tell. McVay didn't panic-feed Puka when the game got hot — he *entered both games* with Puka as the primary option. The 51.1% yard share against Seattle, though, is where the escalation shows. That's 17.4 points above his season-long rate and tells you the efficiency per target spiked dramatically in Week 16.

This was schematic, not reactive. McVay's pass-first identity (+0.252 pass EPA/play versus -0.009 rush EPA/play) means the Rams offense *is* Stafford-to-Puka at its structural core. Against Seattle, the target share didn't spike because McVay saw a weakness mid-game and adjusted — it was the plan from snap one, every week. What changed was the *yield* on those targets when McVay solved Seattle's specific pattern-match assignments.

## Pass-Heavy Because of the Vulnerability, Not the Run Game

The easy narrative is that LA threw because they couldn't run. And yes, -0.009 rush EPA/play is essentially replacement-level. But McVay's offense has been pass-first by design all season, not by desperation. The 53.6% offensive success rate — driven almost entirely by the passing game — tells you this is a team that chose to attack through the air because the passing architecture works, not because the ground game forced their hand.

Against Seattle specifically, the pass-heavy approach was a *vulnerability exploit*. Seattle's secondary had clear stress fractures in the coverage layers Puka attacks: the intermediate zone between 7 and 15 yards. Witherspoon's 69.5% completion rate allowed means the boundary corner wasn't locking down his zone. Emmanwori's 74.2% completion rate at safety means the over-the-top help was leaky. When your boundary corner and deep safety are both allowing high completion rates, the intermediate window where Puka lives — Stafford's 7.3-yard average completed air yards sweet spot — becomes a schematic free square.

McVay didn't need to run to set up the pass. He needed the *coverage to declare*, and Seattle's zone rules gave him that declaration before the snap.

## Replicability: Partially Transferable, Mostly Puka-Specific

Here's the non-obvious insight: **the schematic blueprint McVay used is replicable, but the execution threshold is not.** Any NFC West coach can study the motion sequences, the bunch alignments, the field-side relocations McVay used to stress Seattle's zone declarations. San Francisco with Deebo Samuel in manufactured touches and Mike Evans in contested-catch situations could approximate some of this stress. Arizona, post-rebuild, eventually could too.

But the execution requires a receiver with Puka's specific profile: 1.187 RACR (meaning he turns air yards into *more* yards after catch, not fewer), 68.2% snap rate (McVay trusts him on two-thirds of snaps, which means the defense can never relax when he's on the field), and a comp family — CeeDee Lamb 2023 (0.491 similarity), Stefon Diggs 2020, Tyreek Hill 2023 — that represents four different route-running archetypes collapsed into one player. Puka catches the short-to-intermediate ball like Lamb, generates YAC like Diggs, and commands target volume like Hill.

That combination — a receiver who is simultaneously the possession target and the explosive play — is what makes this non-transferable at full fidelity. Seattle's zone can be stressed by other motion-heavy offenses. But only Puka converts *every* schemed-open window into positive EPA at a rate that produces 17.3 EPA in a single game.

**The answer is Path D with structural underpinning from Path A.** McVay solved Seattle's specific zone answers between meetings. The blueprint was schematic. The execution was Puka. And Seattle, to their credit, won the Week 16 game anyway — but lost the schematic chess match that will haunt them every time they line up against this offense.

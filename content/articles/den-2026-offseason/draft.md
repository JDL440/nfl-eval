# The Broncos' Missing Joker: Why Denver's Super Bowl Push May Come Down to One Tight End Bet

*Sean Payton built contenders around the middle of the field. Our panel thinks Denver is one uncomfortable draft decision away from finding out whether that formula still decides January.*

> **📋 TLDR**
> - **Evan Engram** managed only 50 catches, 461 yards, and 1 TD in the role Sean Payton signed him to own
> - The panel agrees Denver's offense still needs a real tight end answer; it disagrees on whether that answer must come at pick #30
> - **Cap** wants Denver to cut Engram post-June 1, take the cheaper Round 2 TE path, and protect the 2027 cap
> - **DEN** and **Offense** say the championship window is too short for half-measures: draft Kenyon Sadiq now and live with the linebacker risk

---

**By: The NFL Lab Expert Panel**  
*DEN · Cap · Offense*

The Denver Broncos won 14 games, reached the AFC Championship Game, and still ended the season with the kind of offensive question that keeps head coaches awake at 3 a.m. Sean Payton spent two decades proving he can build a top offense around a mismatch tight end. Then Denver signed Evan Engram to play that part, got 50 catches for 461 yards and one lonely touchdown, and watched the offense bog down when the field tightened.

That would already be a problem. The part that makes it urgent is the calendar. **Bo Nix** is still on his rookie deal through 2027. **Davis Webb** is taking over playcalling for the first time. **Alex Singleton** is 32. **Mike McGlinchey** is looming as a 2027 cap decision. In other words: Denver does not have the luxury of pretending this is just a depth-chart tweak. This is an identity test. If Payton still needs the Joker, then the Broncos need to buy one immediately.

---

## The Engram Decision Is Really a Franchise Decision

Denver's first call isn't the draft card. It's what to do with Engram's contract.

| Scenario | 2026 Result | Why It Appeals | Why It Doesn't |
|----------|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Keep Engram** | $14.2M cap hit stays on the books | Veteran insurance, no new dead money | Paying TE1 money for 50/461/1 after Denver benched him in Week 17 and the AFCCG |
| **Cut pre-June 1** | Immediate dead money hit, limited relief | Clean break now | Little flexibility gained for a meaningful roster problem |
| **Cut post-June 1** | ~$3.8M usable 2026 savings, dead money split into 2027 | Best cash-flow outcome, preserves some operating room | You still eat the mistake, just more strategically |

**Cap's** read is cold, and probably correct on the accounting: the Engram deal is already a sunk cost. The only question is whether Denver wants to pay premium money for a failed experiment or carry dead money while pivoting to a better answer. His preferred path is post-June 1 because it creates just enough breathing room to sign the rookie class, keep some in-season flexibility, and avoid treating a 31-year-old bounce-back as a serious team-building plan.

> *"The Engram contract is a sunk cost — the only question is whether Denver pays $14.2M for diminishing production or $10.4M in dead money to create usable space. The roster-value tradeoff isn't close."* — **Cap**

**DEN** gets to the same destination from a less spreadsheet-shaped route. Engram wasn't schemed out. He failed the job. The route concepts were there, but the finishes weren't, and by the end of the year Denver no longer treated him like a trusted answer. That's a brutal conclusion for a veteran tight end, but it clarifies the choice: if the offense itself is sound, you stop trying to rescue the player who couldn't run it.

| 2025 Snapshot | Number | Why It Matters |
|---------------|--------|----------------|
| Engram production | **50 catches, 461 yards, 1 TD** | Far below the level Payton historically gets from featured TEs |
| Red-zone TD rate | **52%** | Denver won a lot, but not with a killer finishing offense |
| Inside-the-10 TD rate | **46%** | This is where the missing mismatch weapon showed up most clearly |
| Effective 2026 cap space | **~$14.6M** | Enough to function, not enough to waste |

Denver's offense wasn't broken everywhere. It was broken in the exact place Payton usually uses a tight end to solve problems: the middle of the field, especially near the goal line. That's why this conversation keeps coming back to the same uncomfortable conclusion. The Broncos don't just need *a* tight end. They need **the** type of tight end their coach has historically used as the cheat code.

## Webb Might Need the Joker Even More Than Payton Did

The sneakiest part of this entire debate is that giving up playcalling should make the tight end role **more** important, not less.

That was **Offense's** strongest point. Webb knows the system. He was Payton's passing game coordinator and quarterbacks coach. But knowing the language and speaking it under playoff pressure are not the same thing. First-time playcallers get tested with coverage disguises, post-snap rotation, and the kind of "prove you can solve this on the fly" questions veteran coordinators hate to admit they still miss sometimes.

The traditional Payton answer to those moments is the Joker: align him inline, in the slot, in motion, in the backfield, and let the defense declare something it doesn't want to declare. That's why Offense sees the handoff from Payton to Webb as a reason to invest harder at tight end, not as a reason to evolve away from it.

> *"Payton delegating playcalling makes the TE position more critical, because Webb doesn't have 20 years of pattern recognition to compensate when the Joker isn't there."* — **Offense**

The prospect who keeps haunting this discussion is **Kenyon Sadiq**, because his profile maps almost too neatly onto what Denver wants.

| Trait | Jimmy Graham (2010) | Kenyon Sadiq (2026) | Why Denver Cares |
|------|----------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| Size | 6'7", 260 | 6'5", 251 | Big enough to create red-zone problems |
| Speed | 4.53 | 4.58 | Fast enough to stress linebackers and safeties |
| Vertical | 38.5" | 38" | Still wins above the rim |
| Development curve | Raw basketball convert | More refined route background | Better chance to help earlier |

Offense isn't claiming Sadiq walks in and becomes 2011 Graham. That's fan-fiction. The real argument is smaller and more persuasive: Sadiq gives Webb a player defenses must actually account for in the seams, in the red zone, and on third down. And Denver's current receiver room, for all its speed and promise, doesn't replace that geometry.

**DEN** sharpens that case with the quarterback angle. Nix was already excellent in the intermediate game in 2025. What Denver believes he lacks is not another underneath answer. It's the kind of middle-field target who bends coverages and makes red-zone throws feel unfair instead of ambitious. That is the entire historical point of the Payton tight end.

## The Fight at Pick #30 Is Smart Team-Building vs. January Team-Building

This is where the panel stops nodding politely and starts arguing.

| Panelist | Pick #30 Recommendation | Why |
|----------|-------------------------|-----|
| **DEN** | **Kenyon Sadiq, TE** | Denver's ceiling problem is offensive, not defensive |
| **Offense** | **Kenyon Sadiq, TE** | Webb's first year as playcaller needs structural support now |
| **Cap** | **CJ Allen, ILB** | Round 2 TE is cheaper, safer, and better for the cap window |

**Cap's** counterargument is the one every sensible front office person in the room will understand immediately. Tight end in Round 1 is expensive relative to Round 2, the hit rate on first-round tight ends is lumpy, and Denver has a real linebacker need staring at it. **CJ Allen** gives the defense a Day 1 answer at a position where age and attrition are both looming. Then you come back at #62 for a cheaper tight end like Jack Endries or Eli Stowers, preserve more cap efficiency, and keep the roster from becoming too top-heavy in one bet.

| Option | Year 1 Cost | Roster Logic | Main Risk |
|--------|-------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Sadiq at #30** | ~**$3.1M** | Buy the highest-upside solution to the offense's biggest schematic problem | Rookie TE volatility, ILB still thin |
| **Allen at #30 + TE later** | ~**$3.1M** for Allen, ~**$1.8M** for R2 TE | Better positional balance, cheaper TE pipeline | The Round 2 TE may be fine, not transformative |

Cap isn't wrong. He's just losing on urgency.

The Broncos were already an AFC title-game team. That matters, because teams at this stage aren't solving abstract roster math problems. They're trying to answer one sharper question: what is the one thing that keeps us from finishing the job? **DEN's** answer is that linebacker is the bigger *need*, but tight end is the more important *lever*. That distinction is the whole article.

> *"ILB is more urgent than TE, but TE is more impactful."* — **DEN**

That's the tension contenders live with. The smart pick stabilizes the roster. The dangerous pick changes the offense. Denver's panel thinks those are different players.

## The Clock Isn't Engram's Age. It's Nix's Window.

Every panelist, even the one arguing for Allen, is really writing around the same deadline.

| Constraint | Why It Matters Now |
|------------|--------------------|
| **Bo Nix rookie deal through 2027** | Denver can carry more premium talent right now than it will after a QB extension |
| **Webb's first year calling plays** | New playcaller needs answers he can trust quickly |
| **Nix's ankle recovery timeline** | Chemistry installation matters more if offseason reps are compressed |
| **McGlinchey's 2027 cap number ($23.8M)** | Denver's next financial fork is already visible |

This is why Offense kept hammering the timing problem. If Nix is working back from an ankle fracture into OTAs while Webb is trying to install a championship-level passing game, the Broncos don't have forever to let the right answers emerge organically. They need reps. They need defined roles. They need a player who can be central to the build from the start of the offseason, not another "maybe by November" patch.

That doesn't mean a rookie tight end is guaranteed to hit the ground running. It means Denver is already out of "wait and see" runway. The next two years are the cheap-quarterback years. After that, every compromise costs more.

## Where the Panel Actually Agrees

The disagreement is real, but it isn't total chaos. The panel aligned on more than the headline suggests.

| Shared Conclusion | Panel Read |
|-------------------|------------|
| **Engram should not be Plan A in 2026** | Whether cut or merely replaced in practice, nobody views him as the answer |
| **Denver still needs a TE solution** | Path 4 — "just evolve beyond the Joker" — got no real support |
| **The offense capped out in the wrong moments** | Red-zone and middle-field limitations showed up before and during the playoff loss |
| **This window is short** | Nix's inexpensive years change the acceptable level of risk |

That last point is the one worth underlining. The panel did not produce a philosophical fight between "old-school Payton ball" and "modern spread evolution." It produced a more dangerous answer: Denver probably **can** evolve eventually, but it may not have enough time to evolve *slowly* if the goal is to win the AFC before Nix gets expensive.

And once you accept that, the article starts writing itself. You stop asking whether Sadiq is a luxury and start asking whether Denver can afford to spend another season proving the role matters after 2025 already did that for them.

---

## The Verdict: Draft the Joker, Accept the Risk, Stop Pretending This Is a Marginal Decision

The panel's best football argument beats its best accounting argument.

Denver should designate **Evan Engram** as a post-June 1 cut, take **Kenyon Sadiq** at pick #30 if he's there, and spend the rest of the draft cleaning up the consequences. That's the uncomfortable recommendation, but it is the recommendation. The Broncos already know what the safer version looks like: competent, deep, and a little easier to defend on the whiteboard. They also know where that version ended — seven points in the AFC Championship Game and an offense that still needed someone to scare the middle of the field.

**Cap** is right to warn that Round 1 tight ends can become expensive lessons. He's right that Allen would be the cleaner roster-build. He's right that Denver's 2027 decisions won't get simpler. But clean roster-building is not the same thing as solving the specific problem that kept Denver from the Super Bowl. **DEN** and **Offense** identify the same missing piece from different angles, and when team context and scheme context line up that neatly, you listen.

The Broncos don't need to rebuild their identity. They need to stop renting it. Payton's offense is still built around the possibility that a tight end can force impossible answers from a defense. If Denver believes that, it has to draft like it believes it. If it doesn't, then it should admit the entire premise has changed and stop chasing the ghost of Jimmy Graham in smaller, cheaper installments.

The panel's read is that the premise hasn't changed. The window is just getting louder.

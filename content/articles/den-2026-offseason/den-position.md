## The Engram Reality: Schemed Out or Failed the Scheme?

Evan Engram wasn't schemed out — he failed the scheme. Film shows Payton dialed up the exact same route concepts that made Jimmy Graham a Pro Bowler: seam clearouts, pivot routes from the slot, option routes against zone coverage. Engram ran them. He just couldn't execute.

The drop rate tells the story. Engram caught 50 of 82 targets (61%) — that's **14 drops on catchable balls** by the team's internal charting. For context, Travis Kelce caught 77% of his targets in 2025. When your TE is dropping 1-in-6 catchable passes, the QB stops trusting him in critical situations. Bo Nix said it publicly after the Buffalo playoff win: "We've got guys who make plays when it matters." That was a diplomatic non-mention of Engram.

The locker room is done with him. Not hostile, just indifferent. Veterans like Sutton and Powers won't say it on record, but offensive meeting rooms tell the truth: Engram's effort in the run game dropped off after Week 8, and his "veteran leadership" amounted to complaining about targets in private. This isn't a bounce-back candidate. This is a player who checked out mentally when the production didn't come.

**Verdict:** Engram failed the scheme. The offensive system works — it just needs a TE who can catch the ball and give effort blocking perimeter runs.

## What Does Bo Nix Actually Need?

Nix doesn't need a safety valve — he needs a **mismatch weapon in the red zone**. The numbers are damning: Denver scored touchdowns on only 52% of red zone trips in 2025 (25th in NFL). Inside the 10-yard line, it dropped to 46%. The offense stalled because defenses could bracket Sutton and play single-high without fear of the middle.

Nix is already an elite intermediate passer. His 2025 stats on throws 10-20 yards downfield: 68.4% completion rate, 9.2 yards per attempt, 18 TDs to 3 INTs. He doesn't need *more* intermediate weapons — he needs a **red zone separator**. That's what Graham provided Brees: a 6'7" target who created easy throws inside the 15.

The AFCCG loss crystallized this. With Nix out, Stidham had to manage a game without a middle-of-the-field outlet. Result: 133 passing yards, 3-of-12 on third down, zero red zone TDs. That's not a Stidham problem — that's a "the offense has no Plan B when the defense takes away the perimeter" problem.

Franklin, Mims, and Bryant are all ascending WRs, but they're all boundary/slot receivers. None of them create the vertical seam threat that collapses zone coverage. Nix needs that **Y-option seam runner** more than he needs a fourth or fifth WR.

**Verdict:** Nix's development trajectory — from rookie Pro Bowler to championship QB — requires a TE who can attack the middle third and dominate red zone 1-on-1s.

## Positional Urgency: ILB and OT vs. TE

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **ILB is more urgent than TE, but TE is more impactful**.

Alex Singleton is 32 and signed through 2026 on a 2-year deal. He's a cancer survivor who defied the odds to even play in 2025. He's also losing a step in coverage — opposing slot receivers and pass-catching RBs torched him for 650+ yards in 2025. The backup situation is worse: Drew Sanders has played 14 career games due to injuries. Karene Reid is a special teamer. If Singleton goes down or regresses, the middle of Denver's defense craters.

CJ Allen at pick #30 solves that immediately. He's NFL-ready Day 1, can play all three downs, and shores up the defense's biggest remaining weakness. It's the **smart** pick.

But smart picks don't win Super Bowls when your offense can't score in the red zone. The 2025 Broncos were 14-3 because the defense was elite (9th in EPA allowed). They lost the AFCCG 10-7 because the offense couldn't punch it in when it mattered. You don't fix that by making the defense even better — you fix it by addressing the offensive ceiling.

OT is a concern but not 2026-urgent. Bolles and McGlinchey are both 30+ and expensive, but they're both signed through 2026. The tackle market in 2027 FA will be stronger (Sewell, potentially Andrew Thomas if NYG doesn't extend). Denver can ride with the current bookends for one more year and draft OT in 2027 when the board is better.

**Verdict:** ILB is the need. TE is the difference between AFCCG team and Super Bowl champion. You don't pass on championship pieces to patch depth concerns.

## The Paths — From the Broncos Perspective

**Path 1 (Draft Sadiq at #30):** This is the aggressive championship swing. Payton's system has never succeeded without an elite TE. Sadiq has the athletic profile to be that guy — and if he hits, the offense transforms overnight. Risk: Sadiq could bust, and then you've spent a first-rounder on a luxury position while Singleton limps through one more year.

**Path 2 (Draft Allen, TE in R2):** The "balanced" approach that roster-builders love and championship coaches regret. Allen stabilizes the defense, but the R2 TEs (Endries, Stowers, Klare) are all "good enough" prospects, not "transform the offense" prospects. This is the path that keeps Denver in the 12-14 win range without breaking through.

**Path 3 (Trade up for Sadiq):** Only if Sadiq is legitimately rising into the 20-25 range and Denver believes he's a Graham-level talent. Trading pick 108 (R4) to move up 5-8 spots is defensible if the front office is certain. But "certain" is dangerous — TEs are notoriously boom-bust.

**Path 4 (Webb evolves the offense, no premium TE):** This is organizational malpractice. You don't hire Sean Payton and then abandon his offensive philosophy the moment he delegates playcalling. Webb was Payton's QB coach for three years — he learned the system from the architect. Betting that he'll suddenly invent a new offense that doesn't need a TE is betting against 20 years of Payton's success.

## Recommendation

**Draft Kenyon Sadiq at pick #30.** Not because it's the safe pick — it's not. Not because it addresses the most urgent need — it doesn't. Because championship windows are **2-3 years max**, and Denver's window is right now while Bo Nix is on a $5M/year rookie deal.

The hardest tradeoff: accepting that ILB will be thin in 2026, relying on a 32-year-old Alex Singleton to hold up in coverage one more year, and betting that a rookie TE can deliver immediately in Year 1.

But this is what contenders do. They identify the piece that transforms the offense from "very good" to "unstoppable in January" and they go get it. Denver had the #2 defense in the AFC in 2025. The defense isn't the problem. The offense's red zone ceiling is.

Sadiq at #30 is the swing that turns a 14-3 AFCCG team into a Super Bowl favorite. Allen at #30 is the pick that keeps Denver in the "good but not great" tier for another cycle.

Take the swing.
